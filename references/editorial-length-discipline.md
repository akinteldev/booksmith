# Editorial Length Discipline

## Trigger

Use this when Booksmith chapter prompts, self-reviews, or drafting gates mention target word counts, under-length chapters, or expansion requests.

## Durable Lesson

A chapter that cleanly tells its assigned story at ~4,000 words should not be forced to 5,500–7,000 words solely to satisfy a range. Forced expansion is a high-risk path to padding, vague abstraction, invented scene detail, or hallucinated specificity.

## Operational Rule

- Treat word-count ranges as soft orientation, not pass/fail gates.
- Editorial completeness overrides length targets.
- Self-review must not mark a chapter as requiring expansion only because it is below target length.
- Expansion is justified only when it adds missing sourced substance, missing narrative connective tissue, or required prompt coverage.
- If the story is complete and source coverage is sufficient, approve the shorter chapter.

## Self-Review Guidance

When reviewing chapter length, ask:

1. Are all required source facts and chapter-purpose elements present?
2. Does the chapter have a clear narrative arc and satisfying close?
3. Are any omissions substantive rather than cosmetic?
4. Would extra words add source-grounded insight, or merely restate the thesis?

Only request expansion if the answer to #3 is yes and #4 favors source-grounded insight.

## Vignette Risk

Do not add invented specificity to reach length. Composite vignettes should clearly read as grounded in documented mechanics, not as disguised real incidents. Avoid exact invented details such as data volumes, payment outcomes, named document categories, employee counts, or precise timelines unless sourced.

## Preferred Template Direction

Replace strict language like:

> Target length: 5,500–7,000 words.

with softer language such as:

> Expected length: write to editorial completeness. Most chapters may fall around 4,000–6,500 words, but do not pad to reach a number. If the assigned story is complete, concise is preferred.
