# Foreword

The book that follows is built on a specific kind of evidence and was written under a specific kind of discipline. It is worth saying so plainly, before the narrative begins, so that the reader knows what is being claimed and on what basis.

The factual skeleton of Ransomware, Inc. is drawn from five exhaustive 2025–2026 research reports. The reports synthesize the published work of several dozen primary sources: vendor incident-response findings from Check Point Research, Broadcom's Symantec unit, Rapid7, Cisco Talos, Mandiant, Unit 42, Trend Micro, Zscaler ThreatLabz, GuidePoint Security, AhnLab, Brandefense, Secure Blink, BreachSense, Lyrie Threat Intelligence, DeepStrike, Huntress, NCC Group, Recorded Future, NordStellar, BlackFog, ReliaQuest, Olzak, CyberPress, Dark Reading, CyberFortress, Level, Industrial Cyber, Blair Technology, Nightwing, Semperis, ITBrief, ZeroFox, and CYFIRMA; government and standards-body output from the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity, the United States Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the United States Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, the Federal Reserve, and the National Institute of Standards and Technology; and the leak-site telemetry, criminal-forum monitoring, and negotiation-transcript analyses that the threat-intelligence community publishes regularly.

Every statistic in this book — every percentage, every dollar figure, every victim count, every attributed claim — traces back to one of those five reports. Where reports overlap on a figure, the prose preferred the source closest to the chapter's primary subject and noted the convergence where it appeared. Where a single source made a claim that other sources had not corroborated, the prose preserved the caveat. The 2026 claim of live ML-KEM use in ransomware payloads, for instance, is presented in the closing chapter as the unverified forecast the reporting itself names it as, not as documented operational fact. The discipline is not an editorial preference. It is the only honest way to write about a field where the difference between confirmed activity and projected activity is the difference between describing an industry and inventing one.

The vignettes that open each chapter — the IT director watching a leak-site countdown, the help-desk technician on a phone call, the negotiator on the victim side of the chat window, the incident-response lead in the windowless lab — are clearly framed as hypothetical scenes. They are constructed to humanize mechanics that the reports describe in aggregate. The mechanics are real. The victims, the tactics, the tooling, the economics, the institutional failures are real. The individuals are composite illustrations, anonymized by design. A reader who wants to test the technical claims behind any vignette can do so by reading the report that the chapter draws from. The reports do not require the reader to take any sentence on trust.

The five reports are open-sourced. They are published, in full, at the public research repository at [https://github.com/akinteldev/research](https://github.com/akinteldev/research). The repository is intended as a working archive, not as a marketing channel. A reader who wants to verify a figure, follow a citation to the original threat-intelligence publication, or read the underlying analysis at the level of detail the chapters could not accommodate will find the reports there. The repository preserves the chain from raw threat-intelligence reporting through the synthesis the reports performed to the narrative this book builds. The chain is the basis on which the book asks the reader to take its argument seriously.

A word about what this book is not.

It is not a how-to. The reporting it draws from contains, in places, specific operational detail about intrusion technique, tooling, and tradecraft. Where that detail was necessary for a reader to understand why a defense fails or why an attack succeeds, the prose included it. Where the detail was operational in a way that would arm a copycat rather than inform a defender, the prose did not. The principle the chapters were written under — information, not ammunition — was a working constraint, applied paragraph by paragraph, not a slogan applied to the project as a whole. A reader looking for a manual will not find one. A reader looking for an explanation of why the manual is no longer the relevant question will, this book hopes, find that explanation here.

It is not an academic monograph. The reports are footnoted exhaustively. The book is not. The prose carries attribution in the language threat-intelligence reporting uses — "Check Point Research observed," "ENISA reported," "the FinCEN figures show" — but does not interrupt itself with bracketed citation marks or numbered footnotes. The decision was made deliberately, on the calculation that a reader who wants the academic apparatus will reach for the underlying reports and a reader who wants the narrative will be served better by prose that does not stop reading every few sentences to identify itself. The choice has costs. The reader who wants to test a single claim against its source will have to do the work of locating it in the report repository. The reports make that work tractable. The reports are organized so the work can be done.

It is not a prediction. The closing chapter describes the cryptographic transition as a forecast, and the rest of the book honors the same restraint where the reporting required it. The forecasts in this book are the forecasts the threat-intelligence community itself was making at the time the underlying reports were written, calibrated against the language those reports used. Where the reporting hedged, the prose hedged. Where the reporting was confident, the prose carried the confidence. The reader who wants to know how confident a claim is can read the report it came from. The reports are careful about their epistemic posture in a way that the public conversation about cybercrime has not always been, and the book has tried to be careful in the same way.

The economy the chapters describe is large. It is well-funded. It is organized. It is, in the operational sense the security industry uses the word, mature. The reports the book draws on were written by people who track this economy professionally and who have, across years of disciplined work, refused to dramatize it. The book has tried to honor that work. The reader who finds the chapters alarming should know that the alarm is doing work the reports themselves take seriously and that the reports' authors would, in the language of their own publications, recognize.

The reports are available. The work is open. The reader who wants to do the verification can. That is the discipline this book was written under. The prose that follows is what came out of the discipline.

# Introduction

Before the narrative begins, the reader has to be introduced to the cast. The cast in this book is not a list of people. It is a list of systems, markets, institutions, incentives, and mechanisms that recur across every chapter and that together compose the economy the book is about. The chapters will return to each of them. The chapters will deepen each of them. The chapters will, by the closing argument, ask the reader to see them as a single industrialized whole rather than as the separate problems they have usually been described as. The introduction's job is to put the names on the table so that, when the chapters reach for them, the reader is not meeting them for the first time.

The first member of the cast is the platform. Ransomware in 2026 is not, in the way the public conversation still tends to describe it, malware written by a gang. It is software, infrastructure, brand, marketing apparatus, customer-relations workflow, and labor-market presence operated by a small number of central organizations that behave, in every measurable respect, like companies. Qilin, LockBit (now in its 5.0 incarnation), Akira, The Gentlemen, DragonForce — these are not gang names. They are product names. Each platform builds and maintains the encryption tooling, the leak-site infrastructure, the negotiation portal, the affiliate-onboarding system, the cryptocurrency-payment plumbing, and the reputational presence that together make extortion possible at industrial scale. Each platform competes with the others on price, on payout, on tooling, on brand credibility, on the willingness to enter sensitive sectors that competitors have ruled off-limits. The platforms are the first thing the book asks the reader to see. The book's central argument depends on the reader seeing them.

The second member of the cast is the affiliate. The affiliate is the semi-independent contractor who, by paying a deposit and accepting the terms of the platform's affiliate program, gains the right to use the platform's tooling against the victims the affiliate chooses to attack. The affiliate retains, depending on the platform, eighty or eighty-five or in earlier years ninety percent of the ransom payment. The platform retains the remainder. The split is the engine that powers the labor market beneath the brand layer. When a platform falls — as RansomHub did in 2025, as LockBit did under Operation Cronos in 2024 — the affiliates do not vanish. They migrate. They take their access, their skills, their relationships with initial-access brokers, and their operational discipline with them to the platforms that have absorbed the demand the falling platform leaves behind. The affiliate is the reason that takedowns of named brands have not, across the past several years, reduced the volume of the industry. The affiliate is the part of the industry that is, in the operational sense, durable.

The third member of the cast is the access economy. Beneath the platform and the affiliate sits a supply chain dedicated to producing the initial intrusion point that the affiliate's later work depends on. Infostealer crews — operators of malware purpose-built to harvest credentials, session cookies, browser data, and tokens from infected endpoints — feed criminal marketplaces with stolen identity material. Initial-access brokers buy that material in bulk, validate which of the credentials actually unlock corporate environments, package the working access into priced listings, and resell the listings to affiliates who need a way in. The pricing is calibrated. Low-privilege credentials sell for twenty dollars. Domain-administrator access sells for ten thousand dollars or more. Session cookies that bypass multi-factor authentication sell for prices in between. The access economy is the part of the industry that the public conversation has been slowest to absorb, because it is invisible from the outside. The victim never sees the broker. The broker is the reason the victim is attacked.

The fourth member of the cast is the leak site. A leak site is the public-facing arm of a ransomware platform. It is a website, usually hosted on the dark-web infrastructure that criminal operators have refined into a routine technical practice, on which the platform posts the names of its victims, the countdown timers to data publication, the discounts available for prompt payment, and the eventual leaks themselves when negotiations fail. The leak site is the platform's marketing infrastructure. It is where the platform builds reputation, demonstrates capability, and applies the public pressure that makes the next ransom demand sound credible to the next chief financial officer. The leak site is also, for the threat-intelligence community, the primary measurement instrument for the industry. The victim counts the reporting cites — 2,122 victims in Q1 2026, 7,960 leak-site listings in 2025 — are leak-site counts. They are not intrusion counts. They are not revenue figures. They are the number of organizations the platforms have publicly named. The number of organizations the platforms have privately attacked is, in every credible estimate, larger.

The fifth member of the cast is the negotiation desk. Behind the leak site sits a chat window. The chat window is staffed, on the criminal side, by people who behave less like criminals than like sales representatives. They open with anchoring demands designed to be reduced through staged discounts. They run the negotiation against the regulatory disclosure clock the victim is, in many jurisdictions, simultaneously running. They upsell — "data deletion" assurances, additional decryption-tool features, even legal-assistance products marketed to the affiliates who are running the intrusions. They close at conversion rates that, in the NordStellar transcripts the book draws on, reach a quarter of all engagements. They achieve median discounts of fifty-seven percent against opening demands. They operate, by every measurement available, like a sales pipeline. The chat window is where the industry monetizes. The negotiation desk is the part of the industry the victims actually interact with.

The sixth member of the cast is the recovery trap. Modern ransomware operators do not, in their most damaging campaigns, settle for encrypting the victim's files. They go after the systems that would allow the victim to recover without paying. Backups are deleted, corrupted, or rendered inaccessible. The directory services that govern who can log into what — Active Directory on premises, Entra ID in the cloud — are compromised early in the intrusion and used as the lever that converts a single foothold into enterprise-wide control. Hypervisors, especially VMware's ESXi, are targeted because a single compromised hypervisor can render an entire virtualization estate unrecoverable inside minutes. Edge devices — VPN appliances, firewalls, remote-access gateways — are targeted as the entry point. The recovery-trap mechanic is the part of the industry that converts ransomware from a data-loss problem into a survival problem. It is what determines whether the victim, when they reach the negotiation desk, has any alternative to paying.

The seventh member of the cast is the artificial-intelligence layer. Across the past several years, the criminal market has begun to integrate large-language-model tooling into the workflows that connect the other members of the cast. The integration is not full autonomy. The reporting is careful to call it augmentation. Affiliates use models to triage the access listings they buy, to draft phishing material calibrated against publicly available data about specific victims, to run conversational extortion bots that handle the early stages of negotiation, to script intrusion logic across reconnaissance and lateral movement, to generate persona-simulation pipelines that make voice-cloned vishing calls land more convincingly. The model is not yet the attacker. The model is the labor multiplier that lets one human attacker do the work of several. The AI layer is the part of the industry that has changed the operational tempo most visibly across 2025 and 2026.

The eighth member of the cast is the strategic victimology. The industry does not attack at random. It attacks where coercive leverage is highest. Manufacturing, healthcare, public administration, and digital infrastructure dominate the ENISA breakdowns because each of them combines operational fragility with reputational asymmetry and regulatory exposure. A hospital cannot tolerate a multi-week outage. A manufacturer cannot tolerate a halt in its scheduling software. A municipality cannot tolerate the public visibility of its citizens' data appearing on a criminal storefront. The geographic distribution — Germany, Italy, Spain, France, Belgium leading the European victim counts — is not the artifact of where the criminals happen to be. It is the artifact of where the leverage is. The map of the victims is the map of the leverage.

The ninth member of the cast — the one the book builds toward — is the cryptographic wall. The standardization of post-quantum cryptography by the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology in 2024 produced, as a side effect of the work that was intended to protect the legitimate digital economy, the cryptographic primitives that the criminal-software market is now positioned to integrate into operational payloads. The integration, when it lands at scale, will close off the last residual hope that defeated victims have sometimes had: the hope that the operators made an implementation mistake the defenders could exploit. The cryptographic wall is the part of the future the book argues the defenders have a closing window to prepare for. The closing chapter is about the wall directly. Every chapter before it is about the operational defenses that the wall, when it arrives, will make the only defenses worth building.

These nine members — platform, affiliate, access economy, leak site, negotiation desk, recovery trap, AI layer, strategic victimology, cryptographic wall — are the cast. They will recur. The chapters that follow will return to each of them, deepen each of them, and connect them to the others. The reader who carries the cast list into the chapters will see, in each new vignette, a system that has already been introduced. The reader who sees the systems will see the industry.

The book is not asking the reader to be afraid. The book is asking the reader to recognize what has been built.

# Prologue

The first call came in at three minutes past two in the morning, local time, on a Tuesday in February.

The on-call analyst at a managed-detection-and-response firm in the American Midwest was twenty-six years old and three months into her first job out of a community-college cybersecurity program. She had been doing what overnight analysts do at that hour, which is reading through the queue of low-priority alerts the daytime team had triaged down without closing. The alert that broke the routine was tagged moderate, not high, and it had taken almost forty minutes to surface in her queue. The alert was a single line. An authentication had succeeded against a customer's cloud-identity tenant from an internet address that the firm's threat-intelligence feed had, eleven hours earlier, flagged as associated with a ransomware-affiliate forum. The authentication had used a valid corporate credential. It had passed multi-factor authentication on the first attempt. The session token it received was, by every measurement the system could perform, legitimate.

She opened the alert. She looked at the username. She looked at the timestamp. She looked at the source address. She looked at the geographic enrichment the system had attached: the address was hosted at a Lithuanian data center known to the threat-intelligence community as a transit waypoint for affiliate operations. She looked at the user's profile in the customer's directory: a regional sales-operations manager whose job did not require him to be logging in from Lithuania at three in the morning.

She picked up the phone.

What she did not know, at the moment she picked up the phone, was that the credential the attacker had used had been stolen six weeks earlier from the sales manager's home laptop by an infostealer the manager had installed without knowing he had installed it. The infostealer had been bundled with a cracked productivity tool the manager had downloaded after seeing an advertisement on a social-media site. The infostealer had, in the seven hours it had run before the manager's home antivirus had finally noticed it, exfiltrated the contents of the manager's browser password store, his saved session cookies for every site he had logged into in the previous three months, his email client's authentication tokens, and a clean copy of his corporate-network single-sign-on credential. The bundle had been uploaded to a criminal marketplace within the hour. The bundle had been purchased, three days later, by an initial-access broker for forty-two dollars. The broker had validated the credential against the company's authentication portal, confirmed it still worked, packaged it together with the session cookie that allowed multi-factor authentication to be bypassed, and listed the package on a higher-tier marketplace for fourteen hundred dollars. The package had sold, two weeks later, to an affiliate of a ransomware platform whose name the threat-intelligence community had been tracking for eleven months. The affiliate had paid in cryptocurrency. The transaction had not, by any measurement available to the firm whose sales manager would lose his credential, generated a record that anyone outside the criminal market would see.

The analyst's call woke the firm's senior incident responder. He listened to her describe the alert. He listened to her describe the geographic enrichment. He listened to her describe the user's profile. He asked her to put the affected customer on a watch list and to escalate the alert to high priority. He asked her to wake the customer's on-call security contact. He told her she had done well to notice. He hung up. He stared at the ceiling of his bedroom for a long moment. He thought about what he was about to spend the next four days doing.

The customer's on-call security contact did not pick up. The contact had, six months earlier, switched cellular carriers without updating his entry in the company's on-call rotation. The number the analyst dialed was now assigned to a confused retiree in Phoenix who did not understand what the analyst was asking for and who hung up before she could explain. The analyst dialed the customer's secondary contact. The secondary contact picked up on the third ring, recognized the firm's number from his caller ID, and asked the analyst to hold for a moment while he found his work laptop. The hold lasted four minutes. During those four minutes, the affiliate on the other end of the intrusion was completing the lateral-movement phase of an attack pattern the threat-intelligence community had documented across more than three hundred prior victims. The affiliate was using a domain-administrator account he had elevated to within the first eleven minutes after his initial login. The affiliate was, with the help of a language model running on a server in another country, scripting the file-encryption phase against the customer's primary file server, the customer's two virtualization clusters, the customer's backup catalog, and the customer's identity infrastructure. The model was generating the commands the affiliate would execute. The affiliate was reviewing the commands before he executed them. The pace was, by the measurement the firm's incident responders would later perform, faster than the pace at which the firm's standard playbook had been written to respond.

The secondary contact came back on the line. The analyst started to brief him on what she had seen. The contact began to ask her clarifying questions. The clarifying questions were the kind of questions the firm's playbook had told the contact to ask, and they were the right questions, and the analyst had the right answers. The questions and the answers took eight minutes. During those eight minutes, the affiliate completed his preparation. The contact ended the call by telling the analyst he was driving to the office. The drive would take forty minutes. The contact would arrive at the office at four minutes past three in the morning. The encryption of the customer's environment would begin at one minute past three.

The analyst stayed on the phone with the senior incident responder. The senior incident responder watched, in the firm's monitoring console, the alert volume from the customer's environment cross from elevated into the kind of cascade that did not have any other explanation. He watched the customer's file servers go offline. He watched the customer's directory services go unresponsive. He watched the customer's backup-catalog service throw an error that the playbook had not previously documented. He picked up the phone again, this time to wake the firm's recovery-engagement lead.

The recovery-engagement lead was awake before the phone finished its first ring. He had been expecting the call for weeks. The customer was a regional logistics firm with eight hundred employees, three distribution centers, two warehouses, and a fleet of one hundred and twenty trucks. The firm moved roughly four percent of the perishable food supply for a metropolitan area of four million people. The firm's scheduling software, its dispatch routing, its driver hours-of-service logging, its customs paperwork for its cross-border shipments, and its warehouse inventory management all ran on the systems that had, in the previous eleven minutes, been encrypted. The firm's recovery time objective, as documented in the contract the firm had signed with the managed-detection-and-response firm three years earlier, was six hours. The realistic recovery time, the recovery-engagement lead was about to spend the next four days explaining to the firm's chief executive officer, was going to be measured in weeks.

The leak site posted the firm's name at seven minutes past three in the morning. The countdown timer started at seventy-two hours. The opening demand was four-point-one million dollars in cryptocurrency. The discount schedule was visible beneath the timer. Pay within twenty-four hours and the demand dropped to two-point-eight. Wait, and the discount evaporated in stages, until the timer reached zero and the data — fourteen hundred gigabytes of it, including the personal information of every driver the firm employed, the contract terms of every grocery chain the firm shipped to, and the proprietary routing algorithms the firm's logistics team had spent four years building — would, the leak site said, be published.

The firm's chief executive officer would, by mid-morning, be on a conference call with his board, his outside counsel, his cyber-insurance carrier, his managed-detection-and-response firm's incident responders, and a specialist negotiation firm the insurance carrier had brought in. The negotiation would begin within the hour. The firm would, across the next four days, reach a settlement at roughly thirty-seven percent of the opening demand. The payment would be wired. The decryption key would arrive. The firm would, across the following three weeks, restore enough of its scheduling software to resume operations at degraded capacity. Two of the grocery chains the firm had been shipping to would terminate their contracts during the outage. The firm would, in the six months that followed, lay off eighty-seven employees.

The analyst, in the office she had been working from when the alert had first surfaced in her queue, would by mid-morning of the next day write the after-action note that summarized what she had seen. She would write the note carefully. She would describe the alert. She would describe the timing. She would describe the eight minutes she had spent on the phone with the secondary contact. She would describe what she had learned in the days since about the credential that had been used to log in, the infostealer that had taken it, the marketplace it had been sold on, the broker who had bought it, the affiliate who had bought it from the broker, and the platform that had hosted the affiliate's intrusion against her customer. She would describe the language model the affiliate had used to script the encryption. She would describe the leak site the platform had used to post the firm's name. She would describe the negotiation the firm had conducted. She would describe the payment.

The after-action note would not, in any meaningful sense, be unusual. The firm's incident-response team handled, by the time the analyst wrote her note, between three and five intrusions like this every week. The firm was one of several dozen managed-detection-and-response providers in the country. The intrusions all looked like this. The architecture all looked like this. The economics all looked like this.

This is the economy this book describes. Each layer of the architecture the analyst would document in her note is the subject of a chapter. The platform is the subject of the first part. The affiliate is the subject of the second. The access economy is the subject of the third. The strategic victimology is the subject of the fourth. The recovery trap is the subject of the fifth. The artificial-intelligence acceleration is the subject of the sixth. The social engineering that turned the original credential theft into a usable attack vector is the subject of the seventh. The negotiation desk is the subject of the eighth. The cryptographic primitives that, in the closing chapter, will make payment the only remaining option are the subject of the ninth.

The analyst is not in any of the chapters. The intrusion she watched is not in any of the chapters. The chapters are about the industry that produced the intrusion. The chapters are about the systems that, eleven months after the analyst's after-action note was filed, would produce the next intrusion in another company on another night, in another part of the world, against another sales manager who had downloaded another bundled tool.

The pages that follow are an account of an industry. The industry is not new. The industry has been industrializing.

The book begins.

# Chapter 1: The Cartel Year

The IT director was already three coffees into a Tuesday when his deputy walked into the office without knocking. There is a particular silence that a deputy carries when they have just seen the company's name on a leak site, and it filled the room before either of them said anything. The deputy turned the laptop so the director could see the screen. The leak site looked like a startup landing page. Clean typography, a sparse navigation bar, a countdown timer ticking off the hours in red. Above the timer was the name of his manufacturing firm, the city where its headquarters sat, an estimated revenue figure pulled from a public business directory, and a teaser line announcing that 1.4 terabytes of data — financial spreadsheets, design files, the human-resources directory, customer contracts — had been exfiltrated and would be released in stages unless the deadline was met. There was a chat link. There was, neatly listed below the deadline, a discount schedule. Pay within the first seventy-two hours and the demand dropped. The discount disappeared in increments after that, like an airline seat upgrade going stale.

The director stared at the page for a long moment. He was looking at the countdown, but he was also looking at the design. The site was not amateur. It did not look like the work of someone hunched in a basement. It looked like the front end of a product. He thought, in a moment of recognition that would not let him go for months afterward, that whoever had built this had thought carefully about the user experience of being extorted.

There were 2,121 other organizations on similar sites that quarter. His company was the 2,122nd.

This is the central fact of ransomware in 2026, and it is the fact the rest of this book sets out to explain. In the first quarter of the year, Check Point Research tracked more than two thousand organizations posted to the data-leak pages of seventy-some criminal brands. Brandefense, working from a partially overlapping dataset, counted 2,135 confirmed ransomware incidents involving sixty-eight active groups across ninety-eight countries, and noted that the quarterly volume was up roughly fifty percent over the previous three months. Check Point's longer-horizon count of leak-site listings — the public marketing arm of the ransomware industry — reached 7,960 organizations in 2025 alone, a fifty-three percent jump from the year before. These are not numbers in the same way that a death toll is a number, because every entry on a leak site represents an organization that has already lost something: a hospital that has had to divert ambulances, a school district scrambling for paper attendance sheets, a small machine shop watching its scheduling software refuse to open. But as a measurement of pace, the figures are unmistakable. The ransomware economy is not a residual problem. It is an expanding sector.

What changed in 2026 is not the existence of ransomware. What changed is the structure of the industry that produces it. To understand the manufacturer's IT director and the countdown on his screen, you have to understand that the page he was looking at was not the work of a gang. It was the front end of a market, and that market had, in the months leading up to his Tuesday morning, undergone a quiet but enormous consolidation.

The first thing the data tells you is that the public face of ransomware is shrinking. In the third quarter of 2025, eighty-five distinct criminal brands were actively posting victims. By the first quarter of 2026, that figure had dropped to seventy-one. On its face, fewer active brands sounds like progress. It is not. The shrinkage is the visible artifact of a concentration that runs much deeper. The top ten ransomware groups in Q1 2026 accounted for roughly seventy-one percent of all listed victims. A few quarters earlier, when the field was more fragmented, that share had been closer to fifty-seven percent. The trajectory is the inverse of what the public conversation around law-enforcement takedowns and criminal-gang disruptions would suggest. As individual brands have been disrupted, the volume has not dispersed across the long tail of small players. It has flowed upward.

The clearest illustration is the top four. In the first quarter of 2026, just four operators — Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit, operating under their relaunched 5.0 brand — claimed forty-one percent of all leak-site postings. Forty-one percent of the global ransomware extortion market, by victim count, came from four logos. By any normal industrial measure, this is the signature of an oligopoly: a small number of dominant providers, each large enough to set terms in the market, each with the operational resilience to absorb the customers of failing competitors. The fact that the providers happen to be criminal does not change the shape of the curve. It only changes who pays.

At the top of the pyramid sits Qilin. For the third consecutive quarter, Qilin was the most active ransomware operation in the world. In the first three months of 2026, it posted 338 victims to its leak site. That single number deserves to be sat with for a moment. Qilin alone, in three months, posted more organizations than the bottom fifty ransomware brands combined. By the end of 2025, the group had published more than a thousand victims on its site after they refused to pay. Check Point's longer assessment was that Qilin's monthly output had nearly tripled across 2025, from roughly thirty-five victims a month in the first quarter to more than a hundred and fifty per month in the fourth. The trajectory looks less like a crime wave than like a company hitting its growth targets.

The Gentlemen, the second number worth pausing on, arrived in the top tier from almost nowhere. In the fourth quarter of 2025, the brand posted forty victims. In the first quarter of 2026, it posted 166. A more-than-fourfold jump in a single quarter is not the kind of growth a small crew produces by hustle alone. The available reporting attributes the surge to a pre-staged stockpile of compromised perimeter devices — roughly fourteen thousand FortiGate appliances that had been quietly compromised in advance and held in inventory. When the operation chose to scale, it did not need to break in. It already had the keys. This is what it looks like when a ransomware brand enters the market the way a venture-backed startup enters a market: not with a clever pitch, but with capacity already warehoused and a pipeline ready to convert.

LockBit's 163 Q1 2026 victims represent a third kind of dominance, and arguably the most telling. LockBit had been the largest ransomware brand on the planet in 2024 before it was struck by Operation Cronos, a multinational law-enforcement action that seized its infrastructure, exposed its source code and affiliate panel, and humiliated its leadership in public. For most of 2025, the brand fell out of the top thirty in major datasets. By the standards of any normal business, LockBit should have been dead. Instead, in September 2025, its operators relaunched the platform as LockBit 5.0. The new version was rebuilt to target Windows, Linux, and VMware's ESXi hypervisor in the same package, with faster encryption routines and randomized file extensions designed to make incident response harder. Its affiliate program required a deposit of roughly five hundred dollars in Bitcoin from anyone who wanted to join — a small barrier in absolute terms, but a meaningful signal of intent. The deposit was less about revenue than about screening. It was the criminal-market equivalent of a franchise application fee. By Q1 2026, the rebuild had landed; LockBit was back in the global top tier, posting more victims in three months than the next dozen smaller brands combined.

These are not separate stories. They are the same story told from three angles. Each of them is a story about the durability of capability when individual brands fall, and the speed with which the market reorganizes around the survivors.

The cleanest worked example of that reorganization is what happened to a brand called RansomHub. For most of 2024 and the first months of 2025, RansomHub was one of the largest ransomware operations in the world. It had accumulated more than seven hundred and sixty victims on its leak site since its debut. Its affiliate program offered a ninety percent revenue share — an aggressive payout designed to attract affiliates from rival platforms. In April 2025, RansomHub abruptly went offline. There was no announcement, no exit message, no public arrest. The infrastructure simply stopped responding. To anyone watching only the brand layer of the ransomware market, RansomHub's disappearance looked like a victory. A major operator was gone.

What actually happened was that hundreds of affiliates — semi-independent criminal contractors who had been running intrusions and deploying RansomHub's payload — were now unemployed. They had skills. They had access. They had relationships with initial-access brokers and infostealer crews who fed them credentials. What they did not have was a platform. Within weeks, they had found new ones. Broadcom's Symantec unit watched the migration in real time and reported, calmly, that former RansomHub affiliates rapidly moved to other groups. Akira and Qilin each ended up accounting for roughly sixteen percent of claimed ransomware attacks in 2025. The DragonForce operators ran active recruitment campaigns on criminal forums explicitly aimed at displaced RansomHub and LockBit affiliates. AhnLab, in its end-of-year analysis, described it as the largest structural shift in the ransomware ecosystem since LockBit's disruption in 2024.

The market did not get smaller. The market got faster. The top brands got bigger. The kind of takedown that, on a press-conference podium in 2018, would have been described as a generational blow to a criminal enterprise was, in the empirical record of 2025 and 2026, a redistribution event. The capability did not live in the brand. The capability lived in the labor market, in the access market, and in the infrastructure that connected them.

This pattern has a name, and the analysts who track it use the language deliberately. They call it creative destruction. The phrase is borrowed from twentieth-century economics, where it describes the way innovation in capitalist markets destroys old firms in order to free up resources for new ones. Applied to ransomware, the term sounds glib. There is nothing creative about a hospital diverting its emergency room or a manufacturer laying off its workers because the payroll system is encrypted. But the structural mechanic is real. When a ransomware operator is taken down, the resources it controlled — affiliates, infrastructure recipes, established communication channels, brand credibility — do not vanish. They are redistributed. They flow toward whichever surviving operator can absorb them most quickly. The takedown culls the brand layer of the market. It does not cull the capacity beneath it.

What this means in practice is that the strategic implications of every disruption are nearly the opposite of what the press release would suggest. If you read the Operation Cronos announcement in 2024 and assumed that LockBit's role in the ransomware ecosystem was over, you were wrong. The role was filled. The role was filled twice. Qilin grew into the vacuum during 2025, and then when RansomHub collapsed and added another wave of affiliates to the labor pool, Qilin grew again. When LockBit eventually relaunched, it did not have to rebuild its market — the market had been kept warm by the operators who had absorbed its old workforce. By the time the brand returned in late 2025, there were affiliates with current operational experience ready to come back. The brand reentered a position that had been held open for it by the structure of the industry itself.

To make sense of this, you have to abandon the mental model of ransomware as a malware category. You have to adopt the mental model of ransomware as a platform economy.

That phrase, platform economy, has been used so much in the technology trade press over the last decade that it has lost most of its edges. So it is worth being literal about what it means here. A platform economy is one in which a small number of central operators provide infrastructure — software, payments, communications, reputation, dispute resolution — to a much larger population of independent contractors, who use that infrastructure to deliver a service to end users. In a legitimate platform economy, the central operator is a rideshare company or a food-delivery app, the contractors are drivers or couriers, and the end users are passengers or diners. In the ransomware platform economy, the central operator is a brand like Qilin or LockBit or DragonForce, the contractors are affiliates who run intrusions, and the end users — the customers, in the bleakest possible sense of the word — are the organizations being extorted.

The structural elements that make this comparison precise are not metaphorical. They are observable. GuidePoint Security's analysis of Qilin describes the brand as openly recruiting affiliates on dark-web forums, licensing its ransomware to them, and retaining a percentage of each ransom payment in exchange for maintaining the software and providing access to leak-site and chat infrastructure. The reported affiliate split is eighty percent to the affiliate for ransom payments of three million dollars or less, and eighty-five percent to the affiliate for ransom payments above three million. Translated into language that any product manager would recognize, Qilin is offering a tiered revenue-share program in which the more successful partners receive a more favorable cut. The same analysis notes that Qilin's affiliate panel added features over 2025 including spam-campaign capabilities, automated ransom-negotiation tooling, and a service marketed to affiliates as a way to access legal counsel during the extortion process. The product is not the encryption routine. The product is the platform.

DragonForce went a step further, into a model that would be recognizable to anyone who has worked in the franchising side of the food-service industry. AhnLab and Dark Reading both describe DragonForce as offering an explicit white-label ransomware service. An affiliate can use DragonForce's infrastructure, encryption tooling, leak site, and negotiation portal while presenting itself to victims under a name of its own choosing. The affiliate retains roughly eighty percent of each ransom payment. To the victim, the brand on the leak site looks independent, distinct, perhaps small enough that the victim's incident-response firm has never heard of it. Beneath the brand, the infrastructure is shared. The model lowers the barriers to entry for criminals who do not have the technical skill to build their own ransomware operation, much as a fast-food franchise lowers the barriers for an entrepreneur who could not have built a restaurant chain on their own. Some of the new "groups" that appear briefly on leak-site dashboards and post a handful of victims before disappearing are not really independent ransomware operations at all. They are franchised storefronts running on someone else's backend.

This is one of the reasons that simply counting active brands tells you less than it used to. A market that looks fragmented at the storefront layer can be quietly consolidated at the service-provider layer. AhnLab and Secure Blink both reported in early 2026 that DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin had attempted to formalize this dynamic into something resembling a cartel. Public messaging from the operators floated language about creating equal competition conditions, avoiding open conflict between brands, and dictating market conditions to victims. Whether this represents a true alliance or a piece of theater meant to project strength is a question on which the threat-intelligence community has not yet reached consensus. ZeroFox and other reporting are skeptical of the alliance language, noting that public signaling between criminal brands is not the same as verified operational integration. The most useful way to read the cartel talk is as a market signal, not a structural reality. The leading ransomware brands are large enough, and aware enough of their own market position, that they have begun to use the public vocabulary of cartels.

What is not in doubt is that the platforms compete. They compete for affiliates by raising profit shares. They compete by adding panel features that make extortion easier or more lucrative. They compete by adapting payloads to new operating environments — Windows, Linux, and especially the hypervisors that power large enterprise virtualization. They compete on brand credibility. A LockBit affiliate trying to extort a hospital benefits from the brand's history of leaking data when victims do not pay. A Qilin affiliate trying to extort a law firm benefits from the brand's reputation for posting victim names quickly and visibly. The reputation is the platform's intangible asset. It is what makes the next ransom demand sound credible to the next chief financial officer who reads the chat window.

The platform competition also shapes who gets into the market in the first place. AhnLab's reporting and Secure Blink's analysis both describe a labor pool that behaves like a workforce. Affiliates can be acquired or lost. They can be poached with higher splits. They can be screened with deposits. They can be coached by automated tooling in the operator's panel. They can be governed by service-level agreements that look, from the outside, eerily like enterprise customer-success workflows. Some operators publish internal rules about which sectors are off-limits — not for ethical reasons but for risk management, because attacking hospitals or government agencies attracts the kind of law-enforcement attention that disrupts the brand. Other operators, like the relaunched LockBit 5.0, dropped previous prohibitions and explicitly allowed affiliates to target critical-infrastructure categories. Each of these decisions is a market positioning move.

There is a separate story sitting alongside the consolidation story, and it is worth naming briefly because it tells you something about how the market disposes of its losers. In the first quarter of 2026, twenty-one new ransomware brands appeared on the data-leak landscape. Most of them posted fewer than ten victims and then went quiet. A handful posted one or two and never returned. Some of these new entrants were almost certainly white-label storefronts running on someone else's infrastructure, briefly attached to an affiliate's experiment with a new brand identity. Others were genuine independent attempts at ransomware operations that failed to acquire the affiliates, the access, or the operational discipline necessary to compete in a market this saturated. The economics of the long tail are brutal. New brands without infrastructure cannot offer competitive splits. Without competitive splits, they cannot recruit affiliates. Without affiliates, they cannot generate the victim volume that builds the reputational pressure that makes future ransoms more likely to be paid. The few new brands that do break through tend to break through because they have something most new entrants do not: pre-staged access, an infrastructure partnership, or affiliates poached from a recently collapsed competitor. The Gentlemen had all three. Most of the twenty-one new brands of Q1 2026 did not, and the data-leak dashboards forgot about them within weeks.

The implication for defenders is unsettling. The strategic threat is no longer the brand you have heard of. The strategic threat is the structure that produces brands. A chief information security officer who builds a defensive program around the indicators-of-compromise associated with LockBit or Qilin or Akira is building against a moving target, because the underlying capability — the affiliates, the access, the negotiation infrastructure — can be reattached to a different brand within weeks. The defenders who have understood this most clearly have begun to talk about ransomware not in terms of who is attacking them but in terms of which inputs the attackers are using. The phishing pipelines that deliver the initial access. The session cookies and Entra ID tokens that get traded in the access markets. The remote-access appliances that sit on the perimeter and are sometimes already compromised at the moment a defender thinks they have just bought one. The hypervisors that run the company's virtual machines and that, if a ransomware operation gets to them, will let a single intrusion become a building-wide outage. This is the layer where the fight is actually happening. The brand on the leak site is the receipt.

The implication for everyone else is harder to articulate, because for most readers the leak site is something that happens to someone else. The countdown is on someone else's screen. The deputy walking in without knocking is in someone else's office. It is worth being precise about what is actually at stake. A hospital that is forced offline by ransomware diverts ambulances, postpones surgeries, and sends home patients whose chemotherapy infusions cannot be safely administered without electronic records. A school district that has its student-information system encrypted cannot run attendance, cannot pay its teachers on time, cannot release transcripts to the colleges that are waiting on them. A municipal utility that loses its customer-billing system stops sending bills, and three months later it cannot make payroll because the cash has stopped coming in. The leak site is a piece of marketing, but the marketing is attached to operations that have real consequences in the physical world. The data that gets posted does not stay on the leak site. It gets scraped, indexed, and reposted on criminal forums where credit-card details and Social-Security numbers and medical records become the raw material for the next year of identity-theft losses. The chemotherapy patient who is sent home is real. The school transcript that is not released on time is real. The retirees whose pension-fund administrator is currently negotiating with the operator they have never heard of are real, and the negotiation is happening on a chat interface that does not appear in any news cycle, on a Tuesday morning, in an office that the retirees cannot see.

A reader inclined to dismiss the platform framing as commentary should pause on one more figure. GuidePoint's annual ransomware report tracked one hundred and twenty-four distinct ransomware brands across 2025, a forty-six percent increase year over year, with overall ransomware victim numbers up fifty-eight percent. By the time Q1 2026 arrived, the count of active brands had begun to contract again, but the victim count had not. That is the empirical signature of a market in which entry is easy, exit is also easy, but the underlying demand for criminal services has not diminished. It is the signature of a market with low brand stickiness and high systemic capacity. New brands appear. Old brands die. The volume keeps growing.

There is a temptation, when looking at any market this large and this organized, to assume that volume equals revenue. That assumption is wrong. Leak-site postings are extortion attempts, not closed deals. GuidePoint Security explicitly cautions in its analysis of Qilin that the brand's high operational tempo should not be mistaken for high profitability. A substantial share of Qilin's victims, according to the analysts who have tracked the negotiations, refuse to pay. Qilin's reputation for rigid communications and inflexible negotiation may be part of why. Akira presents the inverse pattern. Its reported total proceeds — roughly two hundred and forty-four million dollars across the operation's lifetime — suggest a brand that has optimized for revenue per intrusion rather than for raw posting volume. Akira's targeting choices skew toward sectors where downtime is unusually expensive: consumer goods, industrial manufacturing, the kinds of businesses where each day offline translates into measurable lost output. An Akira affiliate may post fewer victims than a Qilin affiliate, but the average ransom may be larger and the average conversion rate higher. In the platform-economy framing, this is the difference between a scale strategy and a margin strategy. Both are legitimate strategies. Both are present in the market.

The IT director from the opening of this chapter eventually paid. He paid less than the operator initially demanded, because his negotiator was experienced, because his counsel had built a careful case for the manufacturer's actual ability to pay, and because the operator's chat representative — the human being on the other side of the keyboard, working from a script that had been refined across hundreds of prior extortions — was willing to accept a reduction. The director's company was not the largest victim posted that quarter. It was not the smallest. It sat somewhere in the middle of the 2,122 organizations whose names appeared on the leak sites between January and March of 2026. He never met the people who had taken his data. He never knew which brand of ransomware would be running on the affiliates' next intrusion, or whether the affiliate who had targeted him would be working under a different logo within six months. He knew only what he had paid, and what he had been required to assume about the integrity of the people who had been paid.

He had also, without quite realizing it, paid into the structural maintenance of a market. The ransom he sent helped fund the platform that hosted the affiliate that ran the intrusion that ended his company's quarter. Some portion of his payment flowed to the operator's brand, to its infrastructure, to its negotiation tooling, to its leak-site reach. Some portion flowed to whichever initial-access broker or infostealer crew had originally supplied the credentials that the affiliate used. Some portion flowed to the affiliate, who would use part of it to bid for the next set of credentials in the next access market. The market does not need any individual victim to keep functioning. But every victim feeds it, in a small and unwilling way.

This is the picture you have to hold in your head as you read the chapters that follow. Ransomware in 2026 is not a category of malware. It is a market with brands, with platforms, with affiliates, with deposits and revenue shares, with cartels that may or may not be real, with reputational competition and franchise models and pricing tiers. The malware is one product the market sells. It is no longer the most interesting product, and in some operations it has stopped being the central one. What the market is really selling is leverage — the ability to apply coercive pressure to organizations that depend on data, on identity, on operational continuity, on the integrity of their public reputation. The leak site that the IT director saw on Tuesday morning was a piece of marketing. The countdown was a sales tactic. The discount schedule was a pricing strategy. None of it was improvised. All of it had been refined across thousands of previous transactions, by people who had read the same data the analysts had and had built a business around the conclusions.

Disrupting individual brands will not stop this. It has not stopped it. It will not, in any horizon visible from where the threat-intelligence community sits today, stop it. The capability does not live in the brand. It lives in the labor market, in the access market, and in the infrastructure beneath them. That is the inheritance of the cartel year. The rest of this book is about what that inheritance looks like when you take it apart and examine the moving parts.

The next chapter starts with the franchise.

# Chapter 2: The Franchise Floor

The recruitment post had been on the forum for less than an hour when the man who would eventually decide to take the offer first opened it. He read it the way a contractor reads a request for proposals. There was a banner image at the top of the thread, a sober and almost corporate logo, and beneath it a numbered list of terms. The platform was rebuilding, the post explained. The previous version had been hit. The new version was better. Multi-platform support — Windows, Linux, the ESXi hypervisors that ran the virtual machines inside almost every datacenter worth attacking. Faster encryption routines. Randomized file extensions, so the incident-response firms could not write detection signatures against a single string. An onboarding deposit of roughly five hundred dollars in Bitcoin, payable up front. The post invited interested affiliates to reply through a specific channel, supply a brief résumé of past operational experience, and prepare to be screened.

The man reading the post had been an affiliate at RansomHub before its infrastructure went dark in April. He had spent the months since then working casual jobs on smaller platforms — a few intrusions through a brand that paid promptly but had a leak site nobody recognized, a couple of opportunistic monetizations through a white-label operation that had treated him well but offered very limited tooling. He had been thinking about the LockBit relaunch for weeks. Five hundred dollars was nothing, in the scheme of his operation. The deposit was not the question. The question was whether the brand was worth the deposit. He scrolled to the section on profit splits and then to the section on operational rules. He read the language about which sectors the platform would now permit affiliates to target. He noted, with a professional's eye, that the previous version's prohibitions on hospitals and critical-infrastructure categories had been quietly removed. He did the arithmetic on what a credible LockBit brand could extract from a manufacturer of the kind he had been working through for the past two years, and what fraction of that the platform would let him keep. He compared the result, mentally, to the terms DragonForce was offering on a parallel thread two pages down.

What he was doing, although neither he nor the platforms recruiting him would have used the language, was evaluating franchise agreements.

This is the layer of the ransomware industry that legacy reporting tends to miss, because legacy reporting still treats ransomware as a malware problem. The malware exists. The malware matters. But the people running the malware are not, in any meaningful operational sense, a gang. They are a service business with a partner channel, and the partner channel is competitive. To understand why the market behaves the way it does — why brands collapse and reappear, why the same affiliates show up under different logos within months of each other, why dominant platforms add features the way an enterprise software vendor adds features — you have to understand what the platforms are actually selling, and to whom.

The clearest example of the franchise model in its undisguised form is DragonForce. Across 2025, DragonForce did something that older ransomware operators would have found strange. It stopped trying to make itself the only brand its affiliates worked under. Instead, the operators built a back-end service: encryption tooling, a hardened leak site running on Tor, a negotiation portal, a victim-communications workflow, a set of templates and scripts for staging public pressure. Affiliates could license the back end and operate under their own branding. AhnLab and Dark Reading both described the arrangement in terms that any business analyst would recognize. DragonForce had become an infrastructure provider. The brand on the victim's chat window was the affiliate's. The infrastructure underneath was DragonForce's. The reported split was eighty percent of each ransom to the affiliate, twenty percent to the platform.

Dark Reading drew the analogy directly. DragonForce's model, the analysis observed, was structurally similar to a managed-services offering for threat actors. The platform provider was running what could plausibly be called a software-as-a-service business for extortion. Affiliates were the customers. The customers paid not in a monthly subscription but in a slice of each successful ransom. The platform provided the things a customer would otherwise have to build for themselves: secure encryption tooling tested across operating systems, a leak site that would stay up under enforcement pressure, a chat interface for negotiations, escalation playbooks, and an audience of journalists and incident-response analysts who would react when a victim's name appeared on the site.

The brilliance of the white-label model, from the operator's perspective, is what it does to the visibility of the market. From the outside, it looks like there are many small ransomware brands. Some are real. Some are storefronts. Some are an affiliate's first attempt at running their own operation under a name they picked themselves, briefly attached to DragonForce's back end while they tried it out. AhnLab noted in its 2026 outlook that this storefront layer can inflate the count of active ransomware groups without changing the count of active platforms. The data-leak landscape can look fragmented at the brand layer and remain consolidated at the service-provider layer. A defender looking at sixty-eight active brands in the Brandefense dataset is not necessarily looking at sixty-eight independent ransomware operations. They may be looking at twenty-five infrastructure providers and forty-three franchised storefronts. The market's true concentration is hidden by its own surface.

The economics of the eighty-percent affiliate split are aggressive enough that they reset expectations across the industry. Before its abrupt April 2025 collapse, RansomHub had been advertising what appeared to be the highest affiliate share on the open market: ninety percent of each ransom payment. The remaining ten percent went to the platform. By any historical standard, ninety percent was an extraordinary number. It was the kind of split a luxury rideshare service would offer drivers in a market it was trying to dominate, or a cloud-services vendor would offer partners in a category it had just entered. RansomHub used the high split as a recruiting weapon. The pitch to affiliates was unambiguous: switch to us, and you keep more of what you earn. The pitch worked. By the time RansomHub went dark, more than seven hundred and sixty victims had been posted to its leak site, and a substantial fraction of the global pool of experienced ransomware affiliates had run at least some operations through its platform.

When RansomHub vanished, the structural consequence was not the loss of a brand. It was the loss of a recruiting standard. The affiliates who had grown comfortable with a ninety percent split now had to negotiate down. The platforms that had been losing affiliates to RansomHub's terms now had room to set their own. DragonForce's eighty percent offer was not a generous concession in this environment. It was a market-clearing price, set high enough to attract displaced affiliates but lower than the standard RansomHub had been advertising. The price was an economic signal. It said, in effect, that the affiliate labor market had absorbed the loss of its most generous platform and was returning to terms that were sustainable for the operators rather than maximally favorable to the workforce. The seven hundred and sixty unemployed criminals who had been working through RansomHub found new platforms quickly. The new platforms paid a little less. Nobody, in the criminal labor market of 2025, was in a strong position to refuse.

Then LockBit returned, and the conversation about terms changed again.

LockBit's relaunch in September 2025 — branded LockBit 5.0, announced on a dark-web forum called RAMP — was a different kind of business signal. Operation Cronos had hit the previous version of LockBit hard in early 2024. The disruption had seized infrastructure, exposed source code, made the operator's affiliate panel public, and dragged the brand through what was effectively a series of public humiliations: leaked chat logs, doxxed identities, screenshots of internal disputes. For most of 2025, LockBit fell out of the top thirty ransomware operations in major datasets. By the standards of any other industry, the brand should have been dead.

The relaunch ignored that judgment. The new version of the platform — built, apparently, with the operators' remaining technical talent and a hardened set of lessons learned from the takedown — was framed not as a continuation but as a product launch. The technical features were itemized like a release-notes page. Windows, Linux, and ESXi targeting in one package, addressing the three environments that mattered to enterprise victims. Anti-analysis techniques designed to slow incident response. Faster encryption routines that reduced the window in which a defender could intervene during deployment. Randomized sixteen-character file extensions, which made signature-based detection harder. None of these features were revolutionary in isolation. What mattered was the packaging. LockBit was telling the affiliate market that it had returned with a better product than the one that had been seized.

The deposit was the other part of the signal. The new LockBit affiliate program required a Bitcoin payment of approximately five hundred dollars from anyone who wanted to join. The amount was small enough that any serious operator could pay it without thinking. The amount was meaningful, though, because of what it screened for. A deposit is what economists call a costly signal. The platform was not asking affiliates to prove they had money. The platform was asking affiliates to prove they had skin in the game. An affiliate who had paid into the platform had a reason to engage with the platform's rules, to use its infrastructure as intended, to act in ways that protected the brand. An affiliate who had not paid had no such incentive. The five hundred dollars was a vetting tool dressed as a fee. It said: we have learned from the way our previous version was infiltrated, and we are no longer letting anyone in for free.

The deposit also did something subtler. It established that LockBit was, once again, in a position to demand things from affiliates rather than offer concessions to attract them. The act of charging a deposit, however small, was a public statement that the platform did not need to compete with DragonForce's eighty percent split on the same axis. LockBit was selling something different. It was selling brand. The deposit said: paying us a small amount up front is worth it, because the leverage you will get when you put our name on a leak site is greater than the leverage you would get from any of our competitors. Whether that claim was true was a question for the next three quarters of victim counts to answer. The relaunch announced its first 163 victims in Q1 2026. The claim was holding.

What all of this meant for the criminal labor market, taken together, was that the terms of affiliate work had become highly differentiated. An aspiring operator weighing where to take their access could now choose between several distinct franchise models. They could go to DragonForce, pay nothing up front, run under their own brand name with DragonForce's infrastructure underneath, and keep eighty percent of each ransom. They could go to LockBit 5.0, pay the five-hundred-dollar deposit, operate under the LockBit brand with its reconstructed reputation, and accept whatever split the operators were offering — a figure not publicly confirmed in the available reporting but consistent, by analyst estimate, with the broader 60-to-80 percent industry range. They could go to Qilin, where the published affiliate terms were eighty percent on ransoms of three million dollars or less and eighty-five percent on ransoms above three million, with no required deposit but with the understanding that the platform's negotiation style was rigid and its conversion rate not always the best. They could go to one of the dozens of smaller storefronts running on shared infrastructure, accept the terms on offer, and hope the brand grew. Or they could try to build their own operation from scratch, knowing that they would have to handle their own malware development, their own leak site, their own negotiation portal, and their own brand-building in a market where established platforms had already absorbed most of the available affiliate labor.

The fourth choice was, for the vast majority of would-be ransomware operators, no longer a serious option. The infrastructure cost of running an independent operation had risen far enough that almost nobody who was not already at the top of the market could absorb it. Building a credible leak site required ongoing operational security work, the ability to keep Tor hidden services online under takedown pressure, and an audience of journalists and analysts who would take a new brand seriously enough to write about its victims. Building a negotiation platform required a chat interface, a victim-management workflow, and a record of past payments that could be referenced when victims asked, as victims always do, whether anyone had ever actually received their files back. Building a brand required, above all else, time. A criminal market in which the dominant brands had been refining their reputations for years was a market in which a new independent operator had to compete against name recognition they could not match. This is what franchise economies do. They make independent operation prohibitively expensive, and they channel new entrants into the franchise model.

It is at this point that the cartel question enters the conversation, and it has to be handled carefully.

In September 2025, on the same dark-web forum where LockBit had announced its relaunch, DragonForce posted a public proposal. The post invited LockBit and Qilin to enter into what DragonForce described as a coalition. The language used in the proposal, as documented by Secure Blink's open-source analysis, was specific. The coalition would establish what DragonForce called equal competition conditions. The participating brands would agree to avoid public insults and forum conflicts. They would, in DragonForce's words, work together to dictate market conditions. According to the reporting, LockBit's operator responded affirmatively. DragonForce later declared the coalition active.

If you take the public statements at face value, this is a cartel announcement. Three of the largest ransomware brands in the world, controlling some significant fraction of the global affiliate labor market and the global victim-extortion volume, have agreed in public to coordinate. The vocabulary is the vocabulary of cartels. The aspiration — to dictate market conditions to victims — is the aspiration of cartels. The structural logic, in a market this concentrated, is the logic of cartels.

The harder question is what the announcement actually accomplishes operationally. Cartel signaling, in any market, is not the same as cartel integration. A trio of consumer-goods companies that publicly announce they will not undercut each other on price can change market behavior even if they have no formal mechanism for enforcing the agreement. The announcement creates expectations. The announcement coordinates affiliate behavior. The announcement reassures victims, or terrifies them, depending on whose side of the chat window they are on. Whether the three operators actually share infrastructure, pool affiliates, or honor non-aggression terms in any verifiable sense is a different question, and the threat-intelligence community has been notably cautious about answering it.

ZeroFox, in its assessment of the coalition language, expressed skepticism that the announcement reflected real operational integration. The analysis pointed out that public signaling between criminal brands has always been part of the marketing layer of ransomware. Operators have long used forum posts to claim alliances they did not have, to inflate their perceived market power, and to attract affiliates who wanted to work for a winning team. ZeroFox treated the September announcement as unverified at the time of writing, and noted that subsequent operational behavior would be the meaningful test. Secure Blink's framing was more credulous, though still measured. It described the coalition as cartel-style coordination involving independent brands potentially agreeing to non-aggression pacts, affiliate-sharing frameworks, and infrastructure pooling. The word potentially is doing a great deal of work in that sentence. It is the analyst's way of saying that the claim is real, the language is real, but the operational reality cannot be confirmed from the outside.

This is the calibration that matters. The cartel language is not propaganda invented by threat analysts. The operators themselves used it, on their own forums, to one another. The fact that they used it tells you something important: the ransomware market has reached a scale and concentration where its participants think of themselves as cartel participants, where the language of coordination has become a useful tool of recruitment and pressure. That is a structural fact about the industry, and it is independent of whether the coalition is functioning as advertised. Even if no actual non-aggression pact is being honored, even if no actual infrastructure is being shared, the public announcement of one shifts what affiliates expect, shifts what victims fear, and shifts what new entrants believe is possible to negotiate.

The honest summary, then, is that the leading ransomware brands have begun talking like a cartel, and that their talk has measurable effects on the affiliate labor market and on victim perception, but that no available evidence confirms operational integration deep enough to call the coalition a true merger. The signaling is real. The coordination is partial. The threat to victims is amplified less by the coordination itself than by the fact that the operators have learned the vocabulary that amplifies it.

There is a second dimension to the franchise model that deserves more attention than it usually gets, because it goes to the heart of what the platforms are really selling. The encryption tooling is part of the product. The leak-site infrastructure is part of the product. The chat interface is part of the product. But the most valuable part of the product, increasingly, is something the legacy reporting tends to fold into a single dismissive phrase: negotiation support. What that phrase actually describes is a fully realized sales-and-collections apparatus that the platform provides to its affiliates, ready to use, refined across thousands of previous extortions.

The Anubis operation, a smaller brand than the top-tier platforms but a useful example of how the model develops, took this productization further than most. The reported Anubis package included what the operator called an investigative article service. The platform would take the data an affiliate had stolen and prepare a narrative analysis of it — the kind of write-up a journalist might produce, except aimed at the victim. The analysis identified the most damaging documents in the trove, framed them in ways calculated to maximize pressure, and packaged them into negotiation artifacts the affiliate could deploy at strategic moments. A non-technical affiliate who had obtained access through a purchased credential and exfiltrated a few terabytes of data did not, on their own, know which documents in the trove would cause the most pain. The platform did. The platform read the data. The platform wrote the threat. The affiliate delivered it.

This is a shift in the nature of the extortion product, and it matters because it changes what a successful ransomware operation requires of the criminal at the keyboard. A decade ago, a ransomware operator needed to be technically capable. They needed to understand intrusion, lateral movement, encryption, and the basics of operational security. A successful operation against a meaningful target required real expertise. The platformization of the last several years has steadily reduced that requirement. The technical work has been absorbed by the platform. The intrusion can be purchased upstream. The negotiation can be outsourced downstream. What is left for the affiliate to provide is the willingness to participate. The skill floor has dropped, and the population of people who can plausibly run a ransomware operation has expanded accordingly.

The macro consequence is straightforward, and grim. A criminal market with a lower skill floor is a criminal market with more participants. More participants chasing roughly the same pool of victims means more attempted extortions per quarter. More attempted extortions means more leak-site postings, more chat windows with countdown timers, more chemotherapy patients sent home, more municipal payrolls missed, more law firms breached, more school districts forced to revert to paper. The volume figures from the previous chapter — 2,122 victims in a quarter, 7,960 in a year, fifty-percent quarter-over-quarter growth — are the downstream signature of a franchise economy that has successfully lowered its barriers to entry. The franchise model is, among other things, a recruitment funnel for the next year's crime wave.

For the franchise operators themselves, the model also resolves a problem that earlier generations of ransomware brands struggled with: customer support for affiliates. If the affiliates are the platform's revenue-generating customers, the platform has to keep them happy. That means uptime, payment reliability, dispute resolution when an affiliate believes their share has been miscalculated, and responsiveness when something in the workflow breaks. The reporting on Qilin's panel describes features that would be familiar to anyone who has used the admin console of a legitimate cloud service. There is a dashboard. There are templates. There are automated workflows. There are escalation paths. The criminal-customer-success function is staffed. The competitive pressure forces operators to invest in it. A platform that cannot keep its affiliates productive will lose them to a platform that can.

There is one further wrinkle worth naming, because it complicates any affiliate's attempt to read the franchise market clearly. The same platform-economy dynamics that make franchise terms competitive also make platforms opaque even to their own participants. GuidePoint Security's reporting on Qilin includes an observation that is easy to read past on a first pass: the platform's high visible volume of leak-site postings is not the same as a high rate of actual payment. Qilin's affiliates produce intrusions and post victims with great efficiency, but a substantial portion of those victims, according to the analysis, simply refuse to pay. The Qilin negotiation experience has been described by victims and their incident-response advisors as rigid and inflexible — a poor sales process, in the language of legitimate businesses. The brand wins on volume. It loses on conversion. An aspiring affiliate looking at Qilin's published victim counts may overestimate the platform's actual revenue throughput, and may pick the wrong franchise based on numbers that look better than they perform. The criminal market, in other words, suffers from the same information asymmetries as any other market with a single signaling channel. The leak site is the marketing. The payment record is private. The decision gets made on the marketing.

The man reading the LockBit recruitment post that morning may never have run the comparison rigorously. He probably did not. Most affiliates do not. They read the splits, they look at the technical feature lists, they ask other operators on forums whether the platform pays out as promised, and they make a decision the way most contractors make decisions — on the basis of partial information, brand reputation, and the constraints of their own immediate need for work. He paid the deposit. He took the terms. Within a quarter, he was working through the LockBit panel on his first intrusion, using its negotiation workflow, watching its leak-site staging unfold in something close to real time when the moment came to apply pressure. He never met the operators. He had no formal contract. The agreement governing his relationship to the platform was the recruitment post he had read on the forum, the deposit he had paid, and the share of the next ransom he would either receive or not, depending on whether the victim paid.

This is what the franchise floor of the ransomware industry looks like in operation. It is not glamorous. It is not even particularly secretive. It is a labor market in which platforms compete for contractors with explicit terms, the contractors evaluate the terms with something approaching due diligence, and the structural balance of power is gradually consolidating around a handful of platforms that have learned to sound, talk, and behave like a cartel.

The platforms are the franchisors. The affiliates are the franchisees. The cartel signaling is the marketing. The reality, in any quarter you choose to examine, sits somewhere between coordinated and competitive — depending on which brand has just collapsed, which platform has just relaunched, and which terms have just been adjusted in response to the migration of labor.

The labor itself is the next chapter's subject. The franchise floor exists because there is a pool of affiliates large enough, skilled enough, and mobile enough to be courted with eighty-percent splits and screened with five-hundred-dollar deposits. That pool did not arrive by accident. It was built. Beneath the franchise layer is a deeper supply chain, an economy of access brokers and credential thieves and small-time specialists who have spent the last several years industrializing the work of breaking into companies in the first place. The franchises buy from them. The platforms run on what they sell. And the cost of breaking into your employer's network, before any ransomware is ever deployed, may be lower than the cost of dinner at a moderately good restaurant.

# Chapter 3: The Wholesalers

The listing scrolled past the buyer's screen at the pace of a stock ticker. It looked, in many ways, like one. There was a row of identifiers for the seller and the seller's reputation score, a short description of what was being offered, a price, an expiration timer, and a one-line note in the seller's broken English about freshness. The asset for sale was someone's saved browser session — the small slice of digital identity that proves, to every web service the user has ever logged into, that they are still themselves. The package included passwords cached by the browser, active session cookies for half a dozen corporate and personal services, and the device fingerprint the browser had been presenting when those services last checked. The seller's note said the session was less than seventy-two hours old. The price was forty dollars.

The buyer was an affiliate. He was looking for fresh access to corporate identity-provider sessions because he had a partner waiting on the other side of a chat window with deployment capability, a list of recently scoped targets, and a payload waiting to be staged. He scrolled past the forty-dollar listing without stopping. The seller's reputation score was middling, the company in the metadata was a small consumer-electronics retailer, and the target's identity-provider environment looked, from the breadcrumbs in the listing, like a Google Workspace deployment rather than the Microsoft Entra environment the affiliate's tooling was best optimized for. He kept scrolling. He stopped on a listing two screens down. This one was higher-priced — eighty-five hundred dollars — and the seller's reputation score was near the top of the marketplace. The description named a mid-sized regional bank in Europe, indicated domain-administrator-level credentials, claimed persistence through a recently established service account, and promised the seller would provide validation evidence on request. The seller had been on the marketplace for two years. The buyer clicked through to the seller's profile, scanned the prior transactions, and opened a private chat.

This is what a credential marketplace in 2026 actually looks like. It is not a clandestine bazaar in an alley somewhere. It is a website. It has search filters, seller-reputation scores, escrow services, and complaint procedures. The transactions move through cryptocurrency. The communications move through Tor and end-to-end-encrypted messengers. The market is competitive enough that sellers compete on the freshness of their inventory, the credibility of their representations, and the speed with which they answer chats. The buyers, the people who turn the inventory into intrusions, are the same affiliates who appeared in the previous chapter — the contractors who franchise their work through the dominant ransomware platforms. The keys they buy here are the keys they use there. The eighty-five hundred dollars the affiliate is about to spend on the regional bank's credentials is the cost of admission to a ransomware operation that may, if everything works, demand and sometimes receive a payment three orders of magnitude larger.

This chapter is about how that asymmetry exists, who maintains it, and what it means that the entire pipeline runs on commodity logic — quoted prices, freshness ratings, reputation scores, validation services, and a labor force as specialized as any legitimate logistics operation.

The supply chain begins, almost always, with infostealer malware. The infostealer is not glamorous. It is a small, mostly disposable piece of software that runs quietly on an infected computer, harvests whatever credentials it can find — saved browser passwords, autofill data, cookies, cloud-service tokens, cryptocurrency wallets, anything stored in the user's local environment that proves identity to a remote system — and ships the entire collection back to a command-and-control server. The user typically does not know they have been infected. The malware does not deploy ransomware. It does not steal money directly. It harvests, packages, and reports. Its operational signature is so low that most consumer antivirus tools do not catch it in real time.

The infostealer ecosystem is enormous. The malware is distributed through cracked software downloads, through phishing pages that mimic legitimate login screens, through public code repositories that host weaponized packages disguised as developer libraries, through fake CAPTCHA pages that ask users to copy and paste a command into a terminal, and through whatever new social-engineering vector the criminal community has converged on in the past six months. The infections accumulate. The harvested data flows to the operators. The operators package the data into what the underground calls logs — single-file archives, one per infected device, containing every credential and cookie the malware found on that machine. The logs are then sold, by the thousand, on Telegram channels and dark-web markets.

The numbers, when you look at them, are large enough to feel abstract. They should not be. Lyrie Threat Intelligence's 2026 access-economy analysis cited Flare's research finding that sixteen percent of infostealer-infected endpoints expose enterprise single-sign-on credentials. That number was six percent in 2024. In two years, the share of stolen-credential collections that include the keys to enterprise identity systems has nearly tripled. Seventy-nine percent of the logs contain Microsoft Entra ID credentials — the cloud-identity service that controls access to Microsoft 365 environments at most of the world's mid-sized and large organizations. And, most consequentially of all, roughly one million one hundred and seventy thousand logs include not just usernames and passwords but the session cookies that prove the user has already passed their multifactor authentication challenge. The credentials by themselves are valuable. The session cookies that accompany them turn the credentials into something more dangerous: the ability to log in as the user without triggering any of the second-factor checks that the user's IT department spent years implementing as a defense.

It is worth pausing on what this means in concrete terms. A session cookie is the small piece of data a website gives the browser after the user has logged in successfully — a digital token that says, in effect, this browser has already proven it belongs to this user. For convenience, websites accept the cookie on subsequent visits and do not ask the user to re-authenticate. If the user has signed in with multifactor authentication, the cookie typically embeds proof of that fact. If an attacker can obtain the cookie, they can present it to the same website and be treated as the authenticated user — including, crucially, as a user who has already cleared the multifactor challenge. The defense most security teams have spent the last several years putting in place — phishing-resistant second factors, push notifications, hardware tokens, one-time codes — is bypassed entirely. The attacker does not need the second factor. The attacker has the cookie. The cookie is the equivalent of a hotel keycard that has already been swiped at the front desk. The system trusts it because the system already trusted the swipe.

A million one hundred and seventy thousand of those keycards, on offer in a single marketplace ecosystem, mostly belonging to people who have no idea they have been compromised.

Above the infostealer crews sits a second class of operator: the Initial Access Broker. The IAB is the middleman of the access economy. Infostealer output, as the market has matured, has become noisy. A given log may contain a corporate password that has already been rotated. It may include a browser cookie that has expired in the hours between exfiltration and sale. It may belong to a personal device with no useful enterprise reach. It may identify a user who has the wrong privileges to be operationally interesting. The IAB's job is to sift. They acquire logs in bulk from the infostealer crews, run them through validation routines, identify which credentials still work, which ones unlock something valuable, and which ones are stale enough to be discarded. They take the validated access, enrich it with context — the target's industry, revenue, geography, endpoint security posture, identity-provider environment — and resell it to ransomware affiliates at a substantial markup.

The pricing reflects the value the broker adds. Lyrie's 2026 defensive playbook documented the IAB market's spread. Low-privilege credentials — a single ordinary user's login to a small or mid-sized organization, with no clear path to administrative access — sell for as little as twenty dollars. At the other end of the market, validated domain-administrator credentials with established persistence inside the target environment can sell for more than ten thousand dollars. The spread is not a function of how many bytes of data are being transferred. It is a function of what the buyer can do with the data after the purchase. Twenty dollars buys a foothold. Ten thousand buys a master keycard, already swiped through every door the target's identity system controls, ready to be used.

That spread, more than any other single fact about the access economy, is what defines the criminal market's economic logic. The cost of acquiring high-quality access to a Fortune 500 company through the legitimate path — research, custom phishing, vulnerability development, exploitation — would be measured in weeks of skilled labor and the kind of operational secrecy most criminals do not have. The cost of acquiring the same access on the IAB market is measured in the cost of a moderately good piece of consumer electronics. The price-to-value ratio is so distorted in the affiliate's favor that the only rational behavior, given the market's structure, is to buy.

This is the part the public conversation about ransomware almost never gets right. The story most people have absorbed is that ransomware operators are sophisticated hackers who break into networks through clever exploits and stealthy lateral movement. Some of them are. The dominant operating mode in 2026, however, is much simpler. Mandiant's 2025 incident-response dataset identified the three leading initial-infection vectors for ransomware as exploitation, stolen credentials, and email phishing. The order varies by quarter. The composition does not. In the majority of intrusions, the attacker did not break in. The attacker logged in. The credentials were already on the market. The session cookie was already in inventory. The exploit, when it was used, was often a vulnerability that the target organization had not yet patched but that the IAB community had already incorporated into its broker workflow. The work the attacker did was not to gain access. The work the attacker did was to monetize access that had already been gained, by someone else, and bought across a marketplace transaction that took five minutes.

The compression of the criminal kill chain follows directly from this market structure. Cisco Talos's first-quarter 2026 reporting, summarized in Lyrie's analysis, captured the speed of the change. Phishing, after several years in which exploitation and stolen-credential attacks had taken the lead, reemerged in Q1 2026 as the top initial-access vector in more than a third of incident-response engagements. At the same time, the share of engagements classified as pre-ransomware — meaning that defenders had identified attacker activity before any ransomware deployment occurred, giving them a chance to evict the intruder — fell from fifty percent in the first quarter of 2025 to eighteen percent in the first quarter of 2026. The most generous interpretation of this drop is that defenders have gotten better at stopping pre-ransomware activity, and the share is falling because the activity is being neutralized before incident responders get involved. The harsher interpretation is that the window between initial access and ransomware deployment has compressed to the point that, by the time a defender's incident-response team is called in, the encryption has already happened. The Lyrie reporting documented attack timelines where the interval from access purchase to ransomware deployment was measured in days, and in some cases under forty-eight hours. The affiliate who bought the regional bank's credentials at eighty-five hundred dollars may already have deployed the payload before the bank's security operations center has finished onboarding the alert it never noticed in the first place.

The labor partitioning that produces this speed is the structural counterpart to the market's price logic. Each role in the pipeline is narrow enough to be repeatable. The phishing crew runs phishing campaigns. The infostealer operator distributes malware and harvests logs. The validator runs credential-checking routines. The IAB packages access and resells. The affiliate operationalizes the access — moves laterally, identifies high-value targets within the network, exfiltrates the data the operator's platform will later use for extortion, and deploys the payload. The platform handles the negotiation, the leak site, the chat interface, and the payment. None of these jobs require the skills the others require. None of them are individually as complex as building a full ransomware operation from scratch would be. The whole pipeline works because nobody has to do all of it. They each have to do one thing well, and the market routes the output of each step to whichever specialist will use it next.

If the picture is reminiscent of an assembly line, the comparison is not accidental. Industrial supply chains evolved in the twentieth century because vertical integration — the practice of building every component of a product inside one company — proved less efficient than specialized firms trading components across a market. The criminal ecosystem has rediscovered the same lesson, two decades into the era of cloud services and digital identity. The vertically integrated ransomware gang, the kind that ran intrusion, deployment, and negotiation under one roof, was the model of the mid-2010s. The platform-and-supply-chain model of 2026 is the same industry, restructured.

There is one further specialization the modern access economy has added, and it is the most uncomfortable one to talk about. Above the access market sits a recruitment market for human assets. The dominant ransomware platforms, NCC Group's January 2026 reporting documented, have begun running formal recruitment programs aimed at insiders — employees of target organizations who can be persuaded to act against their employers in exchange for cash. The reporting describes the recruitment efforts as structured, with explicit commission tiers, anonymity guarantees, and incentive structures designed to scale beyond ad-hoc opportunism. Recorded Future's 2026 analysis tracked similar activity, with particular emphasis on the recruitment of contractors, gig workers, and the kinds of employees who hold privileged access without much organizational visibility: IT support staff, cloud administrators, identity-management technicians, members of security teams.

The pitch, in its honest form, is direct. An employee with access to an identity-provider console can, for a price, hand over administrative credentials that would otherwise require weeks of intrusion work to obtain. An employee with access to a cloud environment can introduce a backdoor in a configuration file. An employee at a managed service provider — the kind of vendor that, by its nature, holds privileged access across dozens of client organizations — can be the single point of compromise for an entire portfolio of victims. The reported commissions vary, but the structural logic is the same as the rest of the access market. The cost of buying an insider is a small fraction of the cost of recruiting and developing the technical capability the insider already has. The operator that successfully recruits one insider at a major MSP has acquired, in a single transaction, the capability to compromise every client the MSP serves.

For most readers, this is the moment to pause and consider how often, in the past month, they have personally interacted with someone whose access — their own access — would be worth a four- or five-figure commission to a criminal who knew how to ask. The help-desk technician who walked them through resetting their password. The IT contractor who reimaged their laptop. The cloud-services engineer in a vendor relationship two or three companies removed from their employer. The person at the call center whose login to a customer-relationship-management system could expose months of customer records. None of these people are villains. Most of them are paid modestly, working in jobs that do not, in the conventional understanding of corporate risk, sit at the center of any threat model. They are not the people the security team builds elaborate defenses around. They are the people the security team forgets. The criminal recruitment programs are not aimed at the people the security team is watching. They are aimed at the people the security team is not.

The implication for the affiliate labor market, looped back to where this chapter started, is that the supply chain has become its own competitive arena. The platforms compete for affiliates across the platform economy. The affiliates, in turn, compete for access — through credit, through reputation with brokers, through long-term relationships with infostealer crews that route premium inventory to favored buyers, through participation in the recruitment of insiders. The brokers compete for quality logs from infostealer operators. The infostealer crews compete on distribution reach and the freshness of their harvest. Rapid7's reporting on affiliate drift — the phenomenon of intrusion operators moving between ransomware brands after disruptions, leaks, or disputes — describes the labor mobility that has become a structural feature of the ecosystem. Affiliates do not stay loyal to any one platform. They follow the terms. The terms follow the supply.

This labor-market mobility is one of the reasons brand takedowns produce so little reduction in ransomware capacity. When a brand collapses, its affiliates do not disappear. They migrate. When an affiliate's preferred broker is arrested, the affiliate finds another broker. When an infostealer crew is disrupted, the underground absorbs the disruption within weeks and replacement crews scale to fill the gap. The market's resilience is a function of its disaggregation. Every link in the chain has substitutes. Every substitute is one chat conversation away. The market does not need any particular criminal to function. It needs the structure, and the structure is robust.

There is a defensive observation buried inside the compression data that is worth stating clearly, because it changes how anyone with operational security responsibility should think about the problem. The window between credential theft and ransomware deployment, in the majority of documented cases, is days to weeks. That is also, almost without exception, the only realistic detection window most defenders have. The credential theft itself is invisible on most endpoint-detection platforms — the infostealer is too small, too disposable, and too quick. The broker's validation is invisible because it happens entirely outside the victim's environment. The affiliate's first login, with valid credentials and a valid session cookie, looks exactly like the legitimate user. The defender's only realistic chance to intercept the attack before encryption begins is in the days-to-weeks gap between when the affiliate first establishes their foothold and when the payload is deployed. That gap is the only window. It is also a window the attackers know is closing. Every quarter, the documented compression of the kill chain narrows it further. The defenders are not losing this race by tactics. They are losing it by clock.

The buyer in this chapter's opening scene closed the chat with the seller forty minutes later, after the seller had provided validation evidence — a screenshot of the regional bank's administrative dashboard, a timestamped pull of a configuration file, the kind of proof that demonstrated the access was real and current. The escrow agent released the eighty-five hundred dollars from the buyer's wallet to the seller's. The buyer downloaded the credentials, the session cookies, and a brief operations-note from the seller about which of the bank's domain controllers were still running the slightly older patch level the broker had identified during validation. The buyer would, within seventy-two hours, log into the bank's identity-provider environment using the seller's package, escalate to additional service accounts, exfiltrate a particular set of files the operator's platform had told him would maximize negotiation pressure, and deploy the payload across the bank's virtual-machine environment in a single coordinated event.

He never met the user whose credentials he had bought. He never met the user whose session cookie had given him the multifactor bypass. He never met the seller, beyond the encrypted chat. He never met anyone at the bank. The work he did was not the work of breaking into a building. The work he did was the work of using a master keycard he had purchased at a discount, in a market that operated like any other commodity floor, to walk through doors that had been left, in every sense that mattered to the people who had built them, locked.

There is a quieter implication in all of this that deserves to be stated plainly, because it changes who the reader has to imagine as the relevant character in any specific intrusion. The criminals running ransomware operations in 2026 do not select their victims with any particular animus toward those victims. They select their victims through a market. The market routes inventory based on price, freshness, and the expected payoff from a given access. The affiliate who ended up targeting the regional bank did not choose the bank because of anything the bank had done. The affiliate chose the bank because the bank's credentials were on the market that morning, at a price that matched the affiliate's tolerance, with validation evidence that satisfied the affiliate's standards. The bank's role in the transaction was not as a target but as inventory. The selection was made by the supply chain, not by the criminal.

This has implications for how individuals — not just security teams, but everyone — should think about their own exposure. The infostealer that harvested the credentials that ended up on the marketplace was probably installed weeks or months before the bank was compromised. The user who got infected may have downloaded a cracked piece of software, clicked through a fake login page, or copied a command into a terminal at the suggestion of a CAPTCHA that did not actually exist. The user is, in almost every case, not at the bank. The user is somewhere upstream, on a personal device, on a coffee-shop Wi-Fi network, using a browser that has saved years of corporate logins because the browser was designed to save them and the user never thought to turn the feature off. The credentials that end up in the infostealer log may belong to the user's employer. They may belong to a vendor the user works with. They may belong to a contractor the vendor uses. Two or three steps of indirection are normal in this market. The compromise that ends up costing a regional bank millions of dollars may have started with a person who has no relationship to the bank at all, beyond an obscure software vendor that connects them.

A reasonable reader will object at this point that this picture cannot be quite right, because it leaves out the part where the user is supposed to be careful. The user is supposed to use unique passwords, store them in a password manager, enable multifactor authentication on every account, keep their browser clean of saved credentials, and avoid the kinds of downloads that distribute infostealer malware in the first place. All of that advice is correct. None of it is, on its own, sufficient. The infostealer ecosystem has scaled enough that even users who do most of those things correctly are exposed if any of their devices, at any point in the past two years, were running software that quietly harvested what was stored. The session cookies in the marketplace's million-plus log inventory are not from people who were uniquely careless. They are from a representative slice of the working population. The market acquires that slice continuously, processes it through its broker layer, and routes the resulting access to whichever affiliate can afford it. The careful user is a smaller fraction of the inventory. The careful user is not zero.

For organizations, the picture has a parallel implication. The security investments that have absorbed most of the budget over the past several years — perimeter defenses, endpoint detection, multifactor authentication, security-awareness training — are not wrong. They are necessary. But they are oriented around a threat model in which the attacker is breaking in. The threat model has changed. The attacker is logging in. The defenses oriented around break-in are not the defenses that catch login. Catching login requires different signals: anomaly detection on session origins, conditional-access policies that re-evaluate trust mid-session, continuous identity-risk scoring, mandatory token rotation, fast invalidation of stolen sessions, and the kind of identity-provider hygiene that almost no organization has actually invested in at the scale the threat requires. The market structure of the access economy has effectively repriced enterprise security. The investments that worked five years ago are not adequate today. The investments that would be adequate today are not yet in place at most organizations. The gap is the affiliate's opportunity.

The next chapter is about where, in this enormous and indifferent supply chain, the keys end up pointing. The market does not produce victims at random. It produces them where the leverage is highest, where the recovery is hardest, and where the cost of refusing to pay is most catastrophic. Those choices are made deliberately. They have a map.

# Chapter 4: The Map of Coercion

The hospital's chief information officer was halfway through her morning when the page came in. The lab system was down. Within fifteen minutes, the radiology system was down. Within forty, the electronic health record had become unreliable enough that the emergency department's charge nurse had begun an orderly transition to paper documentation, which the hospital practiced for exactly this scenario, and which everyone hoped never to use. By the time the CIO got to her office, the leak site already had her hospital's name on it. The countdown had started. The data the operator claimed to have taken included patient demographic files, imaging archives, billing records, and, most worryingly, the database that linked patient identifiers to the treatment regimens their oncologists had scheduled across the next ninety days.

The chief medical officer was already calling. There were three elective surgeries on the morning schedule that were being moved. There were six chemotherapy infusions scheduled for the afternoon that required electronic verification of dosage against patient records before they could be safely administered. The infusion suite would have to decide, patient by patient, which treatments could continue using the printed regimen sheets the pharmacy had kept as backups and which would have to be deferred. The transplant team's protocols required imaging review through a system that was no longer responding. The radiology technicians could acquire the images. They could not, until the system came back, share them with the surgeons who needed to see them before opening a chest. Six patients would, by the end of the day, leave the hospital without the treatment they had come in to receive. Their oncologists would call them by phone. The conversations would be careful. They would not, by hospital policy, describe the actual reason for the delay.

The CIO had been to the conferences. She had read the threat-intelligence reports the hospital subscribed to. She knew, as everyone in her position knew, that her hospital had been targeted because it was a hospital. The map of ransomware victims had a shape. The shape was not random. The hospital sat on the shape exactly where any disciplined analyst, looking at the strategic logic of the criminal market, would have predicted it to sit.

This chapter is about that map, and about what its contours tell you about an industry that has learned, with great precision, where coercion pays.

The single most important number to put on the table is the one ENISA, the European Union's cybersecurity agency, has been quietly publishing for several years and which most of the public has not absorbed. In its 2025 threat-landscape assessment, ENISA found that ransomware accounted for eighty-one point one percent of all cybercrime activity affecting organizations in the European Union during the reporting period. Not eighty-one percent of cybercrime damage. Not eighty-one percent of cybercrime headlines. Eighty-one percent of all measured cybercrime activity against EU organizations was ransomware. The remaining nineteen percent of cybercrime — fraud, business-email compromise, espionage, distributed-denial-of-service attacks, every other category of digital harm being inflicted on the European economy — accounted, collectively, for less than what ransomware contributed on its own.

That number is the size of the iceberg the rest of this book describes. The platforms and franchises of the first three chapters, the access economy of Chapter Three, the cartel signaling and the leak-site theater — all of it is in the service of an extortion economy that has, by ENISA's measurement, captured roughly four-fifths of the cybercrime activity directed at one of the world's largest blocks of advanced economies. The remaining four-fifths of cybercrime fights for the leftover one-fifth of attention. Ransomware is not one threat among many. It is the threat, in any reasonable measurement of where criminal energy in cyberspace is currently being directed.

The second number is what makes the ENISA assessment land in the real world. ENISA also published, in the same reporting period, the sector breakdown of where that cybercrime activity was concentrated. Digital infrastructure and services — the cloud providers, the hosting companies, the data centers, the telecom carriers, and the software vendors that constitute the technical substrate of modern commerce — absorbed thirteen point seven percent of all cybercrime activity. Manufacturing absorbed thirteen point twenty-six percent. Business services absorbed nine point seven percent. Within the subset of incidents classified specifically as ransomware claims, manufacturing led at fourteen point nine percent, with digital infrastructure and services at ten point three percent. Within data-breach claims — the related but distinct category of incidents in which data exposure rather than encryption was the central event — digital infrastructure and services jumped to twenty-eight point two percent and public administration came in at sixteen point eight percent.

These are not, in the way numbers usually are not, especially memorable. They are easier to remember as a picture. Imagine the European economy laid out as a map of industries, each occupying floor space proportional to its size in the cybercrime data. Manufacturing's region is one of the largest, dominated by automotive component suppliers in southern Germany, machine-tool builders in northern Italy, food processors in Spain, aerospace subcontractors in France, chemical plants in Belgium. Digital infrastructure's region overlaps with manufacturing in the cloud services and data centers that support it, but extends into the telecoms and platform companies that everyone uses. Public administration sits where the government services do — municipal information-technology environments, regional health authorities, school districts, court systems, social-services databases. Business services covers law firms, accountants, consultancies, the back-office providers that connect the rest. Healthcare sits where the hospitals and clinics and pharmaceutical labs are. Education, finance, and media occupy smaller regions, each with its own concentrations.

The ransomware market visits each of these regions, in roughly the proportions the data shows, with the deliberateness of a sales team prospecting territories. The visits are not opportunistic. They are weighted.

The geographic mix tells the same story from a different angle. Among EU member states, Germany absorbed twenty-three point four percent of all ransomware and data-breach claims tracked during the reporting period — close to a quarter of the entire bloc's incident load. Italy followed at eleven point three three percent. Spain at nine point eight. France at nine point five. Belgium at three point seven. The ranking does not follow population. Germany has more victims than Italy and Spain combined, but it does not have more people than Italy and Spain combined. The ranking follows what these economies do. Manufacturing dominates across all five. The German Mittelstand, the smaller and mid-sized industrial companies that make the components other economies depend on, sits at the center of the German share. Italian manufacturing in the north — automotive, mechanical engineering, textile machinery, the families of small specialist firms that supply the rest of Europe's industry — accounts for the Italian share. Spanish food processing and Spanish automotive contribute to the Spanish share. French aerospace, French automotive, French chemicals fill the French share. Belgium's smaller percentage reflects a smaller industrial base, but within that smaller base, manufacturing is also dominant. The map of ransomware victims in the EU is, to a first approximation, the map of European manufacturing, with cloud infrastructure and the public sector overlaid on top.

The strategic logic behind this geography is the part that, if you do not look at it directly, you can miss. ENISA's analysts, in the same report, named the logic explicitly. Ransomware operators select victims based on coercive leverage. That coercive leverage is highest in sectors where outages translate quickly and unambiguously into business interruption, public-service disruption, regulatory exposure, patient-safety risk, reputational harm, and contractual penalties. The criminal market has read the European economy with the eye of a sales engineer studying a customer base. It has identified the industries where the cost of being down for a week is so high that paying a ransom looks, to a board of directors weighing the alternatives at three in the morning, like the rational choice. Then it has aimed.

Take manufacturing as the cleanest example. A German auto-parts supplier whose enterprise-resource-planning system goes down at six in the morning has, by eight, lost the ability to track which subcomponents are due to ship to which customer factories that day. The customer factories are running just-in-time production. They have, by industry convention, between four and twelve hours of inventory for any given part. If the supplier's shipping system is still down at noon, the customer factories will begin shutting down their own production lines by mid-afternoon. The cost of one of those customer factories going dark for a day, in lost output and idle labor, is typically in the millions of euros. The cost of the supplier's outage is therefore not just the supplier's own losses; it is a cascading bill that the supplier will, by contract, often be liable for. By the end of the week, the supplier's legal department is on the phone with three of its largest customers, trying to negotiate force-majeure clauses while the production floor sits empty and the operations team tries to rebuild systems from whatever backups have survived. The ransom demand, presented in the chat window on a leak site that has just published a teaser of the supplier's customer-contract files, arrives in this environment. The math is brutal. The math is also why the supplier is on the map.

Healthcare is the same story with an added dimension that no other sector matches. ENISA flagged it explicitly in its 2024 reporting on ransomware impact on EU organizations: the agency expressed specific concern about the disruption of medical procedures. The phrase is bureaucratic. The underlying reality is not. A hospital that cannot run its electronic health record cannot safely administer chemotherapy in the volume it had scheduled. A regional health authority that loses access to its imaging archive cannot perform the surgical reviews it had planned. An ambulance service whose dispatch system is down cannot route trucks to incidents with the latency the system was designed for. Each of these failures has a measurable downstream effect on patient outcomes. Lab results take longer. Imaging studies take longer. Drug-interaction checks revert to paper systems with higher error rates. Treatment decisions get made on incomplete information. The probability that something will go wrong in a given case rises. ENISA's concern about medical procedures is a sober institutional way of saying that ransomware incidents in healthcare environments degrade patient safety in real time, and that the degradation is not theoretical.

The reason hospitals end up on the map of ransomware victims is not despite the patient-safety stakes. It is because of them. The hospital that pays the ransom on the second day of the outage is making a calculation that includes, somewhere in the math, the worry that an additional patient might be harmed if systems do not come back faster. That calculation is what makes the leverage work. The operator does not need to threaten violence. The operator only needs to be the proximate cause of an environment in which patient safety is degrading in real time. The leverage is built into the target's mission. The criminal market has selected, with terrible precision, the targets whose missions are most easily converted into pressure to pay.

Finance is the same kind of leverage, organized around a different axis. A bank that loses its core processing systems cannot post transactions, cannot clear checks, cannot move money. A bank that loses its customer-facing portals cannot serve its customers. The regulatory exposure compounds the operational exposure. Banking regulators in every advanced economy have, in the past several years, tightened the rules around incident reporting, system uptime, and operational resilience. A bank that is down for more than a defined window is required, by regulation, to disclose the incident to its supervisors. The disclosure triggers examinations, capital surcharges, and, in some jurisdictions, public reporting that can affect deposit confidence. The ransomware operator targeting a bank is not just selling the bank a decryption key. The operator is selling the bank an end to the cascade of regulatory and reputational consequences that begins the moment the systems go dark.

Public administration is the leverage of last resort. A municipal government whose systems are down cannot pay its employees, cannot process benefits, cannot collect taxes, cannot run schools, cannot manage the water-and-power utilities under its authority. The pressure to restore services in a town that has been knocked offline is, in a different way from a hospital, immediate. There is no negotiating room. The mayor has to act. The mayor's options are bad. Pay the ransom and hope. Refuse and absorb weeks of degraded services, public anger, and the political consequences. The leak-site countdown is a publicity event the town's leadership cannot afford to let run its course. The criminal market has discovered that small and mid-sized municipalities are particularly vulnerable to this dynamic, because they often lack the technical capacity and budget to maintain the kind of recovery infrastructure that would make payment unnecessary. They appear on the data-breach side of ENISA's tracking — sixteen point eight percent of data-breach claims — at a share well above their economic weight, because they do not have the leverage to negotiate the operators down and they do not have the resilience to refuse outright.

Digital infrastructure is the leverage multiplier. The cloud-services providers, the data centers, the software vendors that sit underneath everything else are the operators' force multipliers. A successful intrusion at a managed service provider does not give the affiliate one victim. It gives them access to dozens — sometimes hundreds — of the MSP's clients. The same logic applies to enterprise software vendors whose products are deployed across thousands of customer environments. A ransomware operation that compromises a single vendor's update channel can deploy its payload across the vendor's entire customer base in a single coordinated event. The data-breach share for digital infrastructure — twenty-eight point two percent, more than twice its share of broader cybercrime activity — reflects this. The category is not just larger than its baseline share. It is larger because compromise here scales. One intrusion buys many victims.

Transport and logistics sit in a similar leverage zone, with a slightly different dynamic. A logistics provider whose tracking and routing systems go dark cannot tell its customers where their shipments are. A port operator whose terminal-management system is encrypted cannot move containers. A trucking firm whose dispatch platform is down cannot route loads. The cascading effects ripple outward into every industry that depends on the movement of goods, which is to say every industry. ENISA's listing of transport among the high-extortion-value sectors reflects this cascade structure directly: outages translate, within hours, into production stoppages at every customer downstream. The criminal market does not need to understand global logistics in detail to exploit it. The market needs to know only that a logistics provider's outage produces a phone call from the chief executives of its largest customers within hours, and that those phone calls produce pressure to pay within days. The pressure does the explaining.

Media organizations represent a smaller share of the data but a notable category, because the leverage there is essentially reputational. A media company whose publishing systems are encrypted may lose hours of news production. A media company whose journalistic source files are stolen and threatened with publication may lose something more valuable: the trust of the sources who confide in them, the protections those sources rely on, and the integrity of stories that have not yet been published. The operators who target media organizations are exploiting the asymmetry between the cost to the operator of leaking the data and the cost to the media organization of having it leaked. The cost asymmetry is the leverage. The leverage is, again, why the targets appear on the map.

There is a useful counterintuition that the ENISA numbers expose. The most common mental image of a ransomware victim is the small business that gets hit by accident — the family-owned manufacturer in a regional town, the local accounting firm, the small clinic that does not have the budget for proper backups. Those victims exist. The data confirms that ransomware affects small organizations in large numbers. But the picture the ENISA reporting paints, when you look at the strategic distribution of victims, is that the criminal market is not primarily aiming at small. The criminal market is aiming where the leverage is. Sometimes the leverage is in small. Often it is not. The bigger picture is that ransomware operators have been refining their targeting logic across the same several years that the operational franchise market described in the preceding chapters has been refining its tooling. The targeting is the other half of the optimization. The franchise produces the affiliates. The market supplies the access. The strategic-victimology logic decides where the access gets used.

It is worth being honest about what the data does not capture. Leak-site postings and ENISA's tracking together represent what is observed and reported, which is not the same as the full incident census. Public-administration ransomware incidents are underreported in some jurisdictions because of the reluctance of officials to publicize attacks that may not have resulted in disclosed data theft. Healthcare incidents are underreported in jurisdictions where mandatory-disclosure rules are weaker or where investigations remain ongoing. Manufacturing's prominence in the data is partly a function of the sector's disclosure obligations to publicly traded parent companies and to supply-chain partners, which make ransomware incidents in manufacturing harder to hide. The visible map of victims is therefore likely closer to a lower bound than to a true census. The shape of the iceberg above the waterline is informative, but the iceberg is bigger than the part you can see.

The macro pulse of the industry confirms that the lower bound is rising. Broadcom's reporting found that claimed ransomware attacks in 2025 reached their highest recorded level. The collapse of major operations such as RansomHub created brief dips in the rolling totals; the dips closed within weeks as activity resumed through other brands and affiliates, exactly as the consolidation dynamic of the access economy would predict. GuidePoint Security characterized 2025 in similar terms — a record-setting year defined by RaaS instability, alliance signaling, mass exploitation of vulnerabilities, and a noticeable Q4 acceleration. The combination of more incidents and tighter strategic targeting means the map is not just larger this year than last. It is also more concentrated in exactly the places the leverage works.

The fragmentation-to-convergence pattern that runs underneath all of this victimology is worth naming, because it sits in the same place the franchise model sits structurally. The brand layer of the ransomware industry has fragmented. There are more brands. There are more storefronts. There are more new logos appearing on leak sites every quarter. But the strategic behavior of the brands, taken as an industry, has converged. The brands target the same sectors. The brands extract data and use it for leverage in the same way. The brands aim at the same kinds of infrastructure dependencies. The brands compromise identity systems and suppress recovery infrastructure in the same patterns. ENISA's reporting puts it directly: the ransomware ecosystem is more fragmented in branding but more convergent in strategic behavior. The targeting playbook is now shared. The platforms differ in their pricing, their branding, and their negotiation styles. The choice of victims has been standardized across the industry. The reason a German auto-parts supplier in Stuttgart, a public-administration office in Naples, a regional hospital in Catalonia, and a logistics provider in Antwerp all end up on leak sites in the same quarter is that the playbook for selecting them was the same playbook. The playbook is what the industry converges on.

There is a related consolidation in the criminal market's reading of supply-chain leverage. Operators have begun explicitly selecting victims for what their information-and-communications technology dependencies extend into. A target's MSP. A target's enterprise-software vendor. A target's identity-provider configuration. A target's third-party access partners. Each of these dependencies can become its own leverage point, either because the target's recovery depends on them or because the target's outage cascades through them to additional victims. The map of coercion is not just a map of victim industries. It is a map of victim dependencies. The strategically-selected victim is selected not just because of what they do, but because of what they cannot operate without.

The CIO at the hospital from the opening of this chapter eventually had to make the decision the operator had been waiting for her to make. The hospital's board met that night. The legal counsel laid out the regulatory exposures. The chief medical officer laid out the procedure-deferral counts. The chief financial officer laid out the cost projections for various lengths of system outage. The recovery team's preliminary assessment was that bringing the systems back from backups would take, depending on how many of the backups had survived the operator's deliberate targeting of the backup environment, between four and eleven days. Each day of outage had a cost in deferred surgical schedules, in chemotherapy postponements, in the gradual degradation of the hospital's ability to operate within its safety standards. The ransom demand, in absolute terms, was smaller than the projected cost of an eight-day outage. The math, as the math always was in these situations, made paying look rational. The math was the leverage. The leverage was the reason the hospital had been chosen.

She did not, in the room that night, say out loud what she had been thinking since the systems first went dark. The thinking was simpler than the formal analysis. The hospital had been targeted because it was a hospital. Some criminal market, operating somewhere in the supply chain that fed every ransomware affiliate currently active in the European theater, had identified the regional hospital network as a target whose downtime translated into patient harm at a rate that maximized the operator's expected ransom. The criminal market had not known her name. It had not known the names of her chemotherapy patients. It had known only that the hospital was the kind of target where the leverage worked. The leverage had worked. The board, after three hours of discussion, authorized the payment.

The next chapter is about what the operator had done, in the days before the ransom demand was issued, to make sure the payment would feel like the only realistic option. The hospital's backups had not survived the attack intact. The hospital's identity systems had been compromised in ways that made any clean recovery effectively impossible within the window the patient-safety calculus required. The hypervisors that ran the hospital's virtual machines had been encrypted at a level that made selective restoration painfully slow. These outcomes were not accidents. They were the work of an operator whose business model had moved past simply encrypting files and had learned to attack, with deliberate precision, the systems on which recovery depends. The map of coercion is a map of where the operators aim. What follows is the story of what they do, in the moments before they pull the trigger, to make sure the target cannot move.

# Chapter 5: The Recovery Trap

The backup administrator's phone vibrated against the bedside table at 3:14 in the morning. He had been on call for the week and the alert system had been quiet, which was the way he liked it. He picked up the phone, squinted at the notification, and was on his feet before he had finished reading. The alert said that the company's primary backup repository had become unreachable. A second alert, arriving thirty seconds behind the first, said the same thing about the secondary repository. By the time he had pulled on a sweatshirt and opened the laptop on the kitchen counter, a third alert was sitting in his inbox. The immutable snapshots — the supposedly tamper-proof copies that the company had paid a substantial premium to maintain as the last-resort safeguard against exactly this scenario — were also failing their integrity checks.

He logged into the backup console. The dashboard rendered, slowly. Where there should have been a green-and-gray graph of the previous week's restore points, there was a sparse red bar chart, with the bars representing not restore points but error counts. The most recent successful backup was forty-one days old. The administrator stared at that number for a long time. Forty-one days was older than the most recent quarterly close. It was older than three weeks of customer records, every new employee onboarded since the start of the previous month, and the company's entire migration of its enterprise resource planning environment to a new vendor's cloud. He scrolled down to the audit log. The deletions had begun nineteen days ago. Whoever had done it had been careful. They had not, in the way amateurs would have, simply wiped the snapshots in a single transaction. They had deleted them gradually, one or two a day, at times that fell inside the backup software's normal maintenance windows, in ways that did not trigger the integrity-monitoring alerts that should have caught a mass deletion. By the time anyone noticed, the safety net was gone.

The administrator picked up the phone again. The on-call security engineer answered after one ring. The administrator said, in the way people say things they have rehearsed in their heads for years, that the backups were gone. He used the present tense because the situation was current. He did not yet know that, by 6 a.m., the company's identity-provider environment would also be compromised, that the domain administrators would discover their own privileged accounts had been quietly stripped of permissions overnight, that the VMware hypervisors hosting four hundred and twenty production virtual machines would receive their encryption payload within ninety minutes of the first ransom note. He did not yet know any of this. He knew only that the recovery path he was supposed to maintain — the entire reason his job existed — had been systematically dismantled while he had been on vacation, while he had been sleeping, while he had been doing the things people do when they trust that the systems they have built are still standing behind them.

This chapter is about what the operators did to him, and what they have been doing to nearly every organization that ends up on a ransomware leak site in 2026, in the weeks before any encryption ever began.

The defining strategic shift of 2025–2026 ransomware operations is the one that is hardest to see from the outside, because it does not show up in the visible artifacts of an attack. The encryption shows up. The leak-site countdown shows up. The chat window with the ransom demand shows up. What does not show up, until it is too late, is the careful, deliberate, weeks-long work of destroying the victim's ability to recover. Ransomware operators in 2026 are no longer primarily in the business of encrypting files. They are in the business of disabling the systems that would otherwise let the victim refuse to pay. The encryption is the punctuation. The real work is everything that comes before it.

ENISA's threat-landscape reporting puts the shift in directly: a defining feature of 2025–early 2026 ransomware operations is the deliberate targeting of recovery infrastructure. The phrase is dry. What it describes is a transformation in what a ransomware operation actually is. The strategic blueprint, as the reporting documents it, has three primary targets. Backups, because they are the victim's fastest path to restoration. Identity systems — Active Directory in on-premises environments, Microsoft Entra ID in cloud environments — because controlling identity controls authentication, privilege escalation, lateral movement, and the enforcement of security policy. And hypervisors, especially VMware's ESXi platform, because hypervisors concentrate dozens or hundreds of critical workloads behind a small number of administrative control points. Compromise any one of these well enough, and the victim's recovery path narrows. Compromise all three, and the recovery path closes.

Backups are the first target, because they are the most obvious one. A traditional ransomware attack against a victim with good backups was, structurally, a recoverable event. The encryption happened. The recovery began. The systems came back from the backup repository. The ransom went unpaid. For most of the history of ransomware, the central argument the security industry made to its customers was straightforward: invest in backups, isolate them from your production environment, test the restore process, and you can survive an attack without paying. The advice was correct. The operators have spent the past several years rendering it insufficient.

The reason is that operators have, with great deliberation, learned to attack the backup environment as part of the intrusion phase, not as part of the encryption phase. The administrator who opened his laptop at three in the morning was not looking at the residue of a fast attack. He was looking at the residue of a patient one. Nineteen days of careful deletion. Timed inside maintenance windows. Calibrated below the threshold of mass-deletion alerts. The intruder had been inside the backup environment for at least three weeks before any encryption began. The intruder's goal during those three weeks was not to take anything. The intruder's goal was to remove the safety net. The encryption, when it finally happened, would happen against an environment where restoration was no longer possible. The victim would arrive at the negotiation table not as someone weighing payment against the cost of restoration. The victim would arrive as someone weighing payment against the cost of having no backups at all.

The technique works across most backup architectures, because most backup architectures, even the ones that market themselves as immutable or air-gapped, depend on administrative interfaces that are reachable from the production network. An intruder who has compromised the production identity system has, in practice, the credentials to reach the backup console. From there, the intruder can age out snapshots, modify retention policies, alter integrity checks, and — in the most advanced cases — quietly modify the backup software itself to report successful backups that no longer correspond to recoverable data. Some backup vendors have responded with hardened immutable architectures, requiring physical-token approvals for deletion or out-of-band administrative networks. These help. They do not, in environments where the rest of the security program has not been hardened to match, prevent the patient kind of attack the backup administrator was now staring at. The operator's three-week dwell time inside the backup environment was the audit.

Identity is the second target, and in some ways it is the more important one. The ENISA framing is direct: compromise of Active Directory or hybrid identity systems lets attackers control authentication, privilege escalation, lateral movement, and security-policy enforcement. Each of those words carries weight. Authentication is the gate through which every user, every service account, every administrator must pass to interact with corporate systems. Privilege escalation is the mechanism by which an ordinary user account can be turned into an administrative one. Lateral movement is the process of using credentials obtained in one part of the network to reach other parts. Security-policy enforcement is what determines which users can do which things — and, critically, which security controls actually apply to which accounts. An attacker who has compromised the identity system controls all four. They can authenticate as anyone. They can elevate any account they choose. They can move anywhere the identity system reaches. They can rewrite the policies that would otherwise contain them.

What this means in practice is that an identity-system compromise turns a contained event into an enterprise-wide one. The administrator who watched his backups disappear was already, by then, the victim of an identity compromise he had not yet discovered. The intruder had obtained domain-administrator credentials weeks earlier, probably through a session-cookie purchase of the kind described in the access-economy chapter, and had used those credentials to move freely across the environment. The reason the intruder could reach the backup console in the first place was the identity compromise. The reason the encryption, when it came, would touch every virtual machine in the production environment was the identity compromise. The reason the domain administrators would, by 6 a.m., find their own permissions stripped from their own accounts was that the intruder, having spent three weeks inside the identity system, had decided that the cleanest way to slow the response was to revoke the accounts the response team would otherwise have used to fight back.

Identity is the force multiplier. ENISA puts the same idea in institutional language: a limited intrusion, once it reaches the identity layer, becomes an enterprise-wide business-continuity event. The attacker can disable the applications the response team would otherwise use, including the incident-response tooling itself. The attacker can lock the administrators out of their own consoles. The attacker can revoke the recovery paths the organization had assumed would be available. By the time the company's chief information security officer reaches her laptop in the morning, the question is no longer whether the attack can be contained. The question is whether the company can authenticate anyone — including its own response team — well enough to begin containment at all.

The third target is the hypervisor. This is the one that converts the intrusion's leverage into operational catastrophe. ESXi is VMware's hypervisor platform — the software that runs underneath dozens or hundreds of virtual machines on a single physical server. In most enterprise environments, ESXi clusters run the production workloads: the databases, the application servers, the file shares, the entire fabric of the company's day-to-day computing. A single ESXi host can run a hundred virtual machines. A cluster of hosts can run thousands. The hypervisor is, structurally, a single locked utility closet that powers the lights, the heating, and the elevators for an entire skyscraper. If the closet is compromised, every floor of the building is dark at once.

Ransomware operators have, across 2025, learned to attack ESXi directly. The reporting documents live exploitation of VMware ESXi zero-day vulnerabilities continuing into January 2026 — vulnerabilities for which the vendor's patches were either not yet available or had not been applied in the affected environments at the time of compromise. Attackers who reach the hypervisor can encrypt all virtual machines on a host with a single payload, often within minutes. They can corrupt the snapshot infrastructure VMware itself uses for recovery. They can render the entire virtualization estate of a victim unrecoverable in a window so short that no defensive response is possible. The encryption of an ESXi cluster is, for most victims, the operational moment at which the question of whether to pay becomes the only remaining question.

The Active Directory compromise, the backup destruction, and the ESXi encryption work in concert. The identity compromise enables the backup destruction. The backup destruction removes the alternative to paying. The ESXi encryption maximizes the cost of refusing to pay. Each phase reinforces the others. Operators have learned to sequence the phases with the discipline of a project plan. The encryption-only ransomware of 2018 is a different threat than the resilience-denial campaign of 2026, in the same way a fire is a different threat from an arson investigation that has also disabled the building's sprinklers, the fire-department's communications, and the building plans the firefighters would otherwise have used to find the people trapped inside.

The same shift is visible in how operators are reaching the targets in the first place. The traditional ransomware narrative began with a phishing email. The 2026 narrative begins, increasingly, at the network's edge. ENISA's reporting cites Trend Micro's 2026 analysis of vulnerability-exploitation breaches: edge-device exploitation rose, in a single year, from three percent to twenty-two percent of the total. A more than sevenfold increase. The shift is structural rather than tactical. Perimeter appliances — VPN concentrators, firewalls, remote-access gateways, edge load-balancers — combine several properties that make them economically attractive targets. They sit on the internet, where they can be discovered and exploited at scale. They hold high trust within the corporate network. They often have weak telemetry, meaning compromises go undetected longer. They are patched more slowly than endpoints because their maintenance windows are coordinated with operations rather than security teams. And, most consequentially, they sit in front of the authentication flows the rest of the corporate environment depends on. An attacker who has compromised the VPN appliance has, in effect, become the gatekeeper to whatever sits behind it.

The worked examples are documented in the reporting. Clop, the long-running data-extortion brand, ran mass-exploitation campaigns against Oracle E-Business Suite vulnerabilities, turning a single class of enterprise-software flaws into a portfolio of victims. Akira, the ransomware brand named in the earlier market-structure chapters, continued to monetize SonicWall vulnerabilities long after the vendor's patches had been released, exploiting the gap between patch availability and patch application in customer environments. Each of these patterns reflects the same logic. The operators are not building novel attack capabilities for each victim. They are aggregating attack capability against a vendor's product, then sweeping the customer base of that vendor for whichever organizations have not yet patched. The vulnerability is a tool of mass production. The customer base is the inventory. The patch-application gap is the operator's margin.

The visibility-denial layer sits underneath all of this. Once an operator has reached the inside of a victim's environment, the next priority is to disable the defenders' ability to see what is happening. The technique that has become standard in 2025–2026 ransomware operations is the bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver attack, abbreviated by the threat-intelligence community as BYOVD. The technique works by loading a legitimately signed but flawed kernel-level driver into the operating system. The driver, because it is signed by a legitimate vendor, is accepted by the operating system's security controls as trusted. The driver, because it is flawed, allows the attacker to disable the security software that would otherwise be watching what they do. Endpoint detection and response platforms, the industry's primary investment in defending the endpoint, can be neutralized by an attacker who has loaded the right vulnerable driver into the kernel before deploying their payload.

The reporting documents a specific 2025 case. A flaw in a Baidu Antivirus driver was used in the deployment of DeadLock ransomware to bypass endpoint detection and response before the encryption began. The driver itself was legitimately developed and signed by a Chinese antivirus vendor; the flaw was a privilege-escalation bug the vendor had not yet patched. The ransomware operator did not need to develop a novel kernel exploit. The operator needed only to acquire the signed driver, load it into the victim's environment, exploit the bug to gain kernel-level access, disable the endpoint-detection product, and then deploy the payload through an unmonitored channel. The technique is repeatable across hundreds of vulnerable drivers in the wild. The defender's investments in endpoint detection are circumvented not by attacker innovation but by attacker exploitation of legitimate, signed code with known flaws.

Privilege escalation outside the driver context is the parallel technique. In November 2025, Microsoft's monthly patch cycle included a Windows kernel privilege-escalation vulnerability that, per the reporting, was actively exploited in ransomware intrusions. An attacker who had established initial access through one of the other vectors — a phished credential, an edge-device exploit, a purchased session cookie — could use the kernel vulnerability to elevate to SYSTEM-level access on the compromised host. SYSTEM is the operating system's highest privilege level, the account under which the operating system itself runs. An attacker with SYSTEM access can read any file, modify any setting, inject code into any running process, and disable any user-mode security control. The Windows patch was available within the normal cycle. The window between patch release and patch deployment in customer environments was, as it always is, the operator's working space. The exploitation happened inside that window.

The trust surface extends beyond the victim's own environment. ENISA's reporting frames managed service providers, enterprise-software vendors, and third-party access partners as downstream ransomware multipliers. The framing is precise. An operator who compromises a single MSP has, in a single intrusion, established the capability to reach every client the MSP serves. The MSP's privileged access into client environments — the kind of access an MSP must have, by the nature of its service — becomes the attacker's privileged access into those same environments. The same applies to enterprise software vendors whose products are deployed inside customer networks with administrative privileges. The same applies to third-party contractors whose remote-access configurations into customer environments are persistent, often inadequately monitored, and rarely revoked. The ICT supply chain is, in the language of the threat-intelligence community, a force-multiplication structure for ransomware operators. The operator who can compromise the right vendor reaches not one victim but a portfolio.

There is a convergence pattern in the techniques themselves that is worth naming, because it changes how defenders should be thinking about the threat. The techniques being used in 2025–2026 ransomware operations — edge-device zero-day exploitation, stealthy persistence inside identity systems, BYOVD-style endpoint-detection evasion, hypervisor compromise — were, a decade ago, the signature of state-sponsored espionage operations. They were techniques that required nation-state-grade tradecraft, the patience to maintain access over months, and the willingness to invest in capabilities that would not yield short-term payoffs. The criminal market has, with the same logic of platformization and franchise economics that produced the front-end of the ransomware industry, learned to industrialize them. The dwell times have lengthened. The persistence techniques have hardened. The willingness to invest weeks of intrusion work before pulling the trigger has become standard. The criminal-tradecraft convergence with state-sponsored techniques is not a metaphor. It is a measurable shift in the techniques being deployed in the threat-intelligence telemetry. A 2026 ransomware intrusion, in the technical sophistication of its earlier phases, increasingly resembles a 2015 espionage operation. The operators are no longer noisy. The operators are patient.

The backup administrator who opened his laptop at three in the morning would not, in the days that followed, ever fully reconstruct what had happened in his environment. The company would hire incident-response consultants. The consultants would identify the original compromise — a phished credential from a contractor who had clicked through a fake login page eleven weeks earlier. The consultants would trace the lateral movement, the identity-system compromise, the patient backup deletion, the ESXi staging that had positioned the encryption payload, the BYOVD-style endpoint-detection disablement, the use of a privilege-escalation flaw the company had not yet patched. The narrative the consultants would produce would be a six-page timeline. The timeline would, when shown to the company's board, prompt a long silence. The board would, after the silence, ask the obvious question. What had failed? The honest answer was that nothing single had failed. The defenses had been built for a threat model in which the attack began at the perimeter and worked inward. The attack had begun at the perimeter and then turned, with cold deliberation, against the systems on which the company's ability to fight back depended. The defenses had been calibrated against the wrong adversary. The wrong adversary was the only one that had existed for several years. The company, like most of the victims on the leak site that month, had been protecting against a class of attack that had been replaced.

The shift this chapter describes is the operational reason every other shift in the book is possible. The leverage on the negotiation table works because the alternative to paying has been removed. The platform economy that pays affiliates for victim conversion can produce conversions only because the affiliates have learned to remove their victims' alternatives. The franchise model works because the affiliates have a repeatable playbook for removing recovery options. The map of coercion works because the map is drawn in places where, once the recovery infrastructure is destroyed, the cost of refusing to pay becomes intolerable. The operators are not, as the public conversation still sometimes implies, simply encrypting files for ransom. They are running multi-week operations whose central purpose is to render their victims unable to recover. The encryption is the bill that gets presented at the end.

What sits underneath this transformation is a labor problem the operators have also begun to solve. The kind of patient, weeks-long intrusion the backup administrator's company experienced is expensive in human terms. It requires affiliates with the skills to compromise identity systems without alerting them, the patience to age out backups gradually, the discipline to time the encryption only after the recovery path has been removed. Those skills are not common. The traditional ransomware affiliate was a smash-and-grab specialist who could encrypt files and demand payment. The 2026 ransomware affiliate has had to become something closer to a project manager running a coordinated intrusion. The skills are scarcer. The patience is rarer. The operators that can produce victims like the backup administrator's company are the ones whose affiliate workforce has been trained — or replaced — to support the new model.

The next chapter is about how the operators have, with increasing reliability, begun to replace the expensive human labor of these intrusions with software. The middle of the kill chain — the reconnaissance, the lateral movement, the privilege escalation, the decision-making that the patient intrusion requires — is being absorbed, piece by piece, by agentic artificial intelligence. The technology is not yet autonomous. It is, by every credible measurement, becoming so. The implication for defenders, for organizations, for the speed at which the recovery-trap playbook can be executed, is the next part of the story. The operators have learned where to point. They have learned what to break. They are now learning to do it faster than any defender can react.

# Chapter 6: The Agent in the Loop

The affiliate's workstation was running three browser windows, each connected to a different target environment. He was, by his own count, in the middle of seven concurrent intrusions. A year earlier, when he had been working alone on individual victims, the same volume of work would have required a team of four. Now he was doing it himself, with the help of an interface that, to anyone looking over his shoulder, would have looked like a chat window with a model that was answering his questions.

The chat window was open in the corner of his screen. He had typed a question into it ten minutes earlier: which of the seven targets has the largest concentration of design files for products under active development, based on the file-listing output from yesterday's reconnaissance. The model had responded with a ranked list of the targets, the reasoning behind each rank, the directories it would recommend prioritizing for exfiltration in each environment, and a brief note on which of the seven companies' product-development cycles were most likely to produce regulatory disclosure obligations if the data appeared on a leak site. The note included a footnote suggesting that one of the targets, a mid-sized aerospace subcontractor, had publicly traded ownership and that the timing of a planned earnings call later that month might increase the company's willingness to pay quickly to avoid an unscheduled disclosure. The affiliate had not asked for the regulatory analysis. The model had provided it anyway, because the model had been trained on enough public corporate filings to understand that disclosure timing was a relevant variable. The model was being helpful.

The affiliate read the analysis, made a small mental adjustment to his target prioritization for the day, and typed his next question. He wanted to know which of the targets had domain controllers that had not yet patched a vulnerability his payload-development team had been refining over the past week. The model began the analysis. The affiliate, while he waited, switched to one of the other browser windows and resumed the lateral movement he had been running in a different victim's environment ten minutes earlier.

This is what operator-time compression looks like, in 2026, on the inside of a ransomware operation. It is not a science-fiction picture of autonomous attack agents. It is a picture of human attackers whose middle-of-the-kill-chain labor — the reconnaissance, the prioritization, the exploit selection, the analysis of stolen data, the drafting of negotiation messages, the decisions about which victims to escalate first — is increasingly being absorbed by software that does not get tired, that does not lose focus across seven concurrent intrusions, and that, increasingly, is being trained on the exact kinds of corporate data the operators steal.

This chapter is about that absorption, what it is and is not, and what it means for the speed at which the rest of the book's machinery now moves.

The first thing that has to be said about artificial intelligence in ransomware operations is that the reporting community has been disciplined about not overclaiming, and any honest account has to be similarly disciplined. The available evidence in 2025 and early 2026, as the threat-intelligence reports document it, is consistent. Large language models are being used to help with phishing, with translation across language barriers, with the writing of malware components, with the summarization of reconnaissance output, with the scripting of post-intrusion activity, and with the personalization of social-engineering pretexts. The use is augmentation. The model helps the human do their work faster. The human is still in the loop. The forecasts for 2026 and beyond, from vendors and from analytical bodies, extend this picture into agentic territory — multi-agent systems chaining reconnaissance, vulnerability discovery, credential attacks, payload adaptation, lateral movement support, and exfiltration — but the agentic frontier is a forecast, not a confirmed current state. The reports are explicit about this. The honest story is that AI is, today, the connective tissue of ransomware operations, not the operator. The frontier is moving. The frontier has not yet arrived.

That calibration matters because it is easy, in the present cultural moment around artificial intelligence, to lurch into either of two errors. One error is to insist that ransomware operators are already running autonomous attack agents that do everything from reconnaissance to encryption without human input, which is not what the evidence supports. The other error is to dismiss the AI thread entirely on the grounds that the current evidence is mostly about phishing and content generation, which underestimates what is happening. The right reading is in the middle. AI is absorbing labor in the middle of the kill chain. The labor it is absorbing is the labor that has been the most expensive constraint on the operators' ability to scale. The patient identity-system compromise. The reconnaissance of victim environments. The triage of stolen data. The personalization of extortion communications. All of these are tasks where a model that has been trained on enough operational examples can produce a draft that a human operator can refine in minutes rather than hours. The cumulative effect of this absorption — across seven concurrent intrusions, or seventy, or seven hundred — is the operator-time compression the threat-intelligence reports keep returning to.

To understand why the operators are willing to invest in this kind of tooling, you have to look at the revenue side of the business. The U.S. Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, FinCEN, published a multi-year accounting of ransomware payments by U.S. victims that puts the figure at approximately two point one billion dollars across the years 2022, 2023, and 2024. Three years of payments, two billion dollars, U.S. victims only. Global ransomware revenue, by every credible estimate, is several multiples of that. The reporting also notes that payments declined from a 2023 peak, with payment rates falling as some victims have refused to pay, but that the revenue pool remains large enough to sustain substantial investment in operational tooling. Two billion dollars in three years is the engine. The engine funds the automation arms race. It funds the development of better payloads, better leak-site infrastructure, better negotiation platforms, and — most relevantly to this chapter — the integration of language-model tooling into the daily workflow of affiliate operators. When two billion dollars of revenue accumulates in a sector, the sector invests in the tools that scale the revenue. The criminal sector is not different. The investments are happening.

The architectural shift that AI is enabling on the payload side is worth pausing on, because it changes what a ransomware attack actually consists of. For most of the history of ransomware, the payload was conceptually simple. It was a piece of software that encrypted files. The criminal innovation was in distribution, in payment systems, in negotiation tactics. The encryption itself was the constant. In 2025 and 2026, the payload has stopped being a single thing. It has become modular. The encryption is one option. The exfiltration of data is another. The targeted disruption of specific business systems — the financial close, the customer-service workflow, the production-floor controllers — is another. The deliberate corruption of identity systems is another. Each of these capabilities exists as a component that the operator can deploy independently or in combination, depending on what the leverage analysis suggests will most reliably produce payment. The payload is no longer a thing. The payload is a kit.

AI is the connective tissue that lets the kit be assembled at speed. Modular payload architecture is operationally valuable only if the operator can decide, in something close to real time, which modules to deploy against which victim and in what sequence. Making those decisions requires reading the victim's environment quickly, understanding the relative value of different data categories, prioritizing the systems whose disruption will produce the most pressure, and adapting the deployment to the defensive posture the victim has in place. Each of those steps, done by hand, would require hours of expert human analysis. Done with the assistance of a model that has been trained on thousands of similar environments and has internalized the patterns of leverage that produce payment, the steps take minutes. The model does not replace the operator's judgment. The model takes a first cut. The operator reviews, adjusts, and proceeds. The cumulative effect, across the lifecycle of an intrusion, is that the human time required to run an attack falls by a factor that the operators have begun to measure carefully and that the threat-intelligence community is still trying to estimate from the outside.

The vendor forecasts for 2026 and beyond are worth reading in this light. Trend Micro's 2026 outlook describes ransomware operators as moving toward faster and more persistent automated ransomware, with the persistence and scaling effects of AI integration as the dominant trend. The framing is careful. Trend Micro is not predicting that ransomware will become fully autonomous; it is predicting that the integration of AI into ransomware operations will produce attacks that are faster, more persistent, and harder to disrupt. ITBrief's 2026 outlook is more aggressive in its language, forecasting fully automated cybercrime by 2026, but the underlying analysis is consistent: the trajectory of AI integration is toward operations in which the human is supervising rather than executing. Zscaler ThreatLabz's threat-prediction work for 2026, summarized in the same reporting, forecasts AI-managed victim identification, automated exploit selection, and AI-assisted negotiation — the last of which appears in the same forecast that anticipates voice-based social engineering as a major attack vector.

Each of these forecasts is a forecast. The reports are explicit that universal autonomous attack capability is not yet observed in the wild. The threat-intelligence community has been measured in its language for good reason. The history of AI hype in cybersecurity has, for most of the past decade, been a history of overclaiming on the defender's side and overclaiming on the attacker's side in roughly equal measure. The current reporting, conscious of this history, refuses to predict that 2026 will be the year of autonomous attack agents. It predicts, more modestly, that 2026 will be the year in which AI augmentation becomes structural enough to materially compress the operators' kill-chain timelines and to extend their effective reach. The honest framing is that the augmentation is the story. The autonomy is the trajectory the augmentation is on. The defenders have to plan for the augmentation now. They have to start preparing, structurally, for what the trajectory implies in five years.

The augmentation story has a specific operational shape that is worth describing in detail, because it explains why defenders are losing the time-compression race rather than winning it. The middle of the kill chain — the period between an attacker's initial foothold and the moment of payload deployment — has been, historically, the defender's best window. The traditional defensive playbook assumed that an intruder would need days to weeks of dwell time to reconnoiter the environment, identify high-value targets, escalate privileges, move laterally, and stage their attack. That dwell time was the window in which the defender's incident-response team could detect anomalies, investigate, and evict the intruder before the damage was done. The entire architecture of modern endpoint-detection-and-response platforms, of security-information-and-event-management systems, of threat-hunting programs, was built around the assumption that dwell time existed and could be shortened from the defender's side. The assumption was load-bearing.

What AI integration is doing to that assumption is removing it. The reconnaissance phase that used to take an experienced human attacker a week can now, with model assistance, be completed in hours. The privilege-escalation analysis that used to require a specialist's manual exploration of the environment can be partially automated. The lateral-movement planning that used to involve reading network diagrams and identifying high-value paths can be drafted by a model and refined by an operator. The data-triage phase — the identification of which exfiltrated files will produce the most negotiation pressure when threatened with publication — has historically been a labor bottleneck. Operators stole more data than they could read. The leverage they could apply was limited by their ability to understand what they had taken. Language models, trained to summarize and classify documents at scale, have begun to absorb this bottleneck. An operator can now process terabytes of stolen data, identify the most sensitive files, draft a negotiation message that references specific embarrassing or regulatorily-exposing content, and stage the leak-site teaser in a window that, two years ago, would have required a team and a week. With a model, it requires the operator and an afternoon.

Each compression in the middle of the kill chain is a corresponding compression in the defender's window. The defenders are not getting slower. The defenders are, by every metric the security industry tracks, getting better at the detection and response work they invested in across the past decade. They are simply running against a clock that is accelerating faster than their improvements. Pre-ransomware detection — the moment when defenders identify attacker activity before the payload is deployed and have a chance to evict the intruder — has, across the last several years of incident-response reporting, been shrinking as a share of engagements. That drop is, in part, the visible footprint of operator-time compression. The attackers are completing the kill chain before the defenders can interrupt it, in increasing numbers, because the middle of the kill chain has been partially absorbed by software that does not require human time at the scale the defenders had planned around.

The labor implication on the attacker side is the part most observers miss. The traditional ransomware affiliate, in the previous economy, was a specialist who could run one intrusion at a time, end to end, over a span of weeks. The number of affiliates in the market was constrained by the supply of people with the skill set and the patience to do this work. Operator-time compression changes the supply equation. With AI assistance absorbing the middle of the kill chain, a single affiliate can run multiple intrusions concurrently. The skill required to run any individual intrusion drops, because the model is doing the analytical work the affiliate previously had to do. The population of plausible affiliates expands. The criminal labor market acquires new entrants who, in the previous economy, would not have had the technical skill to participate. The franchise platforms, eager to grow throughput, lower their effective skill barriers and increase their affiliate intake. The market grows.

This is, in many ways, the same scaling logic that has driven every previous wave of platformization in the criminal market. The franchise model lowered the skill floor by absorbing technical operations into the platform's back end. The access economy lowered the skill floor by separating intrusion from access acquisition. AI integration is lowering the skill floor by absorbing analytical labor. Each compression of the criminal value chain produces a larger population of participants and a higher rate of attempted intrusions. The downstream consequence — the consequence that the public conversation about cybercrime tends to dismiss because it does not have a single dramatic story — is that the volume of ransomware activity rises across the same period that the per-intrusion human time required falls. The two trends are not in tension. They are the same trend, seen from different angles.

There is a defensive irony in all of this that has not yet been fully digested by the security industry, and that deserves to be stated directly. The same artificial-intelligence tooling that is augmenting attackers is also, in many security products, augmenting defenders. AI-assisted threat detection, AI-assisted log analysis, AI-assisted incident-response orchestration — each of these has become a standard category of security investment over the past several years. The vendor pitch is that AI will help the defenders close the time-compression gap. The pitch is not wrong. The defenders do get faster. The problem is that the gap is widening rather than closing, because the attackers are augmenting against the same set of operational primitives and the asymmetry is structural. The attacker needs to succeed against one victim, once, in any given operation. The defender needs to defend everywhere, always. AI augmentation amplifies whichever side has the simpler problem. The attacker's problem — finding one weakness, exploiting it, removing the alternative to paying — is the simpler problem. The defender's problem — maintaining the integrity of every system across every environment against every possible attack — is the harder one. The same tooling that gives both sides leverage gives more leverage to whichever side has fewer constraints. The constraints are on the defender. The asymmetry widens.

The data-leverage implication of all of this is worth pausing on, because it sits at the center of where the rest of the book is going. The shift from encryption-centric ransomware to data-extortion-centric ransomware is, in part, an AI story. Encryption has always been a relatively automated phase of an attack. Data exfiltration and the construction of negotiation leverage from stolen data have, historically, been the labor-intensive phases. The volume of data an operator can effectively use as leverage is, in any operation, limited by the operator's ability to identify which files matter most and to package them into the kind of public-pressure artifacts that compel payment. The platforms have been investing in automated data-triage services for several years. Language models accelerate those services dramatically. The operator can run an exfiltration, point a model at the stolen files, and receive within hours a structured analysis identifying the documents most likely to embarrass the victim, the regulatory-disclosure obligations the data might trigger, the names of customers or counterparties whose exposure would compound the leverage, and a draft negotiation message customized to the victim's industry and circumstance. The work that used to require a human team of analysts now requires a single operator and a chat window.

This is, finally, the part that is hardest to translate into the everyday-life impact the reader can feel. Most ransomware stories describe consequences that an ordinary person can see — a hospital that cannot administer chemotherapy, a manufacturer whose plant floor goes dark, a backup administrator whose snapshots are gone. The AI thread describes a structural shift in the operations that produce those consequences. The shift is invisible from the outside. An ordinary person does not see the affiliate running seven concurrent intrusions, or the language model drafting the data-triage analysis, or the chat window where the day's target prioritization gets generated. What the ordinary person sees is the result. The result is that the things that happened a quarter ago are happening more often this quarter, and the dwell time defenders had to catch them has shrunk. The ordinary person sees more leak-site postings, more news cycles about ransomware incidents, more conversations in their professional circles about which of their employers' vendors got hit this month. The AI thread is the part of the story that explains why the curve keeps bending upward, even as the defenders invest more aggressively in the tools they were told would solve the problem.

The affiliate from the opening scene of this chapter would, by the end of his Tuesday, have advanced four of his seven concurrent intrusions to the data-exfiltration phase, staged the encryption payloads on the two highest-priority targets, drafted three negotiation messages with the model's assistance and refined them in his own voice, and processed roughly forty terabytes of exfiltrated data through the triage workflow that produced the rank-ordered lists of high-pressure documents he would use in the negotiation phase. He would have done this work alone, with the help of an interface that was, by the standards of any frontier-AI research lab, not particularly advanced. The model was not running the operation. The model was helping the operator run it. The human was still in the loop. The human had, simply, become an order of magnitude more productive than the same human would have been two years earlier.

What this kind of productivity means is not yet a settled question on the defender's side. The simple version is that more victims will be reached per quarter, by fewer operators, with shorter dwell times and tighter kill chains. The harder version is that the defensive disciplines that worked when the asymmetry was smaller — detection, response, restoration — have to be supplemented by disciplines that work when the asymmetry is structural. Identity hygiene at a level no major enterprise has yet achieved. Recovery infrastructure isolated and validated at a frequency no operations team has yet sustained. Edge-device patching at a cadence no vendor's product has yet enabled. Each of these is, in the language of the threat-intelligence community, the kind of investment that has to be made before the AI thread fully matures, because the window in which the investments can be made without operating under attack is closing.

The next chapter takes the AI thread into its most uncanny operational manifestation: the use of language-model and voice-cloning tooling in social engineering against the human beings inside target organizations. The middle of the kill chain has been compressed. The front of the kill chain — the part where the attacker first reaches into the target's environment by deceiving a human being into letting them in — is being compressed too, and the deception is being personalized to a degree that the previous generation of phishing training was not built to defend against. The voice on the phone, the email that sounds like the CFO, the chat message from a colleague who is not actually the colleague — these are the next chapter's subjects. The model that helped the affiliate triage his stolen data on Tuesday afternoon is, on Tuesday evening, helping a different affiliate write the script for the call that will compromise the next victim. The agent in the loop is, with increasing frequency, the agent that opens the door.

# Chapter 7: The Voice on the Phone

The help-desk technician's phone rang at nine-fourteen on a Tuesday morning. She had been at her desk for forty-six minutes. She had already handled eleven tickets, most of them routine: a contractor whose VPN client had stopped authenticating after a laptop update, a finance analyst whose single-sign-on token had expired in the middle of a regulatory filing, two new hires waiting for their corporate phones to enroll into the mobile-device-management platform. The eleventh ticket had taken her nineteen minutes to resolve. The clock on her terminal showed she was four minutes behind her queue's average response time for the morning. She picked up the phone on the second ring.

The voice on the other end was familiar. It belonged to a man she had spoken with twice before. The first time, six months earlier, he had needed an emergency password reset after a transatlantic flight had landed two hours late and his laptop had not been able to reach the company's network from the airline lounge. The second time, three months after that, he had needed help enrolling a new mobile device into the authentication app because his previous phone had cracked during a child's soccer game. He had been pleasant both times, the kind of senior executive who knew the help desk staff's first names and remembered to thank them. He was, she knew, one of the company's executive vice presidents — the head of corporate development, the person whose name appeared on the merger announcements the firm published every quarter or two. The voice on the phone was, by every cue her brain processed in the first two seconds of the call, his voice.

He was apologetic. He had landed in Frankfurt the previous evening and his hotel's Wi-Fi had not been able to authenticate him into the corporate environment overnight. The authentication app on his phone, the one she had helped him enroll three months earlier, had stopped responding to the push notifications the company's identity system was sending it. He had tried to reach his executive assistant, but it was four in the morning her time and she was not picking up. He had a Frankfurt board call in forty-five minutes for which he needed to access the deal-room repository where the slides had been staged. He needed her to reset his multi-factor authentication so he could enroll a new device. He read out his employee ID number, his last four social-security digits, and the answer to the security question the help-desk procedure required for an over-the-phone identity verification. The answers were correct. The voice was correct. The story was the kind of story she heard from traveling executives twice a week.

She processed the reset. She walked him through the enrollment of the new authenticator. She wished him good luck with the Frankfurt call. The call ended at nine-twenty-one. The clock on her terminal showed she had recovered two of the four minutes she had been behind. She moved to the twelfth ticket of the morning. By the time the firm's security operations team noticed, eleven days later, that the executive vice president's account had been used to access the deal-room repository from a virtual private server in Eastern Europe between the hours of two and four in the morning local time on the same Tuesday and the three following Tuesdays, the data of the firm's two largest pending acquisitions had been on a leak-site preview page for nine days. The voice on the phone had not been the executive vice president. The voice on the phone had been a synthetic reconstruction of his voice, trained on the public speaking footage from three industry conferences he had keynoted in the previous two years, calibrated against the recording of an earnings call he had participated in the previous quarter, and routed through a softphone connection that masked the call's origin. The executive vice president had been asleep in Connecticut. The help-desk technician had let in the people who would take her company's pending mergers public.

This is what social engineering looks like, in 2026, on the inside of a ransomware operation. It is not a phishing email with grammatical errors and a suspicious link. It is a phone call, in a familiar voice, with the correct verification answers, at a plausible hour, with a plausible story, lasting seven minutes. The voice on the phone is the front door of the access economy. The technician on the other end is the person the access economy has learned to talk past.

The pipeline that produced the voice on the phone is the part of the story that has changed most consequentially in the past two years. The AI thread that has been absorbing the middle of the kill chain — the reconnaissance, the lateral movement, the data triage — has, on the front of the kill chain, been narrowing onto the surface where it is most personal: identity and trust. The same models that help an affiliate triage stolen data and prioritize concurrent intrusions are also being used to build hyper-personalized lures, clone the voices of executives, draft pretext stories that read like internal company correspondence, and conduct, in increasing numbers, the entire phase of the attack in which a target is convinced to open a door they would not have opened a year earlier. The skill barrier for convincing impersonation has fallen. The economics of personalization have changed. The thing the security industry has spent two decades teaching employees to recognize — the suspicious request, the unusual sender, the misspelled domain — has become harder to recognize because the suspicious cues have, with the help of language models and voice synthesis, been engineered out.

The persona-simulation pipeline starts with reconnaissance, and the reconnaissance is no longer expensive. The raw materials are public. An executive's keynote talks are on conference websites and in industry-publication archives. The same executive's earnings-call transcripts are available through investor-relations portals. Their interview clips circulate on professional networking sites. Their writing — the open letters they have published, the LinkedIn posts they have written, the email signatures that appear in correspondence forwarded outside the firm — has accumulated, over the years, into a corpus a model can train against. Their direct reports' names are in the company's press releases. The names of their executive assistants are in conference attendee lists. The relationships that structure their decisions — the vendors, the law firm, the auditors, the lead investors — are reconstructable from a few hours of open-source research. None of this requires intrusion. None of this requires criminal infrastructure. None of this requires anything more sophisticated than a language model patient enough to read everything an executive has ever said in public and a voice model trained on a few minutes of clean speech.

What the operators have built on top of these raw materials is a synthesis layer that did not exist in usable form three years ago. A language model trained on an executive's public communications can produce text that reads like them. A voice model trained on a few minutes of clean speech can produce phone-quality audio that resembles them, including their cadence, their accent, their characteristic verbal tics. The two outputs, combined, produce a pretext that arrives in the medium of the target's choice. An email that sounds like the chief financial officer asking for an urgent wire transfer, written in the chief financial officer's actual writing style, referencing a vendor relationship the operators know exists, sent at the time of day the chief financial officer typically sends end-of-quarter communications. A phone call that sounds like the corporate-development vice president asking the help desk for a multi-factor authentication reset, told as a story the help desk has heard from the same executive twice before. A chat message that arrives in the company's internal communications platform, from an account that has been compromised through the same access-economy supply chain, in the voice of a colleague whose tone the model has internalized from the previous six months of messages in the same channel.

The reporting documents this synthesis pipeline with care. Hyper-personalization, the threat-intelligence community now writes, is the fusion of three data sources: public information, stolen credentials or messages, and victim-specific internal data. The public information is the conferences, the filings, the social-media history. The stolen credentials and messages come from earlier intrusions in the same supply chain — infostealer logs that contain not just passwords but the contents of compromised mailboxes, the cached internal documents from collaboration platforms, the partial chat histories from messaging applications a user has logged into through a compromised browser. The victim-specific internal data comes from previous staging in the same target — the early reconnaissance an affiliate or a partnered initial-access broker has done in the environment before the social-engineering call is made. Layered together, the three sources produce a pretext that is not generic. It is a pretext built specifically for one target, on one Tuesday morning, against one help-desk technician, with the verification answers correct and the story exactly the kind of story the technician has heard from this caller before.

The voice and video layer is the part of the pipeline that has shifted public perception fastest, because it has produced the kind of demonstrable, audible artifact that no one needs technical training to understand. The threat-intelligence reports describe voice cloning, vishing, and deepfake-enabled help-desk fraud as a convergence trend that the 2026 forecasts treat as a structural enabler of ransomware. Zscaler ThreatLabz, whose 2026 threat-prediction work has identified voice-based social engineering as one of the major attack vectors of the year, sits alongside Trend Micro and ITBrief in framing the synthesis pipeline as a structural rather than tactical shift. The reporting is consistent about the operational shape. The attacker harvests public audio. The attacker trains a voice model. The attacker places the call, in the cloned voice, against the part of the target's environment where the identity controls have a human-in-the-loop fallback — the help desk, the account-recovery line, the IT support function, the vendor-management team. The human-in-the-loop fallback is, by design, the place where the identity system permits exceptions. It is the place where, when the system says no, a person can say yes. The operators have learned where that person sits, what verification questions they ask, and what stories make the person comfortable saying yes.

The deepfake-enabled help-desk fraud is the operational case the reporting keeps returning to, because the help desk is the load-bearing failure point in most identity programs. An organization's authentication system can be engineered to be phishing-resistant, with hardware-token-based multi-factor authentication and continuous access evaluation and device-compliance checks. None of that engineering matters if a human being on the help desk can be persuaded, over the phone, to reset the authentication and enroll a new device for a caller they cannot see. The help desk exists, in every enterprise, because the engineered system needs an exception channel. The exception channel exists because real employees lose their phones, travel without their hardware tokens, get locked out at inconvenient hours. The exception channel is, structurally, the only path an attacker needs. The attacker who can produce a convincing voice, the right verification answers, and a plausible story has not bypassed the multi-factor authentication system. The attacker has used the multi-factor authentication system's own exception channel against it.

What sits behind the exception channel is the same access economy that the infostealer-to-broker-to-affiliate supply chain has, across the past three years, industrialized. The credentials, the session cookies, the single-sign-on tokens, the Entra ID identities, the verification answers that the help-desk procedure requires — none of these have to be reinvented for each victim. They exist already, in the underground markets that move infostealer output between brokers and affiliates. An attacker preparing a help-desk-fraud call against a specific executive will, in many cases, have already purchased a freshly harvested infostealer log from that executive's home computer, that executive's spouse's laptop, that executive's adult child's college device — somewhere in the household where the executive has, at some point, signed into a corporate system. The log contains not just the executive's credentials but the answers to the verification questions, the names of the executive's direct reports, the company's internal terminology, the executive's recent travel patterns inferred from the calendar entries the browser had cached. The attacker who places the help-desk call has, by that point, already read the executive's life. The voice on the phone is the application of that read.

The economic logic of this layer is, in the language of the threat-intelligence community, the convergence of two supply chains. The first supply chain is the access economy: infostealer harvesting, broker validation, the marketplaces where stolen sessions and credentials are routed through brokers to affiliates. The second supply chain is the personalization pipeline: public-data harvesting, voice-model training, persona synthesis, the integration of language-model output into the operational tooling that affiliates use day-to-day. Each supply chain, separately, has been documented in the reporting for years. What the 2025–2026 reporting describes is the merger of the two. The infostealer log that contained the executive's session cookies is now also the source of the corpus from which the executive's writing voice is modeled. The single-sign-on tokens that bypass the multi-factor system are now also being used to access the collaboration platforms whose contents will inform the help-desk-fraud script. The two pipelines feed each other. The same affiliates that buy validated access from initial-access brokers also subscribe to the personalization tooling that turns the access into a credible social-engineering script. The ransomware-as-a-service brand that the affiliate works for provides both. The integration is the product.

The bots are the layer that the public conversation has barely begun to absorb. The reporting describes them under several names — conversational extortion bots, AI-managed negotiation interfaces, automated victim-pressure platforms — but the underlying capability is consistent. A language model, integrated with the operator's negotiation infrastructure, can engage a victim directly, maintain consistent messaging across days or weeks, reference specific facts about the victim's stolen data, and apply psychological pressure with a discipline that no human negotiator could sustain. Trend Micro's 2026 outlook predicts that AI-driven extortion bots will engage victims directly in ransom negotiations. The forecast is calibrated. The technology is not yet universal. The technology is being demonstrated and refined. The trajectory is toward operations in which the negotiation phase, like the reconnaissance phase, is being absorbed by software that does not get tired, that does not lose focus across multiple concurrent victims, and that has been trained on enough operational data to know how to escalate.

The connection between the bots and the help-desk fraud is the part that closes the loop. The same operators who deploy a conversational extortion bot against a victim's executive team have, in many cases, also deployed the voice-cloning pipeline against that victim's help desk to gain the initial access in the first place. The technologies are not separate. The language model that drafted the pretext for the help-desk call on Tuesday morning is, on Tuesday afternoon, drafting the negotiation message the victim's general counsel will read on Tuesday evening. The voice model that synthesized the executive's call to the help desk is, in some operations, also being used to place follow-up calls to the victim's executive team during the negotiation phase — calls in which the cloned voice of the company's own chief executive can be made to threaten the publication of stolen data unless payment is made. The full lifecycle of the attack, from initial access through pressure campaign, runs on the same underlying synthesis pipeline. The model that opened the door is the model that holds the gun.

The defensive implication of all this is the part that the security industry is still working through, and it deserves to be stated directly because the public conversation about it has not yet caught up. For two decades, the defensive answer to social engineering has been training. Employees were taught to recognize phishing emails, to scrutinize sender addresses, to look for grammatical errors, to verify unusual requests through a second channel. The training was useful when the cues it taught people to look for were actually present in the attacks. The cues are no longer reliably present. The hyper-personalized email contains no grammatical errors. The voice on the phone is the correct voice. The pretext story is the kind of story the target has heard before. The verification answers are correct. The training programs that taught employees to spot the wrong cues are, against the current generation of attacks, training employees to spot cues that no longer exist. The category of defense the security industry called "user awareness" has, against AI-augmented social engineering, become a category error. The user cannot be trained to detect what is not detectable by humans.

What the reporting argues instead — and what every credible 2026 forecast supports — is that the defensive load has to shift to controls that do not depend on the human in the loop noticing something is wrong. Phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication that cannot be reset over the phone. Callback verification that runs through pre-registered channels and known devices rather than through whatever number the caller provides. Identity-proof workflows that require multi-party authorization for sensitive changes. Conditional access policies that refuse to grant high-privilege sessions from unfamiliar devices, networks, or geographies regardless of who the credentials belong to. Session-cookie binding to device-posture signals so that a stolen cookie cannot be replayed from a different machine. Continuous access evaluation that revokes sessions in real time when the risk signals shift. Each of these controls is, in the language of the threat-intelligence community, the kind of investment that closes off the human exception channel by making the exception machine-verifiable. The investments are expensive. The investments are not universally deployed. The reporting is explicit that the gap between the controls the leading organizations have implemented and the controls the median organization runs on is the operational space the attackers are working inside.

The connection back to the access economy is where this chapter has to land, because the social-engineering layer is not a separate phenomenon. It is the marketing channel for the access economy. The infostealer-to-broker-to-affiliate supply chain converts harvested credential output into ransomware-ready access. The hyper-personalization pipeline is the parallel path where affiliates and platforms produce their own access through industrialized social engineering rather than buying it from upstream brokers. The two paths are not in competition. They are complementary. An affiliate preparing a campaign against a target may buy a freshly validated session cookie from an initial-access broker and, in parallel, run a voice-cloning attack against the target's help desk to acquire a second, longer-lived access path. The cookie expires. The help-desk-reset persists. The operators use both because using both compresses the time between intrusion and payload deployment. The defensive surface the operators are working against is the surface where identity, credentials, and trust intersect. The intersection has, across the past two years, become the load-bearing layer of every enterprise's exposure to ransomware.

The personal angle is, finally, the part that connects all of this back to the reader. The voice on the phone is, for most readers, an abstraction. The help-desk technician is, for most readers, someone else. The executive whose voice was cloned, the employee whose answers to verification questions were already in an infostealer log, the colleague whose chat-message voice has been internalized by the model that is drafting the pretext for tomorrow's compromise — these are, on most days, other people's problems. They will not stay that way. The same infostealer pipelines that harvested the executive's credentials also harvest the credentials of every employee with browser-stored passwords, every contractor who has signed into a corporate system from a personal device, every household member of a corporate executive whose family network sits one click away from a fake-CAPTCHA prompt. The same language models that draft the pretext for the executive's help-desk-fraud call also draft the pretext for the call that will be placed to the reader's elderly parent next month, the chat message that will arrive in the reader's professional inbox the following Tuesday from a colleague who is not actually the colleague, the email that will appear in the reader's child's college account from an admissions office that does not actually exist. The infrastructure that runs the help-desk fraud is the same infrastructure that runs the consumer fraud. The economy of stolen attention has merged with the economy of stolen identity. The voice on the phone is, for everyone with a phone, the surface the rest of the threat ecosystem reaches through.

The help-desk technician who, on the Tuesday morning of this chapter's opening scene, processed the reset that let the operators into the deal room would, in the weeks that followed, undergo a careful interview with the firm's incident-response team. She would describe the call exactly as it had happened. The interviewers would not, at first, believe that the voice she had heard had been synthetic. They would replay the recorded line and compare it against the executive vice president's earnings-call audio. The waveforms would not match exactly, but they would match closely enough that the interviewers, hearing them played back through the call-center's standard audio compression, would not be able to distinguish them either. The firm would, in the months that followed, change its help-desk procedures. Reset requests for high-privilege accounts would be routed to a callback verification that ran through the executive's pre-registered mobile number rather than through whatever channel the caller used to initiate the request. Verification questions would be supplemented by device-posture checks that could not be answered with information harvested from an infostealer log. The procedural changes would protect the firm against the specific attack that had succeeded. They would not, the incident-response consultants would note in their final report, protect the firm against the next attack, which would be calibrated against whatever the new procedures had become. The synthesis pipeline does not stop adapting. The synthesis pipeline is the operator-time-compression dynamic applied to the identity layer specifically. The operators are absorbing the human-in-the-loop fallback the way they have absorbed the middle of the kill chain. The fallback is being engineered out from the attacker's side faster than it is being engineered out from the defender's.

The next chapter takes this layer's logic into the post-compromise phase: the negotiation desk where the same psychological machinery that placed the help-desk call is now applied to the victim's executives, lawyers, communications staff, and board. The voice on the phone, once the door has been opened, becomes the voice in the negotiation. The pressure campaign is, on the operator's side, a continuation of the same persona-simulation pipeline. The personalization that produced the pretext is the personalization that produces the leverage. The help-desk technician was the first audience. The chief financial officer, ten days later, is the next.

# Chapter 8: The Negotiation Desk

The finance director was alone in the conference room because everyone else had been asked to leave. The conference room was at the corporate headquarters of a regional industrial manufacturer with a thousand employees, six plants, a Tuesday-morning routine that had, until ninety-six hours earlier, been entirely unremarkable, and a chief executive officer who had, at the start of this meeting, told the room that he needed her to make a recommendation by the end of the business day. The recommendation concerned a chat window that was open on the laptop in front of her. The chat window had been open for three days. The countdown timer in the browser tab next to it showed forty-seven hours, eleven minutes, and a number of seconds that was decreasing in real time. The number at the top of the chat window was five million dollars. It had been seven million dollars on Saturday. The "support agent," who described himself in the chat thread as a customer-service representative for the operators' "resolution team," had reduced the figure twice already, citing the company's prompt engagement as the reason. He had attached, on the second day, a small archive file containing three of the company's customer contracts, a spreadsheet of vendor pricing terms, and the personal-identifying information of two hundred and four employees including the chief executive officer's home address. A larger archive, the chat thread mentioned, had been staged on a leak-site preview page. The page was not yet public. It would become public when the timer reached zero. There was, the support agent reminded her, a discount available if engagement reached "resolution phase" inside the next twenty-four hours. She had a chemistry-class textbook open on the corner of the desk, brought in by her teenage son the previous evening. The textbook's spine was broken. She was, at the moment the meeting had ended, looking at it because she could not, for the moment, look at the screen.

This is what the inside of a ransomware negotiation looks like, in 2026, on the victim's side of the chat window. The room is small. The stakes are total. The person at the desk is, in most cases, a finance officer or a general counsel or a chief information security officer who has spent their professional life inside structures that did not prepare them for the conversation they are now in. The person on the other side of the chat window is, by every operational measurement, running a sales pipeline. The pipeline has a discount schedule, an upsell catalog, a deadline-driven escalation system, a customer-service tone, and a brand that has been built across hundreds of previous victims. The negotiation is not, in the ordinary sense, a negotiation. It is a price-discovery process being conducted under the cold mechanics of a public-facing leak site, a countdown clock, and a body of evidence that the operators have already accumulated against the victim. The chat window is the visible surface of an industry. The industry is the place where every prior phase of the operation gets monetized.

This chapter opens the third part of the book. The first two parts have described how the industry was assembled — the platform economy, the labor market, the wholesalers' supply chain, the recovery-trap playbook, the AI integration that has compressed every prior phase. The third part is about where all of that work is converted into payment. The negotiation desk is where the platform meets the victim, where the affiliate's intrusion becomes the operator's revenue, where the careful staging of the prior weeks comes to bear on a single conversation in a single chat window with a single human being who has to decide, on behalf of the institution, whether to pay. The desk is the most procedural part of the operation. It is also, structurally, the most consequential. Everything that came before it has been preparation. The chat window is where the preparation gets resolved.

The scale of the chat window is the first thing to absorb. Across 2025, Check Point's count of ransomware-victim listings on double-extortion leak sites reached 7,960 — a fifty-three-percent year-over-year increase. The number is not a count of attacks; it is a count of organizations whose names have appeared on the operators' public pressure platforms. By the first quarter of 2026, attackers had posted another 2,122 organizations to leak sites, the second-highest first-quarter total on record. The top ten syndicates, across the same quarter, accounted for seventy-one percent of global victims. The market that produced these numbers is not fragmented in the way the public conversation about cybercrime sometimes suggests. It is reconsolidating. Late in 2025, the number of active leak sites fell from eighty-four to seventy-seven even as claimed victims rose by fifty percent quarter-over-quarter and forty percent year-over-year. The dominant operators were absorbing affiliates, infrastructure, and market share from weaker competitors. The reconsolidation produced a smaller number of more capable brands. The more capable brands produced more polished extortion pipelines. The pipelines produced the chat window the finance director was sitting in front of.

The texture of the conversation inside that chat window is, for the first time in the history of ransomware, statistically measurable. NordStellar's analysis of leaked ransomware negotiations spanning 2020 through 2026 reviewed two hundred and fifty-six separate negotiations comprising eleven thousand five hundred and ninety-nine individual messages — an average of forty-seven messages per negotiation. The negotiations were not short. They were not, in most cases, brief threats followed by simple compliance or refusal. They were prolonged transactional exchanges in which the operators applied a series of techniques drawn from sales training, customer-service workflow design, and behavioral economics, against victims who had no prior experience of being on the receiving end of any of them. The NordStellar analysis found that twenty-five-point-six percent of the negotiations ended in payment. Among the victims who paid, the median discount from the initial demand was fifty-seven percent. Some demands were reduced by as much as ninety-six-point-two percent. The numbers tell a story that the public conversation has not yet absorbed. The initial demand is not the price. The initial demand is the anchor. The negotiation is the price-discovery process that follows from the anchor. The operators know this. The operators have built their tooling around it.

The discount mechanics are the operational expression of the price-discovery framing. NordStellar's review of the leaked transcripts found operators consistently offering discount phases early in the exchange — reductions of twenty-five to seventy percent if the victim engaged inside a defined window, typically the first forty-eight to seventy-two hours. The early-engagement discount is not a concession. It is a sales technique calibrated to compress the victim's decision timeline. A finance officer who is told that the demand will drop by half if she responds within two days is not being given a discount; she is being given a deadline. The deadline is reinforced by the countdown timer on the leak-site preview page, the partial data samples that have already been published, and the chat window's "support agent" who is asking, several times a day, whether the victim's executive team has reached a decision. The framing is procedural. The pressure is psychological. The two are designed to converge on the moment, somewhere inside the early-engagement window, where the victim's institutional risk tolerance breaks and a payment becomes a procurement decision rather than a moral one.

The upsells are the part of the system that has surprised the threat-intelligence community most. The operators are not selling a single product. They are selling a catalog. The decryption key, in cases where encryption is part of the attack, is one item on the catalog. The "data deletion" service — the operator's promise to remove the stolen data from their infrastructure once payment is received — is a separate item. The "proof of deletion" — the operator's offer to provide evidence that the deletion has been performed — is a third item. The "post-incident security advice" — a written report describing how the attackers compromised the victim's environment, intended to help the victim avoid future compromise — is a fourth. Each of these items is priced separately. Each is upsold during the negotiation, often after the initial demand has been reduced. The victim who agrees to pay the reduced demand for the decryption key is then asked, in the same chat window, whether they would like to add data deletion. The victim who agrees to data deletion is asked whether they would like proof of deletion. The victim who agrees to proof of deletion is asked whether they would like the post-incident report. The customer-service vocabulary is consistent throughout. The transactional logic is the logic of any modern software-as-a-service sales process. The operators have, in the language the threat-intelligence reports keep returning to, built a hostile customer-service workflow.

The leaked transcript that the NordStellar analysis treats as its anchoring case is, by the standards of the industry, unremarkable. A victim in the dataset received an initial demand of one hundred and fifty thousand dollars. The victim, during the early phase of the negotiation, mentioned that they had contacted law enforcement. The operators, in the next several messages, escalated their tone. They reminded the victim of what would be published if no payment was made. They referenced specific stolen documents. They moved into the kind of intimidation register that defenders sometimes assume is incompatible with continued bargaining. They were wrong about the incompatibility. The same operators, in the same conversation, after the same escalation, continued to negotiate on price. The demand moved from one hundred and fifty thousand dollars to one hundred and twenty thousand dollars — a twenty-percent reduction. The transcript is analytically useful because it demonstrates a property of the modern negotiation desk that defenders and corporate negotiators frequently misunderstand. The threats and the discounts are not sequential. They are simultaneous. The performance of inflexibility and the economics of flexibility are running in parallel in the same chat window. The operator who is threatening to publish the data is also the operator who is reducing the price. The two behaviors are not in tension on the operator's side. They are two channels of the same coercive system.

The pricing at the upper end of the market is what makes the negotiation desk a board-level concern rather than an operational one. Dark Reading's analysis of ransom demands across the first half of 2024 placed the average demand at five-point-two million dollars. The extreme cases, when they appear, run an order of magnitude higher. India's Regional Cancer Center received a one-hundred-million-dollar demand. Synnovis, a pathology services provider whose compromise produced cascading effects across the British National Health Service's blood-test infrastructure, received a fifty-million-dollar demand. London Drugs, a Canadian retail pharmacy chain, received a twenty-five-million-dollar demand. Change Healthcare, the United States health-care payments processor whose 2024 compromise produced what may be the most economically consequential ransomware incident in the industry's history, was reported to have paid more than twenty-two million dollars to its attackers. The Change Healthcare incident is, in many ways, the negotiation desk's most consequential case study. The compromise reached so deep into the United States health-care system that providers across the country could not process insurance claims for weeks. The downstream cost to the health-care economy ran into the billions. The payment to the operators was a fraction of that cost. The payment was rational, on the victim's side, because the alternative was worse. The operators knew the alternative was worse. The negotiation, when it happened, was conducted from a position of leverage the operators had spent the prior weeks engineering into existence.

The leverage is what the multi-extortion architecture is for. The terminology has evolved across the past several years to describe layers that operators add to the basic encryption-for-payment transaction. Double extortion is the model in which data is exfiltrated before encryption and the threat of publication is added to the threat of operational disruption. Triple extortion adds direct pressure against the victim's customers, partners, employees, or counterparties — the operators threaten to notify the people whose data sits in the stolen files. Quadruple extortion adds the regulatory and securities-disclosure dimension — the operators threaten to expose the victim's compliance failures, file complaints with regulators, demand disclosure of material breaches before the victim's executives can manage the disclosure on their own terms. Each layer compounds the pressure of the prior one. Each layer increases the probability that the victim's calculus shifts toward payment. The operators have learned, through hundreds of prior cases, which layers produce the highest conversion rates against which categories of victim. The application of the layers is no longer improvised. It is, in the language of the operators' own internal sales workflows, a product matrix.

The regulatory layer is the part of the architecture that the public conversation has been slowest to absorb, because the public conversation tends to treat regulation as a defense against corporate harm rather than as a surface the attackers can also exploit. The reporting documents the inversion clearly. Public companies in the United States, since the Securities and Exchange Commission's 2023 cybersecurity disclosure rule, are required to disclose material cybersecurity incidents within a defined Form 8-K framework once the materiality determination has been made. The disclosure obligation is structural. Operators have, since the rule took effect, begun to weaponize it. They reference the rule in their negotiation messages. They claim that the victim is already late to file, that executives are concealing material information, that non-payment will trigger regulatory punishment in addition to the publication of the stolen data. The ALPHV/BlackCat ransomware brand, in a widely reported 2023 case, filed an actual complaint with the Securities and Exchange Commission against one of its own victims, MeridianLink, alleging that the company had failed to disclose the breach within the required window. The complaint was theatrical. It was also analytically significant. It demonstrated that a ransomware operator could, with no infrastructure beyond a web browser and a regulator's online complaint form, weaponize a victim's compliance obligations against the victim. The mandatory-breach-reporting regimes in other jurisdictions — the European Union's GDPR, the United Kingdom's Information Commissioner's Office reporting requirements, the sector-specific rules in health care and finance — produce similar surfaces. The operators have learned to point at every one of them.

The harassment layer runs in parallel to all of this. The operators have, across the past three years, increasingly deployed direct pressure against individual people inside the victim organization. Executives receive phone calls at their homes. Board members receive emails with photographs of their family members harvested from social media. Employees receive text messages naming their children's schools. Customers and counterparties receive notifications informing them that their data has been compromised and providing the contact information for the victim organization's executive leadership. Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42 incident-response dataset for 2025 found that harassment appeared in ten percent of cases — a relatively small share, but one whose strategic effect is disproportionate. The harassment is not deployed against every victim. It is deployed against the victims whose negotiations have stalled and whose executives have demonstrated reluctance to pay. The targeting is selective because the operators understand that the harassment is most effective when it converts a stalled commercial negotiation into a personal-safety calculation. The finance director who, on the morning of the third day of the negotiation, opens a text message containing her teenage son's class schedule, is not making the same kind of decision she had been making the previous afternoon. The chat window has, in a single message arriving through a different channel, become a different kind of conversation.

The data-exfiltration layer is, finally, the part of the system that the most recent reporting argues has become structurally central. BlackFog's first-quarter 2026 analysis found that data exfiltration appeared in ninety-six percent of tracked ransomware incidents. The corresponding observation from the same dataset is more striking. Only one in nine ransomware attacks, BlackFog's analysis estimates, became publicly visible. The remainder were resolved, contained, or handled outside the visibility of leak-site listings, news coverage, or regulatory disclosure. The implication is that the public count of leak-site victims is, even at 7,960 for 2025, a substantial undercount of the total volume of extortion activity. The operators' actual product, the reporting argues, has become the management of stolen-data consequences rather than the restoration of encrypted files. The encryption is a lever. The data is the leverage. The leverage outlasts the encryption. A victim whose systems have been fully restored from clean backups, whose recovery has succeeded by every operational measure the security industry's traditional playbook would recognize, is still a victim if the operators retain a copy of their data and continue to threaten publication. The negotiation desk, in such cases, is the only remaining point at which the threat can be resolved.

The volume of activity that does not appear on leak sites is, in turn, the part of the negotiation economy that the public underestimates most consistently. Some victims pay before they are listed. Some victims are extorted privately, on operator-controlled portals that never appear on the public leak-site infrastructure. Some data-only incidents are resolved through closed-channel sales of the stolen data to third parties — fraud groups, competitor-intelligence buyers, sanctioned-state purchasers — rather than through public extortion at all. The leak sites are, in the industry's own framing, the visible surface of a much larger underlying economy. The visible surface is what the threat-intelligence community can count. The unseen volume is what the threat-intelligence community can only estimate. The reporting's working estimate, drawing on BlackFog's one-in-nine figure and the other vendor counts, places the total volume of extortion activity at several multiples of the public count. The negotiation desk is busier than the leak sites suggest.

The law-enforcement adaptation is the structural reason the market is moving toward the data-only model. Operation Secure, the 2025 international coordinated action against ransomware-enabling infrastructure, involved authorities from twenty-six countries and dismantled more than twenty thousand malicious IP addresses and domains tied to sixty-nine separate infostealer variants. The operation temporarily constrained the access-broker and credential-theft supply chains. It did not eliminate the market. The dominant operators absorbed the displaced affiliates. They shifted branding. They revived old franchises. Most consequentially, they accelerated the shift toward data-only extortion campaigns that require less operational dwell time than full encryption operations. The encryptionless attack — in which the operators steal data, threaten publication, and never deploy file-encrypting malware — has, in the language of the threat-intelligence reports, become the operationally rational choice for affiliates working in a post-Operation-Secure market. The fewer systems the operator has to touch, the smaller the operational footprint. The smaller the footprint, the faster the campaign. The faster the campaign, the higher the throughput. The law enforcement that targeted the supply chain produced the unintended consequence of accelerating the market's move toward the model that produces the highest victim throughput per affiliate-hour. The negotiation desk, in the encryptionless model, is the entire operation. Every prior phase has been compressed. The chat window is the only sustained interaction with the victim.

The defensive implication of all this is the part that the security industry is still working through, and it deserves to be stated directly because the public conversation about ransomware tends to treat the negotiation phase as a technical or insurance problem rather than as the central commercial surface it has become. The traditional defensive posture treated negotiation as something that happened only after the technical defenses had failed. The traditional defensive posture allocated the negotiation work, when it was necessary, to specialized ransomware-negotiation firms whose job was to manage the chat window on behalf of the victim. The specialized firms have been useful. They have not been sufficient. The negotiation phase is, in the modern multi-extortion architecture, not a fallback. It is the place where the operators have invested the most operational capability across the past three years. The chat window is staffed, trained, instrumented, and product-managed. The operators have built playbooks. The operators have built pricing matrices. The operators have built tooling that allows their "support agents" to maintain consistent messaging across hundreds of concurrent negotiations. The defenders, on the other side of the chat window, are running a different kind of operation. The defenders are, in most cases, improvising. The improvisation is no match for the playbook. The asymmetry that has run through every prior layer of the operators' machinery is, at the negotiation desk, the asymmetry that determines whether the victim pays.

The finance director who, at the start of this chapter, was looking at her teenage son's chemistry textbook to avoid looking at the chat window would, by the end of the same Tuesday, have made a recommendation to the chief executive officer. The recommendation, after consultation with the company's outside counsel, its cyber-insurance carrier, and the ransomware-negotiation firm the carrier had brought in on the third day, would be to pay. The payment, after the final round of negotiation, would be one-point-eight million dollars — a reduction from the five-million-dollar position in the chat window on Tuesday morning, which was itself a reduction from the seven-million-dollar opening demand four days earlier. The payment would be wired in cryptocurrency to a wallet address provided through the operators' victim portal. The decryption key would arrive in the chat window within two hours of the wire's confirmation. The "data deletion" service would be added as an upsell during the same conversation. The "proof of deletion" would be declined by the company's general counsel on the grounds that any document produced by the operators could not be verified and might itself contain a residual operational compromise. The post-incident "security advice" report would be declined for the same reason. The company would, in the months that followed, restore its systems, brief its board, file its disclosure with the regulator, and never publicly acknowledge the payment. It would appear nowhere on the operators' leak site. The chemistry textbook would, by the following weekend, have been returned to her son's school backpack. The next victim would, by the time her son sat down for his first-period class, already be in the chat window.

The next chapter takes this layer's logic into its cryptographic frontier: the post-quantum payload architecture that, in the operators' near-term planning, may make even the price-discovery conversation of this chapter obsolete. The negotiation desk, in the current generation of attacks, depends on the operators' ability to convert stolen data and disrupted systems into payment leverage. The next generation of payloads may collapse the negotiation into a single fact. The data will be gone. The encryption will be unbreakable. The chat window will, for the operators who reach that frontier first, become a one-way conversation about a price the victim has no remaining alternative to paying.

# Chapter 9: The Cryptographic Wall

The incident-response lead was looking at a recovered binary on a sandbox machine in a windowless lab on the fourth floor of his firm's downtown office. The binary had been pulled, eleven hours earlier, from the file server of a mid-sized regional hospital network whose three campuses had gone dark inside a fourteen-minute window the previous evening. The hospital's chief information security officer had been on the phone with him at one in the morning, then again at four, then again at six, asking the same question in three different ways. The hospital's backup-restoration team was already in motion. The hospital's legal team was already preparing the regulatory disclosures. The hospital's executive team was already in the conference room down the hall from the chief information security officer's office, waiting for the recommendation that would determine whether the hospital was going to pay. The incident-response lead's job, by eight in the morning, was to provide that recommendation. The recommendation depended on what he was looking at on the screen.

He had reverse-engineered ransomware binaries before. He had, across the past decade, taken apart hundreds of them. The architecture he was looking at on the screen was, in most respects, familiar. The symmetric encryption was AES, the same algorithm the binary's predecessors had used. The file-encryption logic walked the file system in the same order. The exclusion list — the operating system directories the malware would not touch, the file extensions it would skip — was the same kind of exclusion list. The countdown logic that staged the encryption work to maximize damage before defenders could react was the same kind of countdown logic. He had seen these patterns before. He had, in the previous year, helped six victims recover from intrusions that used binaries built on the same general architecture. The difference, on this particular Wednesday morning, was in the key wrapper.

The key wrapper was the part of the binary that protected the symmetric session key — the key that had actually been used to encrypt the files, and that the operator would, in the conventional model, sell back to the victim in exchange for payment. In every prior binary the incident-response lead had taken apart, the key wrapper had been an asymmetric construction built on the well-known cryptographic primitives the security industry had been studying for thirty years. RSA. Elliptic-curve cryptography. The kind of mathematics whose vulnerabilities, when they appeared, were documented in academic papers and indexed in vulnerability databases. The kind of mathematics that defenders had, on rare occasions, been able to defeat — not by brute force, which was infeasible, but by exploiting implementation errors the operators had made. A reused key. A leaked private key from a previous campaign. A weak random-number generator that had been seeded predictably. A botched negotiation between the symmetric and asymmetric components. The implementation errors were the defenders' margin. The implementation errors were the reason decryption tools, when they had appeared, had been able to appear at all.

The key wrapper he was looking at on this Wednesday morning was not RSA. It was not elliptic-curve. It was a clean, textbook implementation of ML-KEM — the lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism that the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology had finalized as the Federal Information Processing Standard 203 in the summer of 2024, derived from the algorithm originally known in the academic literature as Kyber. The implementation had no obvious flaws. The parameters had been chosen sensibly. The key-generation logic appeared to use a properly seeded random-number generator. The integration with the symmetric layer was clean. The integration with the operator's command-and-control channel was clean. He looked at it for a long time. He pulled up the published specification on a second monitor and read through it side by side with the binary's decompilation. The implementation matched the specification. He sat back in his chair. He thought about the hospital's chief information security officer down the hall in the conference room. He thought about what he was going to have to tell her. He thought about the binary on the screen as a small, perfect object — a piece of engineering that had been built, somewhere, by someone who had read the same specification he was reading, and had built it correctly. The hospital had no path to decryption that did not go through a payment.

This is the cryptographic wall. It is, in the operators' near-term planning, the place where the entire economy described across this book finds its terminal logic. Every prior layer — the platform, the affiliate, the access economy, the recovery-trap playbook, the artificial intelligence absorbing the middle of the kill chain, the negotiation desk industrializing the chat window — has been engineered to produce victims who arrive at the moment of payment with no remaining alternative. The cryptographic wall is the last alternative. The wall is what happens when the per-victim key cannot, by any means available to the defender, be recovered. The wall is the place where the chat-window conversation stops being a negotiation and starts being a procurement transaction. The wall is, on the operators' side, the part of the system that is still emerging. On the defenders' side, the wall is the part of the system that the next several years of recovery strategy will have to be calibrated against.

This chapter is about that wall. It is about the cryptographic transition that, when it arrives, will close off the last residual hope that defeated victims have sometimes had — the hope of a leaked key, an implementation error, a law-enforcement seizure, a decryption tool released by a security vendor or a coalition of national agencies. The hope has been thin for years. The hope is, with the standardization of post-quantum cryptography and the slow industrialization of its implementation across the criminal market, on the verge of being gone.

The standardization milestone is the place to start, because the public conversation has tended to treat post-quantum cryptography as a distant, abstract concern — something the United States federal government and the largest cloud providers were preparing for in the 2030s and 2040s, against the eventual arrival of a sufficiently powerful quantum computer. The framing missed the point. Post-quantum cryptography did not become a near-term operational concern the day a quantum computer arrived. It became a near-term operational concern the day the standards were finalized. In August of 2024, the National Institute of Standards and Technology published the first three finalized post-quantum encryption standards: ML-KEM, derived from the academic Kyber algorithm, for key establishment; ML-DSA, derived from Dilithium, for digital signatures; and SLH-DSA, derived from SPHINCS+, also for digital signatures. The standards were published as Federal Information Processing Standards 203, 204, and 205. The publication accomplished something that the cryptographic community had been working toward for nearly a decade. It produced a vetted, free-to-implement, formally specified set of primitives that any developer — defender, attacker, hobbyist, criminal organization, nation-state operator — could integrate into a working system.

The publication did not invent post-quantum cryptography. The mathematics behind Kyber and Dilithium had been available in the academic literature for years. The publication did something more practical. It produced a single canonical specification, a reference implementation, a set of test vectors, a parameter selection rationale, and the institutional endorsement that made the algorithms safe to use. It lowered the implementation barrier from the level of a research project to the level of a library call. The same barrier reduction that allowed legitimate developers — banks, cloud providers, identity systems, mobile-device manufacturers — to begin integrating ML-KEM into their key-establishment workflows is the barrier reduction that allowed criminal developers to do the same. The cryptographic transition that protects long-lived sensitive data is the cryptographic transition that strengthens extortion tooling. The paradox is not theoretical. The paradox is a property of how standardization works. Vetted primitives are vetted for everyone.

To understand why this matters operationally, the hybrid-cryptography baseline of modern ransomware has to be stated directly, because the public conversation about ransomware tends to misunderstand how decryption has actually worked across the past decade. Modern ransomware does not encrypt files with a single algorithm. It uses a hybrid construction. A symmetric algorithm — almost always AES, occasionally ChaCha20 — performs the actual file encryption. Each victim, or each file within a victim's environment, receives its own symmetric key, generated freshly by the malware at the moment of encryption. The symmetric key is then "wrapped" — encrypted in turn — by an asymmetric algorithm. The asymmetric algorithm uses a public key that the malware carries within its binary. The corresponding private key is held by the operator, kept on the operator's infrastructure, and released to the victim only after payment. The victim, after paying, receives the private key, uses it to unwrap the symmetric key, uses the symmetric key to decrypt the files. The architecture is, in modern practice, mathematically infeasible to defeat by brute force. The symmetric key is too long to guess. The asymmetric key is too long to factor. The defender who has the encrypted files and the encrypted key wrapper but does not have the private key has, by the laws of computational mathematics as the security industry currently understands them, no path to the original files.

The defenders' historical margin has not been the cryptography. It has been the implementation. Decryptors, when they have appeared, have emerged because the operators made mistakes. They reused keys across victims, allowing one victim's purchased key to decrypt another victim's files. They leaked private keys when their infrastructure was seized by law enforcement. They used predictable random-number generators whose outputs could be reconstructed from a few captured samples. They miswrote the integration between the symmetric and asymmetric components, leaving a key fragment recoverable from the binary itself. They built negotiation portals whose servers stored the private keys in plain text and were then breached by rival operators or by national-agency operations. Each of these implementation errors produced a window of decryption opportunity for some subset of victims. The windows have been small. The windows have been narrow. The windows have produced the occasional decryption tool that has appeared on the website of the No More Ransom project or has been released by the United States Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency in coordination with European partners. The windows have been the only consistent path to decryption without payment. The windows have not, in any meaningful sense, been a defense. They have been the residual benefit of attacker error in a market where the attackers have, with each successive generation, made fewer errors.

What the post-quantum transition does to the implementation-error margin is what makes this chapter consequential. The cryptographic community has spent decades studying the implementation pitfalls of RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography. The body of knowledge is large. The number of ways to get those algorithms wrong is documented exhaustively in academic papers, in vulnerability databases, in implementation guidelines published by standards bodies. The body of knowledge has been useful to attackers because it has helped them avoid implementation errors. The body of knowledge has been useful to defenders because, when attackers have failed to consult it, the resulting errors have been catchable. The post-quantum algorithms are newer. Their implementation pitfalls are less well-understood. The body of side-channel literature, the body of timing-attack literature, the body of fault-injection literature is, by the standards of the older primitives, still being written. The expectation among cryptographers is that ML-KEM and its peers will, over the next several years, produce their own crop of implementation pitfalls — and that the criminal market will, in some cases, fall into those pitfalls and produce decryptable payloads. The expectation is also that the leading criminal developers, the ones whose tooling actually defines the market, will read the cryptographic literature carefully and avoid the pitfalls. The integration the incident-response lead was looking at on his sandbox machine was, by every indication he could find, of the second kind. It was correct. It was textbook. It was — the word he kept coming back to, when he thought about how he was going to describe it to the hospital's chief information security officer — clean.

The threat-intelligence community has been disciplined about not overclaiming on this point, and any honest account has to follow the same discipline. As of the writing of the most current reporting — written in May of 2026 — there is no widely verified public evidence that major ransomware families have broadly deployed standardized post-quantum cryptography in operational payloads at scale. There has been one widely circulated 2026 claim, from SoftwareSeni, alleging live ML-KEM/Kyber1024 use by ransomware actors. The claim has not been corroborated by major incident-response firms. It has not been documented in the malware-repository feeds that researchers in the threat-intelligence community subscribe to. It has not appeared in government advisories. The reporting treats the claim cautiously, as a plausible near-term forecast rather than a confirmed current state. The honest framing is that the cryptographic shift this chapter describes is a forecast. The forecast is well-founded. The standards are in place. The libraries are being implemented. The criminal market has every incentive to adopt the primitives. The barrier to adoption is implementation discipline. The leading developers in the market have, across the past decade, demonstrated implementation discipline at a level that makes their adoption of ML-KEM in operational payloads a question of timing rather than capability.

The harvest-now-decrypt-later threat model is the part of this picture that the public conversation has been quickest to absorb in other contexts but slowest to apply to ransomware. The framing originated in the financial-sector and government-security communities. The argument is straightforward. An adversary who captures encrypted data today, even if they cannot read it today, can store it indefinitely. The encryption that protects the data today is encryption based on cryptographic primitives — RSA, elliptic-curve — whose mathematical foundations may be breakable by a sufficiently powerful quantum computer at some point in the future. The exact arrival date of such a computer is uncertain. The forecasts range from the early 2030s to the late 2040s. The exact range matters less than the operational implication. Data that is sensitive over long time horizons — diplomatic communications, intelligence sources, corporate trade secrets, biometric records, healthcare data, financial records that contain stable identifiers — must be protected today against a cryptanalysis capability that will arrive some time in the future. The Federal Reserve and other financial regulators have, across the past several years, framed this as one of the central reasons to begin the cryptographic transition now rather than later. The migration is a multi-year project. The migration cannot be performed in a panic. The migration has to be calibrated against the assumption that adversaries are already harvesting.

The ransomware overlap with the harvest-now-decrypt-later model is the part that the reporting has begun to surface in 2025 and 2026. Ransomware operators are no longer simply encrypting and demanding payment. They are increasingly stealing data before encryption — and, in the encryptionless model that has become operationally rational in the post-Operation-Secure market, stealing data without encryption at all. The stolen data is, on the operators' infrastructure, encrypted in transit and at rest with the operators' own cryptographic tooling. The data is then used as the leverage that the negotiation desk converts into payment. The harvest-now-decrypt-later threat model applies to this data in a structural way. The operators who steal a victim's healthcare records, or trade secrets, or executive correspondence today, and whose negotiation with the victim ends without a payment, do not have to delete the data. They can retain it. They can encrypt it with quantum-vulnerable schemes if their current tooling uses those schemes. They can wait. The data, in many cases, will remain valuable for decades. The cryptographic protection of the data, on the operators' side, may not. The data is, in the harvest-now-decrypt-later framing, a long-lived asset whose eventual cryptographic exposure is a different problem from the one the original victim's negotiation team was trying to solve. The victim who refused to pay because they assumed the operators would never publish the data has, in the harvest-now-decrypt-later framing, made a bet about cryptographic timelines rather than about operator intentions. The bet may not pay off.

The platform-specific dimension of the cryptographic transition is the part that the public conversation has barely begun to consider. Ransomware payloads are not a single thing. They are platform-specific implementations targeting Windows endpoints, Linux servers, ESXi hypervisors, network-attached storage devices, cloud-storage buckets, identity platforms, backup systems, and managed-service-provider environments. The cryptographic adoption curves across these platforms are not synchronized. Windows endpoints, where the largest existing ransomware codebase lives, are likely to adopt post-quantum primitives unevenly — some operator brands integrating early, others lagging by years. Linux server payloads, where the operators have invested heavily across 2024 and 2025, are likely to track the Windows curve with a lag. ESXi-targeting payloads, which have become the operational signature of the most damaging modern campaigns, are likely to be the platform where post-quantum hardening produces the most consequential operational shift. An ESXi cluster compromise, in the standard recovery-trap model, can render a victim's entire virtualization estate unrecoverable within minutes. If the per-cluster key is wrapped in a well-implemented ML-KEM construction, the victim's only recovery path through cryptography is closed. The recovery path through backups, if backups have been destroyed in the recovery-trap phase of the intrusion, is also closed. The negotiation desk, in such cases, is the entire operation. The chat window is the only point at which the cryptographic wall can be passed. The price the operators set is the only price the victim has the option to consider.

The artificial-intelligence dimension of the post-quantum adoption is the part where the two threads this book has been carrying converge. The integration of language-model tooling into criminal-software development, which the earlier chapters described in the context of social engineering and operational support, applies also to the integration of new cryptographic primitives into existing ransomware codebases. The implementation work that, a decade ago, would have required a specialist developer working full-time for weeks can now, with model assistance, be drafted in days. The model that read every public Kyber implementation and every NIST test vector and every published cryptographic-engineering guideline can produce, on request, a clean integration of ML-KEM into an existing ransomware codebase. The integration will not, in most cases, be perfect on the first attempt. The integration will be revised by a human developer who has the cryptographic engineering background to catch the model's mistakes. The cumulative effect, across the criminal-development market, is a faster adoption curve for the new primitives than the curve the security community had projected when the standards were finalized. The criminal developers who are willing to invest the time to do the integration correctly will produce payloads that are, by the standards of cryptographic engineering, as well-implemented as the payloads any legitimate cryptographic library produces. The threat-intelligence community's expectation is that the better-resourced criminal-software ecosystems will reach this implementation quality across the next several years. The expectation is calibrated. The expectation is not certain. The expectation is, by every credible measurement of the current trajectory, the working forecast.

What this means for the defenders is the part that has to be stated directly, because the public conversation about ransomware tends to retain a hope of cryptographic reversal that the operational reality has, for years, refused to support. The defense against well-implemented modern ransomware has, for a long time, not been cryptographic. It has been operational. The cryptographic wall, as it hardens across the next several years, makes the operational defense the only defense. Prevention — the work of identity hygiene, edge-device patching, phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication, conditional access, continuous access evaluation, the controls that close off the access economy's supply chain into the victim's environment — is the load-bearing layer. Segmentation — the architectural work of ensuring that an attacker who reaches one part of the environment cannot, from there, reach every other part — is the second layer. Immutable backups — the engineering work of maintaining recovery infrastructure that the operators cannot, from inside the victim's environment, destroy in the recovery-trap phase of the intrusion — is the third layer. Identity security — the integrity of the directory services that, as the recovery-trap chapter described, the operators target first and most patiently — is the fourth. Rapid containment — the institutional capability to detect, investigate, and evict an intruder before the cryptographic wall is built — is the fifth. Resilience engineering — the design discipline of building enterprise environments that can sustain partial compromise without producing total operational catastrophe — is the sixth. None of these layers is cryptographic. All of them are operational. All of them have to be built before the cryptographic wall arrives in the criminal market at scale, because the layers cannot be built under the conditions the cryptographic wall produces.

The economic engine that funds the criminal market's transition is the engine the earlier chapters described. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network's accounting of United States ransomware payments puts the figure at approximately two-point-one billion dollars across the three years from 2022 to 2024. The figure is United States victims only. The global figure, by every credible estimate, is several multiples of that. The payments declined from a 2023 peak. The decline is meaningful. The decline does not mean the engine has been shut off. Two billion dollars of revenue across three years is enough to fund the entire criminal-software development market several times over. It is enough to fund the integration of post-quantum cryptography into the leading payload codebases. It is enough to fund the integration of language-model tooling into the operator dashboards. It is enough to fund the leak-site infrastructure, the negotiation portals, the validated-access markets, the affiliate-recruitment programs, the harassment campaigns, the regulatory-disclosure weaponization. The engine is the reason the cryptographic wall, when it arrives, will be a well-engineered wall rather than a sloppy one. The engine is the reason the operators can afford to wait for their developers to do the integration correctly.

The incident-response lead in the windowless lab on the fourth floor would, by mid-morning on that Wednesday, have made his recommendation. The recommendation, after he had walked down the hall to the conference room where the hospital's executive team had been waiting, would be that the binary's cryptographic construction did not contain a flaw he could exploit and that no decryption tool would be coming. The hospital's chief information security officer, on receiving the recommendation, would walk with her chief executive officer back to the smaller office where the firm's outside counsel and the hospital's cyber-insurance carrier were waiting. The hospital, in the hours that followed, would begin the negotiation. The negotiation would, across the next four days, follow the architecture the criminal market had refined to its current shape. The opening demand would be reduced through a series of staged discounts. The "data deletion" service would be upsold. The hospital's executive team, with the help of the negotiation firm the insurance carrier had brought in, would settle on a payment figure roughly forty percent of the opening demand. The payment would be wired in cryptocurrency. The decryption key would arrive. The hospital's systems would, across the following two weeks, be restored. The hospital's patients, during the same two weeks, would receive their care at degraded capacity. Three of them, the consultants would estimate in their post-incident report, would die from delays in their care that could plausibly be attributed to the systems outage. The hospital's board would, in the months that followed, brief its insurers, its regulators, and its community. The board would not, in any of the briefings, mention the cryptographic detail that had set the floor on the negotiation. The detail was, by the standards of the briefings, too technical to be relevant. The detail had, by every measure the briefings did not name, determined the outcome.

The book closes here. The economy this book has described — the platform, the affiliate, the wholesalers, the recovery trap, the artificial intelligence, the voice on the phone, the negotiation desk, the cryptographic wall — is not, in the public conversation about cybercrime, an economy that ordinary readers have been asked to understand. The public conversation has tended to describe each layer of the economy in isolation, as if each were a separate problem with a separate solution. The layers are not separate. They are an industry. The industry has been industrialized. The industrialization has been funded by the payments the prior years' victims have made. The payments will continue to be made because the victims, when they arrive at the moment of decision, will not have alternatives.

The defenders have been working, across the same period, to close off the alternatives the operators have engineered into existence. The work is unglamorous. The work is, by the standards of the public conversation about cybercrime, invisible. The work is the only thing that determines whether the cryptographic wall, when it arrives at scale, finds the defender in a position to refuse the negotiation or in a position to accept its terms. The investments are expensive. The investments are not, in any organization that has not already made them, easy to retrofit. The investments are the only investments that, in the cryptographic-wall future, will matter. The window in which the investments can be made without operating under attack is closing. The window has been closing for some time. The honest framing, which the threat-intelligence community has worked to keep precise across the past several years and which this book has tried to carry faithfully, is that the window is the operational space the defenders have left. The space is the space this book has been describing, layer by layer, from the platform economy to the negotiation desk. The cryptographic wall is the boundary the space ends at. The boundary is, on the operators' side, closer than the public conversation has yet acknowledged. The boundary is, on the defenders' side, the place where the work that has not been done becomes, in the language of the threat-intelligence community, terminal.

What is being asked of the reader, in closing, is not panic. The threat-intelligence community has worked hard to avoid panic, and this book has tried to honor that work. What is being asked of the reader is recognition. The economy this book has described is real. The economy is well-funded. The economy is industrializing. The economy will not be defeated by the next vendor announcement, the next law-enforcement operation, the next congressional hearing, the next news cycle's outrage. The economy will be contained, if at all, by the slow, expensive, structural work of building the operational defenses that the cryptographic wall will, when it arrives, render the only defenses worth building. The reader, at the close of this book, has been asked to understand what is being defended against, what the defense actually requires, and what it will cost. The reader has been asked to carry that understanding back into the institutions they belong to — their employers, their hospitals, their schools, their towns, their parents' households, their children's universities, their own homes. The understanding is the first investment. The understanding is, by itself, not enough. The understanding is the place where the work that the rest of this decade will require has to start.

# Epilogue

The book closes, but the industry does not. It is worth saying so directly, because the architecture of a book — its opening, its middle, its end — tends to imply, by the simple fact of its endedness, that the subject can be put down. The subject in this case cannot be put down. The reader who has reached this page has, the prose hopes, an understanding of the economy the previous chapters described. The understanding is the first investment. The understanding is not, by itself, a defense. The understanding is what makes the defense imaginable.

What the previous chapters described, taken together, is not a problem with a single solution. There is no patch for the platform economy. There is no takedown that defeats the affiliate market. There is no firewall configuration that closes the access economy. There is no policy intervention that disassembles the negotiation desk. The industry has, across the past several years, demonstrated a structural resilience that the conventional countermeasures have not been able to disrupt at the level of the industry as a whole. Individual brands have fallen and the labor has redistributed. Individual platforms have been seized and the infrastructure has been rebuilt. Individual operators have been arrested and the affiliates have moved on. The industry has continued. The industry will continue.

What changes is what the industry can be made to pay for. Every layer of the architecture the previous chapters described costs money to operate. The platform's infrastructure costs money. The affiliate's tooling costs money. The access economy's marketplace operations cost money. The negotiation desk's labor costs money. The artificial-intelligence integration costs money. The cryptographic-engineering investment that the closing chapter described is going to cost a great deal of money. The money comes from the payments. The payments come from the victims. The victims pay because, at the moment of decision, they have no alternative. The work of the defenders, in the framing the previous chapters carried, is the work of building the alternative. The work of the defenders is the work of making the moment of decision a moment in which payment is one option among several rather than the only option available.

The alternative is operational. The work has been described, in pieces, across the previous chapters. The work is the patient unglamorous engineering of identity hygiene, of phishing-resistant authentication, of conditional access, of segmentation, of immutable backups, of recovery-time objectives that have been tested rather than assumed, of endpoint detection that catches the BYOVD load before the kernel-mode driver is in place, of edge-device patching that closes the windows the access brokers have been opening, of incident-response readiness that produces a containment within hours rather than within days. The work is, in any organization that has not already done it, expensive. The work is, in any organization that has already done it, the reason that organization will, when its turn arrives, have an alternative. The work cannot be retrofitted at speed. The work has to be built at the pace organizational change is built. The pace is slow. The window in which the pace can be sustained is, by the measurement the closing chapter described, closing.

What the reader has been asked, across the previous chapters, is not to panic. The threat-intelligence community has worked hard, across years of public communication, to refuse panic as the appropriate response to ransomware. Panic produces purchases. Panic produces postures. Panic produces budget approvals that are, in many cases, allocated to the wrong layer of the defense. Panic does not produce, in the long term, the kind of structural work the closing argument of the book has named as the only work that matters. The reporting the book has drawn from is calm. The reporting names the systems. The reporting calibrates the forecasts. The reporting refuses, in its language, to dramatize. The book has tried to honor the reporting's restraint while making the systems visible. The work the reporting describes is, by every measurement available, the work that has to be done. The work is not the work of the next quarter's security budget. The work is the work of the next several years.

What is being asked of the reader is recognition. The economy the book describes is real. The economy is well-funded. The economy is organized. The economy operates inside the institutions the reader belongs to — the employers, the hospitals, the schools, the towns, the universities, the households — at every level. The economy has, across the past several years, demonstrated that the institutions it preys on are, in many cases, not prepared for the kind of pressure the economy applies. The institutions have, in many cases, made the operational investments that would let them refuse the negotiation. In many other cases, they have not. The institutions that have not have, when their turn arrives, paid. The payments have funded the next round.

What the reader can do, with the recognition the book has tried to make available, is bring the recognition back into the institutions the reader has access to. The board the reader sits on. The chief executive officer the reader reports to. The chief information security officer the reader has a budget conversation with. The municipal council the reader has voted onto. The school board the reader has volunteered for. The parents' association the reader has spoken at. The conversations are the place where the resourcing decisions get made. The resourcing decisions are the place where the operational defenses get built. The operational defenses are the place where the cryptographic wall, when it arrives, will or will not find the institution prepared.

The reader who has read this book and felt the alarm the book has tried to ground in the reporting should know that the alarm is doing work the reporting itself takes seriously. The alarm is not the panic the reporting has refused. The alarm is the recognition that the prior decade's defensive instincts no longer match the shape of the threat. The recognition is, by itself, not sufficient. The recognition is the start. The work that the recognition makes imaginable is the work the next several years will be defined by.

The book has tried to be honest about what it does not know. The forecasts the closing chapter described are forecasts. The cryptographic wall is not yet, by any verified public measurement, in operational use at scale. The artificial-intelligence acceleration is real but is not yet, by any honest reading of the reporting, full autonomy. The economy continues to evolve in ways the reporting will document and the next several years of public conversation will have to absorb. What the book has carried, across its nine chapters and its supplementary material, is a snapshot. The snapshot is the snapshot the reporting available in 2025 and 2026 made possible. The snapshot will become, with time, an early entry in a longer story. The longer story has not yet been written. The longer story will be written by the people whose work the book has drawn on, and by the people who have not yet begun to do that work but who, by the time the story is complete, will have. The book is a contribution to the conversation that will continue beyond it.

The closing observation belongs to the reporting itself, in the framing the threat-intelligence community has refined across years of careful work. The industry the previous chapters described has industrialized. The industry has industrialized because the industry has been profitable. The industry has been profitable because the victims, at the moment of decision, have not had the alternatives that the operational defenses would have given them. The alternatives can be built. The alternatives have to be built. The alternatives are the part of the future that is, by the measurement the book has tried to honor, available.

# Glossary

Access broker. See Initial Access Broker.

Active Directory (AD). Microsoft's on-premises directory service. It governs which users and machines exist in an enterprise network and what each is allowed to do. In the architecture this book describes, a compromise of Active Directory converts a single foothold into enterprise-wide control. Ransomware operators target it early in the intrusion.

Affiliate. A semi-independent criminal contractor who carries out intrusions and deploys a ransomware platform's payload in exchange for the majority share of the resulting ransom. Affiliates pay platforms a deposit to join, accept the platform's terms, and use the platform's tooling, infrastructure, and brand for the duration of their attacks. Affiliates can migrate between platforms when their preferred platform fails.

Affiliate program. The set of terms a ransomware platform offers to recruit affiliates. Includes the revenue-share split, the deposit requirements, the supported targets, the tooling provided, and the operational rules the affiliate is expected to follow.

Agentic AI. A category of artificial-intelligence systems in which a model performs multi-step tasks by selecting and invoking tools, rather than producing a single response to a prompt. In the ransomware context, the reporting frames agentic capabilities as augmentation of human attacker labor rather than autonomous attack. Forecasts of more autonomous agentic capability appear in the underlying reports as 2026 projections rather than confirmed widespread current activity.

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard). The symmetric encryption algorithm used to encrypt files in almost all modern ransomware payloads. Mathematically infeasible to defeat by brute force at standard key lengths.

Anchoring. A negotiation tactic in which the opening demand is set artificially high so that subsequent reductions feel like concessions even when the final price remains high. Used routinely by ransomware negotiation desks.

Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver (BYOVD). An attack technique in which an operator loads a legitimately signed but flawed kernel driver onto a victim's system to disable endpoint security software. The Baidu Antivirus driver case associated with the DeadLock operation is a documented example.

Cartel signaling. Public or semi-public coordination language used by ransomware brands to imply joint market power, even when actual operational integration is limited. Distinct from a verified operational alliance. The threat-intelligence community is divided on whether the 2026 cartel language between DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin reflects coordination or theater.

Conditional access. An identity-security architecture in which authentication and authorization decisions are made dynamically based on user, device, location, and risk signals. Used to make stolen credentials less useful by adding conditions the attacker cannot easily satisfy.

Continuous access evaluation. A complementary identity-security mechanism in which already-granted sessions are revalidated against current risk signals and revoked if conditions change. Reduces the operational value of session-cookie theft.

Creative destruction. An economic term, borrowed from twentieth-century capitalism literature, that describes the redistribution of resources when established firms fail. Applied in the underlying reporting to the ransomware market: when a platform is taken down, its affiliates, infrastructure recipes, and brand credibility do not vanish but redistribute toward the surviving platforms. The term is descriptive, not approving.

Cryptocurrency. The payment medium in which ransomware payments are made. Most commonly Bitcoin and, increasingly, privacy-focused coins such as Monero. The medium's pseudonymity and irreversibility are operational requirements for the criminal market.

Cryptographic wall. The closing-chapter framing of the book: the point at which post-quantum cryptography is integrated into operational ransomware payloads such that the per-victim key cannot, by any means available to the defender, be recovered without payment.

Data Leak Site (DLS). See Leak site.

DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service). A pressure tactic in which a target's services are overwhelmed by traffic from many compromised endpoints. Used by some ransomware operators as one component of multi-extortion.

Discount schedule. The published timeline on a ransomware leak site indicating how the demand will be reduced if payment is made within early windows and how the discount disappears as the deadline approaches. Engineered to accelerate the victim's decision.

Double extortion. The combination of file encryption with the threat to publish stolen data if payment is not made. Standard model across the leading ransomware platforms.

Edge device. Internet-facing hardware such as a VPN appliance, firewall, or remote-access gateway. A common ransomware entry point. The reporting documents edge-device exploitation rising from approximately three percent to twenty-two percent of vulnerability-exploitation breaches.

EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response). Security software running on individual endpoints to detect and respond to malicious activity. Targeted by BYOVD and other techniques designed to disable it before payload deployment.

Encryption. In the ransomware context, the algorithmic transformation of files into a form that cannot be read without the corresponding key. Modern ransomware uses hybrid encryption: a symmetric algorithm (usually AES) for files, an asymmetric algorithm for wrapping the symmetric key.

Encryptionless extortion. A model in which the operator steals data but does not encrypt the victim's files. The leverage comes entirely from the threat of data publication. Has become operationally rational for some operators as legal and regulatory pressure on payment processing has increased.

ENISA (European Union Agency for Cybersecurity). A European Union institution that publishes threat-landscape reports. Source of the ransomware sector and geographic figures the book cites in the chapter on strategic victimology.

Entra ID. Microsoft's cloud identity service, formerly known as Azure Active Directory. Plays the same role in cloud environments that Active Directory plays on premises. A primary target of access brokers and affiliates because compromise of the directory typically converts a single intrusion into enterprise-wide control.

ESXi. VMware's hypervisor product. A single ESXi host can run many virtual machines, so encrypting a hypervisor can disrupt an entire datacenter. ESXi-targeting ransomware payloads have been among the most damaging in the period the reports cover.

Exfiltration. The transfer of stolen data out of a victim's environment to operator-controlled infrastructure. Distinct from encryption. Required for double, triple, and quadruple extortion.

FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network). A bureau of the United States Department of the Treasury responsible for tracking financial crimes including ransomware payments. The source of the approximately two-point-one billion dollar United States ransomware payment figure for the 2022 to 2024 period.

FIPS 203. The Federal Information Processing Standard for ML-KEM, published by the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology in August 2024. The standard that makes post-quantum key establishment available as a library call.

Harvest-now-decrypt-later. The threat model in which encrypted data stolen today is retained against future cryptanalytic capability. Originally framed for diplomatic and intelligence contexts; increasingly relevant to ransomware operators who steal but do not delete data.

Hybrid encryption. The standard modern construction in which a symmetric algorithm encrypts data and an asymmetric algorithm encrypts the symmetric key. Used in essentially all current ransomware.

Hypervisor. A software layer that allows multiple virtual machines to share a single physical server. ESXi is the most operationally consequential hypervisor in the ransomware threat landscape.

Hyper-personalization. A category of social-engineering tactic in which messages and calls are calibrated against detailed public and stolen data about the specific target. The reporting forecasts increasing AI-driven hyper-personalization across phishing and vishing operations.

Immutable backups. Backup systems designed so that data, once written, cannot be modified or deleted by an attacker who has compromised the production environment. A core defensive layer against the recovery-trap mechanic.

Initial Access Broker (IAB). A specialist who breaks into networks, validates the access, packages it, and resells it to ransomware affiliates without participating in the final attack. A central role in the access economy.

Infostealer. Malware designed to harvest credentials, session cookies, browser data, and authentication tokens from a victim's device and exfiltrate them to a market for resale. The primary feedstock of the access economy.

Intrusion. The early phase of an attack in which the operator establishes initial access, elevates privileges, performs reconnaissance, and moves laterally. Distinct from the payload-deployment phase that follows.

Kernel driver. Software that runs at the most privileged level of an operating system. Targeted by BYOVD attacks because compromise of a driver typically defeats endpoint security software.

Kyber. The academic name of the lattice-based key-encapsulation algorithm that NIST standardized as ML-KEM in FIPS 203.

Lateral movement. The phase of an intrusion in which the attacker, having established initial access, moves from one part of the victim's environment to others. Often involves directory-service compromise.

Lattice cryptography. The mathematical family from which ML-KEM and most current post-quantum candidates are constructed. Believed, on current evidence, to resist quantum cryptanalysis.

Leak site (Data Leak Site, DLS). A criminal-operated public website used to name victims, display countdown timers, publish stolen data when negotiations fail, and provide a public face for the platform. The threat-intelligence community uses leak-site listings as the primary measurement instrument for the industry.

LockBit 5.0. The September 2025 relaunch of the LockBit ransomware platform after its 2024 disruption by Operation Cronos. Supports Windows, Linux, and ESXi in a single package. Requires affiliates to deposit approximately five hundred dollars in Bitcoin.

ML-DSA. The NIST-standardized post-quantum digital-signature algorithm derived from Dilithium. Published as FIPS 204.

ML-KEM. The NIST-standardized post-quantum key-encapsulation algorithm derived from Kyber. Published as FIPS 203. The cryptographic primitive at the center of the book's closing chapter.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA). An authentication architecture that requires a second factor beyond a password. Historically considered a strong defense. Increasingly bypassed by session-cookie theft, MFA fatigue attacks, and adversary-in-the-middle phishing.

Multi-extortion. A general term for extortion models that combine multiple pressure layers. Double extortion adds data-publication threat to encryption; triple and quadruple extortion add further layers such as customer notification, DDoS, executive harassment, or regulatory disclosure threats.

Negotiation desk. The criminal-side workflow that runs the chat-window negotiation between operator and victim. Staffed by personnel who, in the transcripts the underlying reports analyze, behave more like enterprise sales representatives than like criminals.

NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology). The United States standards body responsible for, among other things, the FIPS 203, 204, and 205 post-quantum cryptography standards.

NordStellar. The threat-intelligence vendor whose published analysis of 256 ransomware negotiation transcripts the book cites in the chapter on the negotiation desk.

Operation Cronos. The 2024 multinational law-enforcement action against LockBit. Seized infrastructure, exposed source code and affiliate panels, and humiliated leadership in public. Did not prevent LockBit's September 2025 relaunch as LockBit 5.0.

Patch compression. The decreasing window between vulnerability disclosure and active mass exploitation. Documented in the reports as a 2025 trend in ransomware operator behavior.

Phishing-resistant authentication. Authentication mechanisms designed to be resistant to credential theft and adversary-in-the-middle attacks. Includes hardware security keys and certificate-based authentication.

Platform. In this book's framing, a ransomware brand that provides infrastructure, software, and brand presence to affiliates who run intrusions under that brand's banner. Examples in 2026 include Qilin, LockBit 5.0, Akira, The Gentlemen, and DragonForce.

Platform economy. An economic model in which a central operator provides infrastructure to independent contractors who deliver a service to end users. Applied in this book to the ransomware market, in which the platforms provide tooling, the affiliates run attacks, and the victims are extorted.

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC). Cryptography designed to resist attacks by sufficiently powerful future quantum computers. NIST finalized the first PQC standards in 2024.

Qilin. The ransomware platform that was, across 2025 and into 2026, the most active in the world by leak-site volume. Posted 338 victims in Q1 2026.

Quadruple extortion. A multi-extortion model adding a fourth pressure layer beyond encryption, data-publication threat, and DDoS. Often involves executive or customer harassment campaigns or regulatory-disclosure leverage.

Random-number generator (RNG). A component of cryptographic systems that produces unpredictable values for keys and nonces. Implementation flaws in RNG seeding have, historically, produced some of the decryption-tool opportunities defenders have exploited.

Ransomware. Malicious software that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment for the decryption key. In the contemporary market, the term also covers data-theft extortion that may not involve encryption at all.

Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS). The business model in which a core operator builds and maintains a ransomware platform and licenses it to affiliates who pay a share of ransom proceeds. The dominant model across the leading 2025 and 2026 brands.

RansomHub. A ransomware platform that, in April 2025, went offline without announcement. Its affiliates migrated to Akira, Qilin, DragonForce, and other surviving platforms. Cited as the clearest worked example of the access-redistribution dynamic.

Recovery trap. The mechanic in which operators deliberately destroy or impair the victim's ability to recover without paying: backups, directory services, hypervisors, and identity infrastructure are targeted as a precondition for the leverage the negotiation desk later applies.

Recovery time objective (RTO). The maximum tolerable duration of a service outage, as defined in an organization's continuity planning. The realistic ransomware recovery time, in the cases the reports document, routinely exceeds the contractual RTO by orders of magnitude.

Regulatory leverage. The use of regulatory disclosure obligations — securities filings, breach-notification laws, sector-specific reporting — as additional extortion pressure. Documented in the underlying reporting in cases including the SEC complaint that ALPHV filed against MeridianLink.

Resilience-denial. See Recovery trap.

RSA. An asymmetric cryptographic algorithm based on the difficulty of factoring large integers. The dominant key-wrapper algorithm in modern ransomware. Believed, on current evidence, to be vulnerable to a sufficiently powerful quantum computer.

Segmentation. An architectural defense in which networks and identity domains are divided so that an attacker who compromises one segment cannot, from there, reach every other segment. A core defensive layer.

Session cookie. A small piece of authentication data that a browser stores after a successful login, used to keep the user logged in across page loads. Stolen session cookies can be used to bypass multi-factor authentication, since the session is already authenticated. The infostealer market specifically prioritizes session cookies.

Single Sign-On (SSO). An identity architecture in which a single authentication grants access to many applications. Increases convenience for users and risk concentration for defenders.

SLH-DSA. The NIST-standardized post-quantum digital-signature algorithm derived from SPHINCS+. Published as FIPS 205.

SSO exposure. The phenomenon in which infostealer-infected endpoints expose enterprise single-sign-on credentials. The reporting documents approximately sixteen percent of infostealer-infected endpoints exposing enterprise SSO in the period studied.

Symmetric encryption. Encryption that uses the same key for both encryption and decryption. AES is the standard symmetric algorithm in ransomware payloads.

Triple extortion. A multi-extortion model adding a third pressure layer — commonly customer or partner notification, or DDoS — beyond encryption and data-publication threat.

Validated access. Access listings that have been tested by the broker to confirm they still work, before being sold. Commands a price premium over unvalidated listings in the access economy.

Vishing. Voice phishing. Social-engineering attacks conducted by phone. Increasingly combined with voice-cloning technology to impersonate executives or trusted contacts. The Zscaler ThreatLabz reporting forecasts a substantial increase in voice-based social engineering across 2026.

Voice cloning. The use of AI models to reproduce a target person's voice from limited audio samples, used to make vishing calls more convincing. A core component of hyper-personalized social engineering.

White-label ransomware. A criminal franchise model in which a less-skilled operator uses a larger platform's payload, infrastructure, and negotiation systems under the smaller operator's own brand. DragonForce is the documented example in the underlying reports.

Zero-day. A vulnerability exploited before a patch exists. The ESXi zero-day exploitation through January 2026 is documented in the reports.

ZeroFox. The threat-intelligence vendor cited in the book for skepticism of the 2026 cartel signaling between DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin.
