# Chapter 2: The Franchise Floor

The deposit is around five hundred dollars. Paid in Bitcoin. Not enough to be a serious financial barrier for anyone in this line of work, but enough to filter out the curious, the broke, the careless, and the easily impersonated police officer. The wallet receives the transaction. A new account appears on a panel. The panel resembles, in almost every respect, the dashboard of a software-as-a-service product sold by a mid-market vendor to a mid-market business: a left-hand navigation rail, a row of metrics across the top, a victim queue, a chat interface, a billing module, a knowledge base. The new account holder logs in. He is now an affiliate of LockBit 5.0.

He does not know who built the panel. He does not need to. He does not know who maintains the leak site that will eventually publish his victims' names, nor the lawyers who handle the operator's disputes with affiliates who steal funds, nor the developers who patch the encryption routine when researchers find a flaw in it. He knows only what he needs to know: how to feed an intrusion into the platform, how to deploy the payload, how to start a chat with a victim, how to take a percentage out the other side. He is, in effect, an independent contractor for a company that does not legally exist, working on commission, with a clearly defined product, a defined commission structure, and a defined back-office support team that handles the messy parts of the business.

What he is doing is not new. The structure he is doing it inside is.

For most of the last decade, ransomware was a craft. Skilled crews built their own malware, ran their own intrusions, negotiated their own ransoms, laundered their own cryptocurrency. The work was technically demanding and the rewards, when they came, accrued to the small number of people who could do every step of the job. The ransomware-as-a-service model — the model that came to dominate the second half of the 2010s and exploded in the early 2020s — broke that integrated craft into separate roles. The operator runs the platform. The affiliate runs the intrusion. The labor is partitioned, the skill barrier collapses, and the volume scales.

By 2026, the partition has hardened into something that looks startlingly like the org chart of a small software company. The core operator handles product (the malware itself, kept current against defenders), infrastructure (the negotiation portals, the leak sites, the payment rails), sales enablement (the affiliate panel, the knowledge base, the templated ransom notes), customer success (the chat support that helps affiliates close difficult negotiations), brand (the leak-site design, the press statements, the implied threat that a particular logo carries inside a victim's boardroom), finance (laundering, splits, payouts), and governance (the rules affiliates must follow to remain in the program). The affiliate brings what affiliates have always brought: hands. The hands that get into the network. The hands that map the file shares. The hands that pull the data and push the deploy button.

LockBit 5.0, the platform our new affiliate just paid his way into, is the textbook case of how that organizational thinking shows up in product decisions. The September 2025 relaunch was not a malware refresh. It was a corporate relaunch. The operator chose a cross-platform target list — Windows, Linux, and VMware's ESXi hypervisor, which means a single intrusion can paralyze an entire virtualized data center rather than one machine at a time — because affiliates had been asking for it. The faster encryption routine, the randomized file extensions designed to confuse forensic recovery, the new anti-analysis tricks built to slow down endpoint detection: those were features in the product-marketing sense. They were what the platform offered to attract the customer it was actually selling to, which was not the victim. It was the affiliate. The five-hundred-dollar deposit was not a revenue lever. It was an onboarding filter. The operator wanted serious participants, not opportunists, not researchers, not undercovers. The deposit said: we are running a business now, please act like it.

The transformation runs deeper than tooling. It shows up in the language operators use about themselves. GuidePoint Security's analysts spent 2025 watching the dominant brand of the year, Qilin, add capabilities to its affiliate panel that would not look out of place in the release notes of a customer-relationship-management vendor: spam-campaign automation, ransom-negotiation tooling, even a feature labeled "Call Lawyer" that purported to provide legal counsel to its criminal customers. The affiliate split was tiered like a sales-commission plan — eighty percent of the take to the affiliate on ransom payments of three million dollars or less, eighty-five percent on payments above three million, with the operator retaining the rest. The structure was meant to do exactly what tiered commissions do in legitimate sales organizations: reward the affiliates who closed the larger deals.

The brand layer is competing on terms that any product manager would recognize. Affiliates are scarce, mobile, and price-sensitive. They go to the platform that pays best, runs most reliably, signals greatest staying power, and offers the most useful tooling. Operators that fail any of those tests lose their workforce. A platform's value is its ability to convert criminal labor into successful extortion, and the way it competes for that labor is by being a better operator. Better infrastructure. Better commission. Better brand. Better support.

The brand that pushed this logic to its conclusion is DragonForce.

Until 2025, DragonForce was one ransomware operator among many — competent, persistent, but not at the top of any leaderboard. Over the course of the year, it executed a pivot that may turn out to be the single most consequential structural change in the modern history of digital extortion. It opened a white-label program.

The phrase "white-label" is borrowed, intentionally, from legitimate commerce, where it describes an arrangement in which one company manufactures a product that another company sells under its own name. Generic pharmaceuticals are white-labeled. Store-brand cereal is white-labeled. Many of the cloud security tools on the market are white-labeled. The model is older than capitalism. What DragonForce did was apply it to ransomware. Under the white-label program, an affiliate could use DragonForce's encryption tooling, DragonForce's negotiation portal, DragonForce's leak-site infrastructure, DragonForce's payment workflow — all of it — while presenting to the victim as a different brand entirely. Their own brand. A name they chose. A logo they designed. A countdown clock they branded. The affiliate took eighty percent of the proceeds. DragonForce took the rest, plus the strategic advantage of being everywhere and nowhere at once: many of the new ransomware names appearing in 2025 and 2026 were not new operations at all. They were storefronts. The factory behind them was the same factory.

This changes what fragmentation means. In ordinary statistics, if you see twenty new ransomware brands appear in a quarter, you assume twenty new operations have entered the market. A market with many brands is more diverse, harder to consolidate, harder for any one operator to dominate. The white-label model breaks that assumption. The number of public brands says less about the number of operators than analysts had previously believed. The brand layer can fragment while the infrastructure layer concentrates. A defender chasing the latest brand to surface on a leak site may, in fact, be chasing a reseller; the actual operator sits behind it, invisible, taking its cut, branding the next reseller, and the one after that.

The reason DragonForce could pull this off in 2025 was the timing. In April of that year, an operator called RansomHub disappeared. The collapse was abrupt, unexplained, and consequential. RansomHub had accumulated more than seven hundred and sixty victims, had built one of the most attractive affiliate programs in the underground — a reported ninety-percent commission, the most aggressive split anyone had ever offered — and had become the home of choice for a substantial fraction of the criminal workforce that had drifted from the disrupted LockBit operation a year earlier. When RansomHub went dark, that workforce was suddenly between jobs. Recruiting threads appeared on the criminal forums within days. DragonForce was one of the most visible recruiters. So were Qilin and a smaller brand called Lynx. The white-label program was, in effect, DragonForce's hiring pitch: bring your skills, bring your access, run under any name you want, keep eighty percent of whatever you make, and use our infrastructure to do it. Hundreds of affiliates appear to have taken the offer. The product of that absorption is one of the reasons twenty-one new groups posted to leak sites in the first three months of 2026 — and one of the reasons most of those groups posted fewer than ten victims each. Many of them were not real groups. They were re-branded fragments of DragonForce's franchise floor.

By the back half of 2025, the operators had begun to talk among themselves in public.

In September of that year, reporting surfaced of an attempted coalition between DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin. The three operators were not, on the face of it, natural allies. Qilin was the dominant brand, scaling fast, profitable, in no need of partners. LockBit was in the middle of an attempted comeback from the most damaging law-enforcement operation ever launched against a ransomware brand. DragonForce was the franchise-floor innovator, building its share through volume rather than reputation. What they had in common was a strategic interest in stabilizing the underground market against the kind of churn and self-cannibalization that had eaten the previous decade of criminal ecosystems.

The language they used, reported variously by Dark Reading, ReliaQuest, ZeroFox, and CXO Today, was the language of trade associations and trust-busting tycoons. "Equal competition conditions." Non-aggression among brands. The ambition to "dictate market conditions." The implication of a cartel — not in the lazy journalistic sense of the word, but in the precise economic sense: a group of producers coordinating to manage prices, allocate territory, and discourage destructive competition. Open-source reporting framed the alliance with care. ZeroFox's analysts assessed that the rumored coalition had yet to materialize in any observable way. ReliaQuest, cited in the trade press, was skeptical that real joint operations existed. CXO Today reported the alliance announcement and then noted, dryly, that no joint operations had been observed. The cartel as a press release was real. The cartel as an operating institution was not.

But the press release itself was the diagnosis. Three of the largest criminal brands in the world were behaving like an industry. They were issuing communications strategy. They were aligning rhetoric. They were borrowing each other's credibility. For LockBit, association with healthier brands was a way to repair the underground damage of Operation Cronos and reassure displaced affiliates that the platform was still viable. For DragonForce, association with dominant brands validated the white-label proposition: come run your business on our infrastructure, we are a peer to Qilin. For Qilin, the upside was less obvious — possibly a posture of deterrence toward smaller operators that might otherwise have tried to poach its affiliates. None of it required actual operational integration. All of it required the language of corporate strategy. By 2026, the criminals had moved into the rhetorical space of the C-suite, and the rhetorical move alone — the shift from rivalry to alliance, from chaos to "market conditions" — was a defining marker of the industrialization the rest of this book is going to describe.

There is one further consequence, and it is the consequence that should keep defenders up at night. The franchise model and the cartel signaling, taken together, mean that the question every incident-response team has been trained to ask first — *which group is this?* — has lost some of its meaning. A defender finding a particular ransomware payload on a particular machine cannot reliably infer who is on the other side of the keyboard. The brand on the ransom note may be the brand of a white-label reseller renting infrastructure from a larger operator. The negotiation portal may belong to one platform; the malware lineage may belong to another; the affiliate may have just migrated from a third brand last month. The criminal organization chart is no longer a tree. It is a network of subcontractors, the names on its public face are advertising, and the operator who actually runs the franchise underneath is harder to see than at any prior point in this market's history. Attribution, in ransomware, was always imperfect. It is now, by design, deliberately obscured.

What the new affiliate paying his entry deposit into the LockBit 5.0 platform — or the DragonForce platform, or whichever surviving brand will accept his application next — has stumbled into is a workforce. He has not joined a gang. He has joined a labor market. The platform that processed his deposit has no intention of doing the dirty work. It will give him the tools. It will collect its percentage. It will provide chat support when he gets stuck. And it will count on the fact that there are hundreds of other contractors logging into the same panel that morning, each running their own intrusions, each feeding their own victims into the same negotiation infrastructure, each contributing to a victim count that flows up through dashboards the operator looks at the way a SaaS executive looks at active-user metrics.

Whoever the contractors are, however, they all need one thing before they can begin. They need a way into the victim's network. They need credentials. They need sessions. They need access. And there is, of course, a market for that too.
