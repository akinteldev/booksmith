# Chapter 8: The Negotiation Desk

The conference room had been booked under a meeting name that did not exist. The lights were dimmed because the projector was on. The projector was showing, on the long wall opposite the conference table, the operator's victim portal — a website, dark-backgrounded, accessed through a special browser, with the company's logo in the upper-left corner and a countdown clock in the upper-right. Around the table sat the CFO, the chief information officer, two members of the legal team, an outside counsel from the law firm that handled the company's data-breach work, an incident-response lead from the company's retained security vendor, and a board observer who had been told what was happening seventy minutes earlier and had driven in from the suburbs without putting on a tie. It was 2:17 in the morning. The countdown clock on the wall showed sixty-eight hours and twenty-three minutes remaining. The clock had been showing seventy-two hours when they arrived.

Below the clock, on the same portal, was the proof. A short scrolling gallery of file names and small thumbnails: invoices, the names of major customers, screenshots of the quarterly board deck, a glimpse of the salary spreadsheet for the finance department, an HR file that should not have been there at all. Below the proof gallery was a chat window. The chat window was open. Someone on the other side, who had introduced himself with a single capital letter, was typing. The CFO watched the typing indicator. The little dots blinked. A message appeared.

*Hello again. We understand this is a difficult time. We're here to help you resolve this quickly.*

The room read the message in silence. The legal counsel breathed out, looked at the CFO, and said, in a tone that was not entirely steady, that this was where they would now spend the night. The CFO did not respond. He was looking at the timer.

What the room could not see — what no room in any victim company has ever been able to see — was the office on the other side. The other side is not a single hooded figure. The other side, by 2026, is a desk. Not a metaphorical desk. A literal one. The operator runs a negotiation function the way an enterprise software vendor runs a customer-success team. There are agents on a shift. There is an internal ticketing system. There is a chat interface with templated openings and templated escalations. There is, almost certainly, a manager who reviews the conversations and coaches the agents. There may even be — the underground reporting is unclear, but the operational evidence suggests it strongly — a small group of agents whose job is to call victim CFOs on the phone after the chat has stalled. The desk has hours. The desk has performance reviews. The desk has internal language for the various phases of a negotiation. The desk has, in every observable way, the same architecture as any sales pipeline anywhere in the legitimate economy, except the prospect is a CFO who has just lost the ability to do payroll and the close rate is denominated in millions of dollars.

The market that the desk serves is large. In 2025, ransomware operators publicly listed 7,960 organizations on double-extortion leak sites, a fifty-three percent increase over the previous year. In the first three months of 2026 alone, they listed another 2,122. The top ten brands captured seventy-one percent of those listings. The remarkable structural detail of the 2025 data — caught by analysts who track the back-end infrastructure of the leak sites themselves — is that the actual number of leak sites in operation did not grow during the year. It contracted. The number of active sites moved from eighty-four down to seventy-seven by late 2025. Fewer storefronts. More inventory through each one. The platforms had not stopped consolidating; the rest of the market was catching up to what the platforms had already done. The pressure that did exist on the brands came from outside the criminal economy. Operation Secure, a coordinated 2025 law-enforcement action involving twenty-six countries, dismantled more than twenty thousand malicious IP addresses and domains tied to sixty-nine separate infostealer variants. The action reshaped the upstream supply, briefly compressed the available access market, and demonstrated the reach of the public sector. It did not, by any measure, reduce the volume of leak-site listings. The desks were still hiring. The pipeline was still running.

The data on what happens inside the chat windows is rarer than the data on the leak sites, but it exists. The threat-intelligence firm NordStellar gathered a corpus of leaked negotiation transcripts spanning 2020 to 2026 and analyzed two hundred and fifty-six full negotiations containing eleven thousand five hundred and ninety-nine individual messages. The average negotiation, in their data, ran to forty-seven messages between the victim and the operator. Forty-seven separate exchanges. Forty-seven moments at which a chief financial officer in a conference room at two in the morning typed something, watched the typing indicator on the other end, and waited for a response. Of the two hundred and fifty-six negotiations NordStellar studied, twenty-five point six percent ended in payment. Of those that ended in payment, the median discount from the operator's initial demand was fifty-seven percent. In some cases, the reduction was as much as ninety-six point two percent — the demand started at one number and the eventual settlement reached, after dozens of messages, less than four percent of the original ask. One specific case in the corpus showed the same dynamic in miniature: an initial demand of one hundred and fifty thousand dollars, reduced after negotiation to one hundred and twenty thousand. The first number was the anchor. The second number was the close.

The pattern is not a sign of operator weakness. It is the playbook.

What every CFO in every conference-room scenario has discovered, over the last several years, is that the operator's initial number is a deliberately inflated anchor — a benchmark designed not to be paid but to define the range within which the actual negotiation will occur. The early phase of the chat is engineered around a series of carefully calibrated reductions. Operators describe them, internally, as "discount phases." NordStellar's analysis described the typical opening discount window as a reduction of twenty-five to seventy percent off the initial demand, offered explicitly in exchange for engagement within a defined window of time. The structure is identical to a sales technique that anyone who has ever bought a used car will recognize: the price you are quoted is not the price you will pay; the discount you can negotiate is a discount that was already priced into the original quote; the timer that limits the discount is a manufactured urgency intended to force a decision before the buyer has time to consult their alternatives. The only difference is that the alternative, in a used-car negotiation, is to walk away. The alternative, in a ransomware negotiation, is the leak of fourteen years of customer records, the publication of internal communications about a pending acquisition, the public disclosure of a harassment complaint, and a court appearance in front of a regulator who is going to want to know why the company had not deployed two-factor authentication on the account that the attackers used to walk in. The walk-away cost is the leverage. The operator priced it in.

Beyond the initial discount phases sits what the same reporting describes as an "extortion product line." Operators upsell. The base product — the one the victim came to the chat for — is the decryption key. But there are add-ons. A separate fee to delete the stolen data. A separate fee for a proof-of-deletion certificate. A separate fee for what the operator may, depending on the brand, call "post-incident security advice" — a written summary of how the operator breached the environment, intended to help the victim avoid being breached the same way again. The packaging is deliberate. The product names sound like services. The fees are presented like itemized line items on a software-vendor invoice. The structure is meant to make payment feel like a purchase. It is meant to make the chat window feel like a buyer-seller relationship rather than a hostage situation, because a buyer-seller relationship is one the CFO has been trained to navigate. The customer-service smile, in this room, is the most important thing the operator owns. It is the lubricant. It is the reason the close rate is what it is.

The leak site that hosts the countdown clock plays a second, separate role in the same pipeline. It is not merely a public-shaming surface. It is a stage. Operators deliberately curate what appears on the leak-site listing for a particular victim: which file names are visible, which thumbnails are shown, which categories of stolen data are previewed, which redactions are applied to the partial leaks. The choreography is calibrated. The operator decides, sometimes hour by hour, what level of pressure to apply through the leak site while the chat is open in parallel. A reluctant victim may see, over the course of a few hours, the partial-sample gallery expand: a few more file names, a screenshot of an internal email, a sample of customer data. Each escalation is a tactical move. The Medusa brand made the canonical version of this theatrical device a few years ago: a public countdown clock paired with the explicit option to pay a small fee to delay the disclosure deadline, separately from any actual ransom payment. The countdown clock was its own product. Delaying it was its own purchase. The leak site was no longer a punishment for non-payment. It was a separate revenue stream layered on top of the primary extortion.

The 2025–2026 data tells a deeper story about how broadly the same pipeline operates. The threat-intelligence firm BlackFog, looking at ransomware incidents across its tracking corpus, reported that ninety-six percent of them involved data exfiltration — the operator taking copies of the victim's data, not merely encrypting it in place — and estimated that only one in nine ransomware incidents ever become public at all. The leak-site listings the rest of this chapter has been describing represent, by that estimate, roughly an eighth of the actual operation underneath. The other seven-eighths are settled quietly, before any listing happens, while the chat is still in the discount phase, before the proof gallery is updated. The public iceberg of leak-site posts is small. The submerged iceberg of private settlements is enormous. The negotiation desk handles both. The settlements that never become public are, in many ways, the desk's most important product. They are the ones that prove to the rest of the criminal economy that the pipeline works.

Underlying all of this is the long arc of structural change the earlier chapters have already mapped. Encryption — the original mechanism around which the entire industry was built — has been losing its monopoly for several years now. Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42, looking across its incident-response cases, found that encryption appeared in seventy-eight percent of 2025 ransomware extortions, down from ninety-two percent in 2024 and ninety-six percent in 2021. Data theft was present in fifty-seven percent of cases. Harassment — the targeting of executives, employees, customers, and family members with messages, phone calls, and public posts — was present in ten percent, having peaked the previous year at thirteen. The encryption was, by the math, no longer required to generate leverage. In about a quarter of the cases Unit 42 examined, no files were ever encrypted at all. The operator stole data, applied the leak-site theater, applied the harassment campaigns, applied the regulatory threats, ran the negotiation, and closed the deal — without ever scrambling a single file in the victim's environment. The encryption was, in those cases, an optional module. The pressure mechanisms had become a la carte.

The regulatory layer of the pressure is, in 2025–2026, the most consequential of the new add-ons. In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission's breach-disclosure rule requires publicly traded companies to disclose material cyber incidents within four business days. The materiality standard is broad, the four-day window is short, and the consequences of failing to disclose are severe. Operators have begun, openly, to weaponize the rule. The threat made to a publicly traded victim is no longer just "we will leak your data if you do not pay." It is "we will leak your data, and we will inform your regulators that you did not disclose, and the resulting investigation, the resulting securities-fraud exposure, and the resulting drop in your share price will be your responsibility, not ours." The threat is calibrated to the jurisdiction. In other geographies, similar rules — GDPR breach notification in the EU, NIS2 reporting, the breach-disclosure laws of every U.S. state, the sector-specific obligations of healthcare and finance — provide their own leverage. Operators have done the homework. They know which regulators the victim must report to, on which timeline, with what level of detail. They know which of the victim's customers must be notified, and when. They know which of those customers have contractual rights to terminate their relationship if the breach reaches them through a regulator instead of through the victim's own letter. The chat window, when the CFO sits down in the conference room at two in the morning, is being run by someone who has read the disclosure obligations of the victim's industry more carefully than many of the victim's own employees ever will. The regulator has become, without their consent, the operator's silent partner.

The dollars match the architecture. In the first half of 2024 alone, analyzed ransom demands averaged five point two million dollars per incident. Among the cases on the upper end of the distribution: a one-hundred-million-dollar demand against India's Regional Cancer Center, a fifty-million-dollar demand against Synnovis (a pathology services provider supporting the UK's National Health Service), a twenty-five-million-dollar demand against the Canadian retailer London Drugs. The Change Healthcare incident — the breach that paralyzed United States health-care insurance payments for weeks in early 2024 — concluded with a reported payment of more than twenty-two million dollars. These are not edge cases. They are the leading data points of an industry whose demand range, across a single year, spans from the high six figures of the small-business listings to the nine-figure asks of the largest critical-infrastructure targets. The operator is calibrating the demand to the victim's perceived capacity to pay, the victim's perceived capacity to survive without paying, the victim's regulatory exposure, the victim's media profile, and the victim's likely insurance coverage. The base demand is intelligence-driven. The discount phases are pricing strategy. The leak-site theater is marketing. The chat is account management. The result is a sales pipeline.

Behind the pipeline sit all of the structures the rest of this book has described. The franchise platforms, with their affiliates and their commission structures and their cartel signaling. The wholesalers, with their pre-cut keys and their forty-eight-hour delivery times. The AI-assisted reconnaissance tools that read the victim's data before the negotiation began and produced, for the operator's chat agent, a briefing on which threats this particular CFO is likely to find most persuasive. The hyper-personalized social engineering that produced the initial intrusion. The resilience-denial doctrine that destroyed the victim's recovery infrastructure during the three weeks the operator was inside the environment. By the time the chat window opens, every part of that machine has already run. The chat is the last step. The conference room at two in the morning is not where the attack happens. It is where the bill comes.

The reason the bill, in the overwhelming majority of cases, gets paid is not a moral failing on the part of the people in the conference room. It is the predictable output of a process that was designed to produce exactly that result. The operators have been refining the process for years. They have studied the previous victims. They have built the portals. They have hired the agents. They have written the templates. They have priced the discounts. They have engineered the deadlines. They have, in every measurable way, become a coercive sales organization whose customer is a company that did not, before this morning, know it was about to become a customer.

What happens when even the option to refuse evaporates is the subject of the final chapter. Throughout this book, the leverage at the negotiation desk has rested on a specific technical assumption: that the encryption used in the payload is, in principle, breakable. That if a defender, a researcher, a vendor, or law enforcement obtained the operator's keys — through a seizure, an internal leak, an implementation flaw, or a future advance in cryptanalytic capability — the encrypted data could, eventually, be recovered without paying. The assumption has, until recently, been a quiet floor under the entire industry. The next chapter is about what happens when the floor begins to disappear. About a class of cryptography that may make the recovery impossible not in the practical sense but in the mathematical one. About a future in which the negotiation desk no longer has to bluff. About a future in which paying is no longer the predictable output of a sales process. It is the only output.
