# Chapter 4: The Map of Coercion

At 7:42 on a Tuesday morning, the radiology department of a regional hospital lost access to its picture-archiving system. By 7:51, the operating-room scheduling platform refused to load. By 8:04, the pharmacy could not retrieve medication lists for the inpatient wards. By 8:30, the chief medical officer had cancelled every elective procedure for the day and the emergency department had begun diverting ambulances to other facilities thirty miles away. By 9:15, the local newspaper had a reporter on the phone. By 10:00, parents of children scheduled for outpatient procedures were sitting in a parking lot, calling each other, trying to figure out where to go. By the time the hospital's IT director understood what had happened — that the radiology system, the scheduling platform, the pharmacy database, and the imaging archive were all encrypted, all by the same payload, all by the same operator who had spent the previous three weeks inside the network without anyone noticing — the operational consequences had already escaped from the hospital itself and were unfolding in the lives of patients who had never heard of ransomware and did not yet understand why a stranger in another country had decided their next twelve hours would be ruined.

The question every reader silently asks at this point is the question that defines the rest of this book: *why a hospital? why this hospital? why today?*

The honest answer is that the hospital is on a map. Not a literal map. A statistical one. The ransomware economy has spent the last several years drawing it, and the shape of the map is the answer to the question.

In the 2025 reporting period, the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity — ENISA, the EU's equivalent of a national cyber-defense bureau — assessed that ransomware accounted for eighty-one point one percent of the cybercrime activity affecting EU organizations. Not eighty-one percent of all cyberattacks. Not eighty-one percent of all malicious traffic. Eighty-one percent of the *cybercrime that landed on a real organization* and produced a real consequence. The remaining nineteen percent was everything else — data theft without extortion, denial-of-service operations, espionage from criminal actors, identity fraud, financial-system intrusion, and the long tail of every other money-driven category that intelligence agencies track. Inside the criminal half of the digital threat landscape in 2025, ransomware was not merely the largest category. It was nearly all of it.

The ENISA dataset also has the rare virtue of being granular enough to see the shape of the map. Across the EU, the sector that received the largest share of cybercrime in 2025 was digital infrastructure and services — at thirteen point seven percent — followed by manufacturing at thirteen point two-six percent, and business services at nine point seven percent. Inside the narrower category of ransomware claims specifically, manufacturing leapt to the top: fourteen point nine percent of all EU ransomware victims came from a single sector that makes physical things in physical buildings. Digital infrastructure and services came second at ten point three percent. Inside the data-breach subset of the same incidents — the cases in which an attacker stole information before, or instead of, encrypting it — the concentration was more extreme: twenty-eight point two percent of the breaches hit digital infrastructure and services, and sixteen point eight percent hit public administration. One out of every six known data breaches in the EU was a government.

The geographic concentration tells a parallel story. Among EU member states named in 2025 ransomware and data-breach claims, Germany alone accounted for roughly twenty-three point four percent of the total. Italy came next at a little over eleven percent, followed by Spain at nine point eight, France at nine point five, and Belgium at three point seven. Five countries — five economies in the heart of Europe — produced more than half of the visible criminal claims directed at the entire union. Across the Atlantic, the picture was even more lopsided: GuidePoint Security's annual review found that more than half of all 2025 ransomware victims worldwide — fifty-five percent — were based in the United States. Check Point's Q1 2026 dataset put the U.S. share at forty-nine point six percent, with the rest of the world fighting for the remaining half. ENISA's analysts and several vendor researchers cautioned, repeatedly and in print, that leak-site numbers are not a complete incident census — they undercount victims who pay quietly before listing, miscount duplicates, and exclude entire categories of intrusion that never reach the public stage. But the broad shape of the data, across vendors, across geographies, across counting methodologies, is too consistent to dismiss. Ransomware is concentrated. The concentration is not random.

Why these sectors? Why these countries? Why this hospital?

The answer is operational dependency.

For most of ransomware's modern history, the conventional wisdom about victim selection was that attackers picked the wealthiest target they could find. Banks, big-tech companies, deep-pocketed corporations. The wisdom had a logical core — wealth produces capacity to pay — but the actual data told a different story. The wealthiest targets in the world were not the most-attacked. Goldman Sachs was not on the map. JPMorgan Chase was not. The most-attacked sectors were the ones that could least afford an outage, regardless of how wealthy they were on paper. Manufacturing, because a stopped assembly line costs the factory hundreds of thousands of dollars an hour and breaks contractual obligations to customers within days. Healthcare, because every hour of system outage means cancelled procedures, diverted ambulances, deferred medication, and — if the outage lasts long enough — the genuine deaths of human beings. Public administration, because a paralyzed municipal government has no fallback, no competitor, no insurance pool willing to absorb the political fallout of an unpaid ransom. Digital infrastructure and services, because the customers of digital-infrastructure companies are also digital-infrastructure companies, and the cascade of downstream outages produces a fan-out of harm that escalates the pressure faster than any single victim can negotiate it down. Business services, because professional-services firms hold the operational data of dozens or hundreds of other organizations and a single intrusion can ripple through clients in fields the attacker has never personally targeted.

The mental model that fits is leverage, not wealth. The attacker is not asking who has the most money. The attacker is asking who has the least margin to refuse. A hospital with twenty thousand stranded patient records is not necessarily the richest victim on the operator's pipeline; it is the victim who can least afford to spend three weeks restoring from backup while the local news runs the story. A regional carmaker with a stopped final-assembly line is not necessarily the wealthiest firm in the country; it is the firm whose supply contracts include penalty clauses for delivery delay measured in millions of dollars per missed shipment. A small municipal water utility is not a Fortune 500 corporation; it is, however, the only water utility that the eighty thousand residents of a particular city have. The ransomware operator who selects victims by leverage rather than by net worth is selecting victims who are operationally cornered. The negotiation begins, in effect, before the first chat message is sent.

This is the strategic logic underneath every percentage in the ENISA dataset. The reason manufacturing leads is not that manufacturers are uniquely insecure. They are, in many cases, more security-mature than the financial-services firms that ransomware operators avoid. The reason is that manufacturers have inflexible operational dependencies — production schedules, inventory pipelines, supplier contracts, just-in-time logistics — that turn an IT outage into a balance-sheet event within hours. The reason healthcare appears in every regional dataset is not that hospital security is uniquely weak. It is, in much of the EU and the U.S., among the most regulated environments in any industry. The reason is that hospitals cannot stop being hospitals while a negotiation runs. The reason digital-infrastructure companies appear so prominently in data-breach claims is that the data they hold is the data their customers cannot lose, and the threat of disclosure is exponentially more painful than the threat of encryption alone. The map is not a map of wealth. It is a map of who cannot afford the silence of their own systems.

Inside this strategic logic, a second pattern has emerged in 2025–2026 that is, in its own way, more concerning than the sector concentration itself. The pattern is technology commonality.

A modern ransomware affiliate, as the previous chapter described, increasingly buys access rather than building it. The wholesalers who supply the access do not sort their inventory primarily by sector. They sort it by technology stack. A broker with five hundred validated credentials to a particular brand of internet-facing firewall is selling a product that fits whichever buyer needs that firewall — and that buyer's eventual victim may be a hospital, a factory, a municipal water utility, or a law firm, depending on which company in his pipeline happens to be running the matching device. The result is that victim selection has, in part, decoupled from sector. It now follows software. Check Point's Q1 2026 dataset captured this with unusual clarity in two countries. Turkey produced twenty-three publicly listed victims in the first quarter of 2026, and three operators — LockBit, DragonForce, and The Gentlemen — accounted for roughly seventy percent of them. Japan produced twenty-one victims, and three different operators — The Gentlemen, Everest, and Nightspire — produced roughly sixty-two percent. Two of those Japanese operators were linked, in the same reporting, to mass exploitation of the same FortiGate vulnerability. The country had not been chosen because of any strategic interest in Japan. It had been chosen because the technology installed inside the country happened to be vulnerable, and three operators with three different brand names were each working their way through the same available supply.

The map of victims, in 2026, is therefore two maps overlaid. The first map is the strategic one: operators picking targets by operational dependency. The second map is the technological one: affiliates picking targets by which exploit happens to be in inventory this month. Both maps are real. Both maps explain different parts of the data. The intersection — the place where strategic dependency meets a vulnerable technology stack — is the place where victims are most reliably found. A hospital that runs a particular VPN appliance. A manufacturer that exposes a particular remote-access portal. A municipal government that has not yet patched a particular Oracle database server. The combination of "operationally cornered" plus "happens to expose the equipment a wholesaler is currently selling access to" is the combination that generates the highest probability of becoming a public victim in the next quarter.

The map keeps growing. The threat-intelligence firm CYFIRMA, looking at its own four-year incident dataset, reported that December 2025 produced eight hundred and one ransomware incidents — the highest monthly total it had ever recorded — and that January 2026 followed with six hundred and eighty-three, compared with five hundred and eleven in January of the previous year. The elevated baseline of 2025 was not a peak. It was a floor. The volume that would have been considered an industry record in 2022 became, in the back half of 2025, the normal weekly tempo of the public leak sites. Every month that passes adds another set of names to the map, and every name on the map is, in retrospect, a story about a particular organization whose operational dependency made it a worthwhile target for a particular operator who happened to have access to a particular technology stack on a particular morning.

What this tells defenders, and what every reader of the next chapter will need to keep close, is that the question "who is on the map?" was never the most important question. The map is descriptive, not predictive. It describes where coercion has worked, and it tells the operator where coercion is likely to work again, but it does not tell defenders very much about what to do with the information once they have it. The more decisive question is the one the operators began asking quietly several years ago, around the same time encryption began losing its monopoly: once you are inside, which systems do you break?

The answer is not "the data." The data is incidental. The encryption of a few thousand files is a distraction. The systems that actually determine whether the victim has any meaningful leverage in the negotiation are the systems that would, in any other scenario, let the victim recover from the attack at all. The backups. The directory services. The hypervisors. The identity infrastructure that controls who can log into what. The systems that the IT director in our opening hospital had spent her career maintaining, and the systems that, by the morning of the outage, had already been silently dismantled three weeks earlier by an operator who knew exactly which doors to lock from the inside before kicking the front door in. The next chapter is the story of that quieter, more deliberate destruction — the part of a modern ransomware attack that the public never sees and the victim, almost always, discovers far too late.
