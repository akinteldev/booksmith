# Chapter 1: Crime as a Service

## Core Question

What is the Ransomware-as-a-Service model, why is it so resilient to disruption, and when did extortion become a franchise?

## Purpose in the Book

Chapter 1 is the structural foundation. Before the reader can understand any specific aspect of the ransomware economy, they need to understand the model itself: how RaaS separates the criminal developer from the criminal executor, why this division makes it so hard to dismantle, and why law-enforcement "wins" keep failing to produce lasting results. This chapter introduces the book's central metaphor — ransomware as a platform economy — and establishes the intellectual frame for everything that follows.

## Required Content

### Must Cover

- The RaaS model explained: core operators (platform, tooling, infrastructure, branding, leak sites, negotiation portals) vs. affiliates (intrusion, victim selection, execution)
- The consolidation dynamic: how disruption redistributes rather than eliminates ransomware capacity; affiliates migrate, brands die but the workforce doesn't
- Q1 2026 market structure: 2,122 victims, 71 active groups, top 10 groups controlling 71% of victim volume — the platform economy is already here
- The historical arc: how ransomware evolved from scattered criminal experiments to today's industrial model

### Should Include

- The RansomHub collapse as a case study in affiliate labor migration: 760+ victims before going dark in April 2025; affiliates moved to Qilin and DragonForce within weeks
- The platform economy metaphor: ransomware cartels as criminal counterparts to Uber/Amazon Marketplace — HQ sets the rules, affiliates do the work
- Why brand disruption alone fails: the workforce, tools, access pipelines, and criminal relationships survive brand collapse

### Optional (if it fits naturally)

- A brief glimpse at the revenue-sharing model (affiliates keeping 80%–90% of ransom proceeds) to show why affiliates flock to dominant platforms

## Structural Requirements

### Opening

Hypothetical vignette: a criminal affiliate in a rented apartment somewhere in Eastern Europe logs into a criminal forum and applies to join a RaaS program. The application process: deposit, vetting, access to the affiliate panel. He is, in effect, applying for a job at a company he has never met, whose real operators he will never know. This normalizes the corporate structure and grounds the platform economy metaphor early.

### Body Flow

1. The structure: what RaaS actually is — two-tier criminal economy with platform providers and affiliates, each doing specialized work → the franchise metaphor
2. The concentration story: how Q1 2026 looks less like a criminal underground and more like a consolidating industry, with a handful of dominant platforms absorbing market share
3. The resilience argument: why enforcement actions don't end ransomware — they just move the affiliates to the next platform (RansomHub as the proof)

### Closing

End on the revelation: the reason ransomware cannot be "solved" by arresting a few operators is structural — the workforce is mobile, the tools are modular, and the platform model survives any individual disruption. The reader now understands the adversary they are dealing with.
