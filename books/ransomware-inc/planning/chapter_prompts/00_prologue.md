# Prologue: The Invoice

## Core Question

What does it actually feel like when the ransom note appears — and what has already happened by the time you see it?

## Purpose in the Book

The Prologue is the book's on-ramp. Before the reader has any framework for understanding the criminal enterprise behind ransomware, they experience it from the victim's seat. A well-crafted opening vignette drops them into a Monday morning at a mid-sized organization when the screens go dark and the ransom note appears. The goal is visceral recognition: this is a real thing that happens to real organizations, and the reader may be closer to it than they think. The Prologue ends by pulling back the curtain just enough to pose the book's central question: who is on the other end of that chat window?

## Required Content

### Must Cover

- A hypothetical vignette grounded in documented attack mechanics: the moment a victim organization discovers the ransom note, what systems are down, what the note says, and the psychological effect of the countdown timer
- The fact that the attack was almost certainly complete long before the note appeared — data was already stolen, backups may already be gone
- A brief, factual orienting statement: ransomware is no longer a rogue hacker in a hoodie; it is an organized criminal industry that posted more than 2,100 victims in a single quarter of 2026

### Should Include

- A concrete detail about the negotiation portal — victim support chat, "customer service" aesthetic, staged discount offer
- A hint that the organization behind the attack has a division of labor, a brand, and a revenue model
- The emotional register of the victim's first hours: the board call, the lawyer, the silence

### Optional (if it fits naturally)

- A single statistic that reframes scale: in Q1 2026 alone, 2,122 organizations found themselves in this position

## Structural Requirements

### Opening

Cold open: a hypothetical Monday morning. An IT manager at a mid-sized logistics company arrives to find workstations locked, the help desk flooded, and a single text file on every desktop: "Your data has been encrypted. Visit [link] to discuss terms." The tone is controlled panic. The room fills up. Nobody knows what to do first.

### Body Flow

1. The victim's experience: systems down, phones ringing, the ransom note → pull back to show what has already happened (data stolen days ago, backups possibly gone)
2. The chat window: the "support" aesthetic, the countdown clock, the initial demand — reveal that there is a person (or team) on the other end who does this professionally, all day, every day
3. The reveal: who is on the other end? Not a lone hacker — an organized criminal enterprise. This is the pivot that sets up the entire book.

### Closing

End with a question, not a summary: "But who, exactly, is on the other end of that chat window? And how did extortion become a managed service?" — pivot to the book's structural investigation.
