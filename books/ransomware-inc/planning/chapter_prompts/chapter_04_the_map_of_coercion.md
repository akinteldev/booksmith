# Chapter 4: The Map of Coercion

## Core Question
Why does the ransomware economy reliably hit manufacturing, healthcare, and digital infrastructure, and what does the map of victims reveal about what extortion actually rewards?

## Purpose in the Book
This chapter opens Part II. It pivots from how the industry is structured to how it operates: specifically, where it points its operations. The reader must come away understanding that ransomware victimology is not random — it is a deliberate strategic selection based on coercive leverage, operational dependency, regulatory exposure, and downstream effect. Sector and geographic concentration is the visible signature of an extortion economy that has learned where pressure pays. This chapter sets up Chapter 5's drill-down into the recovery-infrastructure assault.

## Required Content

### Must Cover
- ENISA's headline assessment: ransomware accounted for 81.1% of cybercrime activity affecting EU organizations during the 2025 reporting period. Translate the number into intuition.
- The sector mix from Report #5: digital infrastructure and services at 13.7% of cybercrime activity overall, manufacturing at 13.26%, business services at 9.7%; within ransomware claims specifically, manufacturing leading at 14.9% and digital infrastructure and services at 10.3%; for data-breach claims, digital infrastructure and services at 28.2% and public administration at 16.8%.
- The geographic mix: Germany at 23.4%, Italy at 11.33%, Spain at 9.8%, France at 9.5%, Belgium at 3.7% of EU member-state ransomware and data-breach claims, with manufacturing dominant across all five.
- The strategic logic: manufacturing, transport, digital infrastructure, finance, healthcare, media, and public administration present high extortion value because outages translate quickly into production stoppages, public-service disruption, regulatory exposure, patient-safety risk, reputational harm, and contractual penalties.
- Healthcare specifically: ENISA's concern about ransomware impact on medical procedures; the patient-safety dimension as a form of extortion leverage no other sector matches.
- The financial sector framing: attractiveness due to concentration of financial and personal data.

### Should Include
- The fragmentation-to-convergence pattern: branding fragments while strategic behavior converges — steal data, exploit exposed infrastructure, compromise identity, suppress recovery, intensify pressure. Mention but do not unpack the resilience-targeting framing that Chapter 5 will fully develop.
- A grounded vignette: a regional hospital's CIO learning that a leak-site countdown has begun and that elective procedures are being deferred. Or a German auto-parts supplier whose plant floor halts when their ERP goes dark. Use only mechanics described in Report #5.
- A short caveat about reporting bias: leak-site and ENISA data reflect what is observed and reported, not the full incident census. Public-administration and healthcare incidents may be underreported in some jurisdictions; manufacturing's prominence may be partly a function of disclosure obligations.

### Optional (if it fits naturally)
