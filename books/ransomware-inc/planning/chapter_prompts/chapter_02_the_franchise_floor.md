# Chapter 2: The Franchise Floor

## Core Question
How did digital extortion adopt the language and logistics of franchising, and what happens when criminal brands try to behave like a cartel?

## Purpose in the Book
This chapter goes one layer deeper into the platform argument introduced in Chapter 1. It shows the reader how RaaS operators behave structurally like franchisors — they offer branding, infrastructure, support, and revenue terms, and they compete for affiliate labor on those terms. It also examines the more provocative claim that some of these brands have begun signaling cartel-like coordination, and it carefully separates verifiable cooperation from theatrical posturing. By the end of this chapter the reader should understand that ransomware is not only industrialized but franchised — and that the criminal market has its own competitive dynamics, partnership behaviors, and governance frictions.

## Required Content

### Must Cover
- The white-label model in detail, using DragonForce as the canonical example: cartel formation in 2025, 80% affiliate profit share, white-label arrangements that let less-technical operators run under their own brand on DragonForce infrastructure.
- The competing economics: RansomHub's previously offered ~90% affiliate split before its abrupt shutdown, which intensified affiliate competition across the ecosystem.
- The LockBit 5.0 platform in business-model terms: the September 2025 relaunch, the affiliate onboarding deposit of approximately $500 in Bitcoin, multi-platform targeting (Windows, Linux, ESXi), and new evasion features and randomized file extensions as product differentiation.
- The cartel-signaling story: the reported attempted coalition involving DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin; the explicit "equal competition conditions" rhetoric; non-aggression proposals; shared infrastructure framing; structured recruitment.
- The honest calibration: open-source reporting describes coordination signals, not a confirmed merger. The chapter must respect that distinction and not overclaim cartel integration.

### Should Include
- The platform-services analogy: brand reputation, leak-site infrastructure, negotiation workflow, payment handling, dispute resolution, and affiliate governance behave like the services a software vendor offers a partner channel.
- The point that affiliate behavior responds to terms: commission splits, onboarding deposits (trust filters), tooling quality, payment reliability, and brand resilience to takedowns determine where labor flows next.
- A hypothetical vignette grounded in Report #1's mechanics: e.g., a mid-tier criminal operator weighing whether to keep paying the LockBit 5.0 deposit or migrate to DragonForce's white-label terms; or an affiliate displaced by the RansomHub shutdown evaluating new platforms by profit split and infrastructure quality.

### Optional (if it fits naturally)
- A note that high victim volume on a leak site can mask weaker payment performance (the GuidePoint observation about Qilin) — useful here as a reminder that the "market" is not transparent even to its own participants.

## Structural Requirements

### Opening
Open with a grounded scene: an aspiring affiliate operator reading a recruitment post and weighing the terms — deposit required, split offered, infrastructure promised. Make the scene feel like a job evaluation, because that is what it is.

### Body Flow
1. The recruitment scene → transition into the franchise economics behind it.
2. White-label and revenue splits — DragonForce 80%, RansomHub's prior 90% legacy, LockBit 5.0's $500 deposit — as instruments of competitive positioning → transition into the cartel signal.
3. The attempted coalition and its rhetoric; what cartel signaling looks like and what it actually accomplishes → transition into the limits.
4. The honest limits: signaling is not integration; brand alliances are not a merger; the market remains competitive even when its participants speak of solidarity.

### Closing
End on revelation: criminal brands now behave like franchisors competing for labor, and they have begun talking like a cartel. Both facts matter. Set up Chapter 3's pivot to the labor market that actually feeds these platforms.
