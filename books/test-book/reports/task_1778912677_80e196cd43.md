# Technological Evolution of Ransomware Payloads and Automation in 2025–2026: Agentic AI, Autonomous Tradecraft, and the Early Use of Post-Quantum Cryptography

## Abstract

Ransomware operations in 2025 and 2026 show a clear technical and operational transition: from comparatively static malware families and manually coordinated intrusion workflows toward adaptive, AI-assisted, and increasingly autonomous extortion ecosystems. The strongest near-term shift is not merely “better ransomware binaries,” but the industrialization of the entire attack lifecycle through Agentic AI and large language models (LLMs), coupled with a strategic move away from costly full-disk encryption toward data-only extortion, selective or slow encryption, and AI-enhanced negotiation. In parallel, a more speculative but highly consequential trend is emerging around cryptographic hardening of ransomware payloads, including discussion of post-quantum primitives such as ML-KEM/Kyber1024 to protect key exchange and frustrate future recovery efforts. 

Based on the supplied ISACA and ENISA material, and weighed against the broader threat-intelligence framing referenced by those sources, this report concludes that **Agentic AI is already the dominant practical accelerator of ransomware operations in 2025–2026, while post-quantum cryptography (PQC) in ransomware remains an early-stage but strategically dangerous development whose impact could become severe if operationalized at scale**. The evidence is much stronger for AI-enabled automation and hyper-personalized social engineering than for widespread real-world PQC deployment. However, even limited proof-of-concept or niche criminal adoption of PQC merits immediate defensive planning because it directly targets one of the last mitigation hopes after successful encryption: future cryptanalytic or implementation-based recovery.

## Introduction

The ransomware ecosystem in 2025–2026 is evolving along two converging axes:

1. **Automation and attacker productivity**, increasingly powered by Agentic AI and LLM-backed tooling.
2. **Cryptographic resilience of payloads**, including emerging interest in post-quantum mechanisms.

The ISACA industry analysis for 2026 highlights several concrete shifts: a rise in new cyberthreat groups, movement away from resource-heavy encryption to data-only extortion and slow-encryption tactics, increasing ransom demands, and the appearance of malware families that incorporate LLMs directly into execution, including PROMPTFLUX, PROMPTSTEAL, and PROMPTLOCK. ISACA also cites examples of ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) innovation such as AI-driven negotiation support and mobile affiliate control panels, showing that the “ransomware product” is now the entire criminal business platform, not only the encryptor itself ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups)).

ENISA’s work is different in scope but highly relevant. Its post-quantum cryptography publications emphasize that quantum-capable cryptanalysis creates a need to redesign protocols, integrate quantum-safe systems, and consider changes such as double encryption, double signatures, and hybrid migration patterns. While ENISA is discussing legitimate defensive transition, the same logic can be appropriated by threat actors seeking to harden ransomware key management and make decryption infeasible both now and in the future ([in-text citation](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/news/enisa-news/post-quantum-cryptography-anticipating-threats-and-preparing-the-future)).

## The 2025–2026 Ransomware Shift: From Encryption Event to Automated Extortion Platform

### The Decline of Full-Scale, Resource-Heavy Encryption

A key finding in the ISACA reporting is that threat actors are increasingly moving away from “resource-heavy encryption tactics” and toward:

- data-only extortion,
- slow-encryption tactics, and
- higher ransom demands ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups)).

This is strategically rational. Large-scale encryption has several disadvantages for criminals:

- It is noisy and easier to detect.
- It can trigger rapid containment.
- It may create operational instability on victim systems before exfiltration is complete.
- It increasingly offers diminishing returns when data theft alone can create equivalent coercive leverage.

Slow encryption, selective encryption, and extortion-only operations are therefore evolutionary optimizations. In effect, the payload is becoming just one configurable option in a broader extortion playbook.

### RaaS Platformization

ISACA’s cited materials point to ransomware groups such as Qilin and GLOBAL GROUP as examples of RaaS maturation, including **AI-driven negotiation support** and **mobile control panels for affiliates** ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups)). This is important because it indicates that automation is no longer confined to malware execution; it is extending to:

- affiliate onboarding,
- victim triage,
- negotiation workflows,
- campaign monitoring,
- and potentially revenue optimization.

The technical payload is increasingly embedded inside an operator experience resembling legitimate SaaS administration.

## Trend 1: Agentic AI and LLMs Automating 80–90% of the Attack Lifecycle

###! What “Agentic AI” Means in Ransomware Operations

Agentic AI refers to systems that do not only generate text or code on request, but can pursue goals semi-autonomously across multiple steps: gathering information, making decisions, adapting outputs, and chaining actions. In ransomware campaigns, this model can compress the time, expertise, and staffing required for a full intrusion.

The supplied ISACA material states that new malware families such as **PROMPTFLUX, PROMPTSTEAL, and PROMPTLOCK** incorporate LLMs directly into execution, enabling them to:

- dynamically generate malicious scripts,
- obfuscate code in real time to evade detection,
- and create required malicious functions on demand rather than embedding them natively ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups)).

If validated broadly, this is a substantial technical progression. It means the malware binary no longer needs to carry every capability in static form. Instead, the payload can behave more like a runtime orchestration layer that queries or leverages an LLM to synthesize task-specific code.

### Operational Areas Most Susceptible to 80–90% Automation

Although the supplied sources do not provide a formal “80–90%” metric, that estimate is plausible for large portions of the attack chain. The most automatable components are shown below.

| Attack Lifecycle Phase | Traditional Human Burden | Agentic AI Contribution in 2025–2026 | Likely Automation Feasibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reconnaissance | Manual OSINT, organization profiling | Automated collection and summarization of public data, security stack clues, executive mapping | High |
| Initial access preparation | Drafting phishing lures, exploit adaptation notes | Hyper-personalized lure generation, language localization, pretext creation | High |
| Payload staging | Manual script editing and packing | On-demand script generation and obfuscation | High |
| Privilege escalation support | Analyst-crafted commands and task lists | Context-aware command suggestions and adaptive execution logic | Medium |
| Lateral movement planning | Manual host prioritization | Automated path recommendations based on discovered environment data | Medium |
| Exfiltration and extortion prep | Victim data review, leverage identification | Document triage, sensitive data classification, pressure-point extraction | High |
| Negotiation | Human-operated extortion chat | AI-assisted or AI-led negotiation strategy, pricing adaptation, multilingual engagement | High |

The biggest effect is not full “hands-free hacking.” It is **human amplification**. One operator can manage more victims, less-skilled affiliates can execute more sophisticated campaigns, and malware can adapt faster than traditional signature-based defenses.

### Hyper-Personalized Social Engineering

The strongest practical use case for Agentic AI is social engineering. LLMs are well suited to:

- imitate corporate communication style,
- tailor pretexts to a target’s role,
- produce grammatically accurate multilingual lures,
- summarize breached or public data into persuasion hooks,
- and maintain conversational consistency during follow-up exchanges.

This matters because phishing and social pretexting remain among the cheapest initial access vectors. Agentic AI allows criminals to generate individualized outreach at scale, something previously constrained by labor and language quality. In executive fraud, vendor invoice fraud, help-desk impersonation, and callback-phishing scenarios, personalization directly improves conversion.

A likely 2026 workflow is:

1. scrape public and stolen organizational data,
2. identify hierarchy, projects, vendors, and current events,
3. auto-generate lures specific to each employee,
4. adapt wording based on response behavior,
5. deliver malware or credential-harvest links,
6. use AI to continue the conversation if the victim hesitates.

This turns social engineering from a batch spam tactic into an adaptive interaction system.

### LLM-Enabled Malware Transformation and Evasion

ISACA’s reporting on PROMPTFLUX/PROMPTSTEAL/PROMPTLOCK describes three dangerous capabilities: dynamic script generation, real-time obfuscation, and on-demand function creation. These features would undermine many established defensive assumptions:

- **Static signatures degrade** because code can be generated differently per execution.
- **Sandboxing is complicated** because behavior may depend on environmental prompts or runtime context.
- **Code reuse tracking becomes harder** since functionality can be synthesized instead of compiled into a reusable family baseline.

From a defender’s perspective, this shifts emphasis from binary-centric detection to behavior, identity, privilege, outbound communications, and process lineage.

### AI-Assisted Negotiation and Psychological Pressure

ISACA’s cited examples of AI-driven negotiation support are highly significant because they show threat actors optimizing not just intrusion mechanics but revenue extraction. Negotiation bots or copilots can:

- estimate victim payment thresholds,
- recommend pressure tactics based on exfiltrated data,
- localize messages by jurisdiction and industry,
- sequence threats for maximum leverage,
- and operate continuously across time zones.

This can shorten the negotiation cycle and increase conversion rates. It also reduces the need for experienced human extortion specialists, lowering barriers to entry for new groups.

## Trend 2: Post-Quantum Cryptography in Ransomware Payloads

### Why PQC Matters to Ransomware

Historically, ransomware cryptography has usually combined symmetric file encryption with asymmetric protection of session keys. The attacker’s challenge is to ensure victims cannot recover the per-file or per-host keys without paying or obtaining implementation flaws. 

Post-quantum cryptography raises the stakes. ENISA’s reporting emphasizes that quantum-capable cryptanalysis creates pressure to redesign protocols and integrate quantum-safe systems into existing cryptographic stacks, including questions of hybrid constructions, double encryption, and migration architecture ([in-text citation](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/node/8528)). For defenders, this is about long-term resilience. For ransomware developers, the offensive opportunity is straightforward: **replace conventional key encapsulation or exchange with quantum-resistant primitives so that future cryptanalytic breakthroughs do not help victims**.

### The Claimed Example: PE32 Using ML-KEM/Kyber1024

The prompt references a **PE32 ransomware family using ML-KEM/Kyber1024**. That claim is not supported directly in the supplied ISACA or ENISA text, so it must be treated as an **emerging hypothesis or externally asserted case study**, not a confirmed fact from the provided evidence. However, technically, such an implementation is plausible.

Kyber1024, now aligned with the ML-KEM family in modern standardization contexts, is designed as a post-quantum key encapsulation mechanism. In a ransomware design, it could be used to:

1. generate a symmetric session key per victim or per file set,
2. encapsulate that key to the attacker’s public key using ML-KEM/Kyber1024,
3. destroy plaintext key material locally,
4. store only the ciphertext capsule and encrypted content.

The direct implication is not that decryption becomes “mathematically impossible” in an absolute sense—security claims are never absolute—but that **recovery without the private key becomes far less likely to benefit from future advances against classical public-key cryptography**.

### ENISA’s Relevance to Offensive PQC Adoption

ENISA’s post-quantum material highlights several themes that are directly transferable to criminal use:

- integration of post-quantum systems into existing protocols,
- need to design new cryptographic protocols,
- impact of double encryption and double signatures,
- evaluation of quantum-resistant solution families and trade-offs ([in-text citation](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/topics/artificial-intelligence-and-next-gen-technologies)).

A ransomware developer could adopt a **hybrid cryptographic model**, for example:

- AES or ChaCha20 for high-speed file encryption,
- ML-KEM/Kyber1024 for wrapping the symmetric key,
- optional classical backup encapsulation for compatibility,
- signed ransom metadata or decryptor updates using hybrid signature mechanisms.

This would not make the malware intrinsically more destructive during execution, but it would significantly improve attacker confidence in the persistence of cryptographic denial.

### Practical Constraints on PQC Ransomware Adoption

Despite the strategic appeal, PQC use in ransomware is likely still limited in 2025–2026 for practical reasons:

| Constraint | Effect on Criminal Adoption |
|---|---|
| Implementation complexity | Poorly implemented PQC may create bugs that break campaigns or expose keys |
| Payload size and performance | Some PQC schemes introduce larger keys/ciphertexts and integration overhead |
| Criminal economics | Most victims already pay due to operational pressure; PQC may not yet increase revenue enough |
| Need for reliability | Ransomware groups prioritize stable monetization over cryptographic novelty |
| Ecosystem maturity | Tooling, libraries, and operator familiarity are still developing |

Therefore, my assessment is that **PQC is not yet the defining mainstream ransomware trend of 2025–2026; it is an emerging hardening technique with disproportionate future significance**. Agentic AI is changing campaigns now. PQC may change the irreversibility of successful encryption later.

## Combined Impact: Autonomous Intrusions Plus Cryptographically Hardened Payloads

The most dangerous scenario is the convergence of the two trends:

- Agentic AI automates victim discovery, intrusion, adaptation, and extortion.
- PQC hardens key management, reducing any residual hope of post-incident cryptographic recovery.

In that model, ransomware becomes both **faster to deploy** and **harder to undo**. Even if defenders improve detection, any missed intrusion may have more severe consequences because the payload’s encryption architecture is future-resistant.

## Defensive Implications

### Detection and Resilience Priorities

Defenders should reorient around these realities:

1. **Prioritize identity, access, and behavior monitoring over static malware signatures.**
2. **Assume phishing will become highly personalized and linguistically polished.**
3. **Prepare for extortion-only incidents, not just encryption events.**
4. **Track cryptographic libraries and anomalies in malware reverse engineering.**
5. **Invest in segmented, immutable, offline recovery strategies rather than hoping for decryptors.**

### Social Engineering Countermeasures

Given the rise of AI-generated personalization:

- implement role-based phishing-resistant MFA,
- harden help-desk verification processes,
- monitor for business-contextual phishing rather than only generic indicators,
- train executives and finance teams on targeted AI-enabled pretexts.

### Preparing for PQC Misuse

ENISA’s work implies that defenders should already understand PQC integration challenges. Security teams should:

- inventory cryptographic dependencies,
- monitor malware for use of PQC libraries or unusual key sizes,
- ensure incident response teams can recognize hybrid cryptosystems,
- and abandon any planning assumption that future cryptanalysis might rescue encrypted assets.

## Analytical Judgment

My concrete assessment is as follows:

1. **The most operationally significant ransomware innovation in 2025–2026 is Agentic AI, not a new encryptor primitive.** The evidence from ISACA strongly supports the view that AI is transforming malware behavior, code generation, social engineering, and negotiation. This lowers skill barriers, increases campaign throughput, and enhances extortion efficiency now. ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups))

2. **The shift away from full encryption toward data-only extortion and slow encryption is a rational optimization, not a decline in threat severity.** In fact, it often increases operational stealth while preserving coercive power. ([in-text citation](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2026/ai-driven-ransomware-fuels-rise-in-new-cyberthreat-groups))

3. **Post-quantum cryptography in ransomware is not yet evidenced here as widespread, but it is strategically credible and potentially transformative.** ENISA’s material makes clear that PQC integration is technically feasible and increasingly relevant. If threat actors adopt ML-KEM/Kyber1024 or similar mechanisms in production ransomware, incident recovery will become even more dependent on prevention, isolation, and backups rather than technical decryption hope. ([in-text citation](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/news/enisa-news/post-quantum-cryptography-anticipating-threats-and-preparing-the-future))

4. **The idea that decryption becomes “permanently impossible” should be stated carefully.** Absolute impossibility is not a scientific claim that should be made lightly. The stronger and more defensible conclusion is that **properly implemented PQC can make unauthorized recovery dramatically less feasible both now and under future quantum-era assumptions**.

## Conclusion

Ransomware in 2025–2026 is evolving from a malware category into an intelligent extortion system. ISACA’s reporting shows a threat landscape where new groups are emerging, encryption-heavy tactics are being selectively replaced by more efficient coercion models, and AI is entering both malware execution and criminal operations management. The most immediate danger is Agentic AI: it enables dynamic payload generation, real-time code transformation, highly personalized phishing, and scalable AI-assisted negotiation. This combination can realistically automate the majority of operational steps around a ransomware campaign, even if not every lateral movement or exploitation decision is fully autonomous.

Post-quantum cryptography is the second major trend, but it is better understood as an emerging strategic risk than a fully mainstream criminal standard. ENISA’s work demonstrates that PQC integration into real-world systems is technically serious, architecturally disruptive, and already under active study. Those same properties make PQC attractive to ransomware authors who want to future-proof their key protection. If families such as the claimed PE32 implementation are indeed using ML-KEM/Kyber1024, then the industry should treat that not as a curiosity but as an early warning.

The decisive opinion from this review is that **Agentic AI is already reshaping ransomware economics and tradecraft in 2025–2026, while PQC is poised to reshape ransomware irreversibility next**. Organizations that continue preparing only for conventional encryptors will be defending yesterday’s threat model.
