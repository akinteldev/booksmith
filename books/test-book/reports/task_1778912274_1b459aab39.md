# Macro-Economic Structure and Market Consolidation of Ransomware-as-a-Service in Q1 2026

## Executive Assessment

The Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) economy in Q1 2026 showed a decisive shift from competitive fragmentation toward oligopolistic consolidation. The strongest evidence comes from Check Point Research: **2,122 organizations were posted on ransomware data leak sites (DLS) in Q1 2026**, the **second-highest Q1 on record**, while the **top 10 groups captured 71% of all victims**, up sharply from the fragmented conditions seen in Q3 2025 when the top 10 accounted for only 57% and the number of active groups peaked at 85. By Q1 2026, active groups had fallen to **71**, even though **21 new groups emerged**, most of them too small to replace disappearing mid-tier operators. This is the clearest signal that the market is no longer expanding through broad-based proliferation; it is consolidating around better-capitalized, more operationally mature brands ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).

My assessment is concrete: **Q1 2026 marks the transition of RaaS from a fragmented criminal gig economy into a more concentrated platform economy**, where a smaller number of operators increasingly resemble software firms, franchise managers, and access aggregators rather than traditional malware crews. This matters because consolidation does not reduce ransomware risk. It raises the average quality, resilience, speed, and monetization efficiency of attacks. Smaller groups disappeared, but their affiliates, access, tradecraft, and tooling did not; they were absorbed by stronger operators.

The two most important emblematic cases in this transition are **The Gentlemen** and **LockBit 5.0**. The Gentlemen represents **hyper-growth fueled by pre-positioned access and scalable operations**, while LockBit represents **post-disruption recovery through brand reconstruction, technical modernization, and geographic adaptation**. Parallel to this, the rise of **white-label ransomware offerings**—exemplified in the query by **DragonForce’s “RansomBay” model**—lowers the barrier to entry for non-technical criminals and expands the customer base for dominant criminal platforms. Combined with affiliate partnerships, initial access brokers, and infrastructure-sharing alliances, this is creating a more corporate and more dangerous ransomware market.

## Q1 2026 as a Market Turning Point: From Fragmentation to Concentration

### Core market statistics

Check Point’s Q1 2026 data establishes the structural shift clearly:

| Metric | Q1 2026 | Comparative Context |
|---|---:|---|
| Publicly posted ransomware victims | 2,122 | Second-highest Q1 on record |
| Average victims per month | 700+ | Minimal monthly fluctuation |
| Active ransomware DLS tracked | 70+ | Indicates high baseline market activity |
| Share of victims by top 10 groups | 71% | Up from 57% in Q3 2025 |
| Active groups | 71 | Down from peak of 85 in Q3 2025 |
| New groups in quarter | 21 | Most posted fewer than 10 victims |

([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/))

This is not a decline in ransomware. It is a decline in **competitive dispersion**. The market remained near historic highs in total volume while becoming more concentrated. In economic terms, Q1 2026 reflects **industry consolidation without demand destruction**. The attack market did not shrink; market share merely shifted toward stronger firms.

### Why fragmentation declined

Check Point attributes the shift to a familiar post-disruption dynamic: **law enforcement pressure, infrastructure disruption, and competition scatter smaller players; stronger groups survive, absorb displaced affiliates, and grow** ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). This mechanism is economically intuitive:

- **Smaller operators** have weaker infrastructure, lower trust, poorer decryption reliability, and less ability to withstand seizures or leaks.
- **Affiliates** are mobile labor. When a program collapses, they migrate to brands with better payout reliability and tooling.
- **Dominant operators** can absorb that labor because they maintain infrastructure, support, malware development, leak sites, and negotiation processes.

My opinion is that **the labor market for affiliates is now the decisive variable in RaaS consolidation**. It is not simply malware quality that determines winners; it is the ability to recruit, retain, and operationalize affiliates through better economics, more reliable infrastructure, and stronger brand legitimacy in underground markets.

## The Emerging Oligopoly: Qilin, The Gentlemen, Akira, and LockBit

### Victim concentration among leading brands

Check Point found that **Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit together accounted for 41% of all victims in Q1 2026** ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). Within that group:

| Group | Q1 2026 Public Victims | Market Significance |
|---|---:|---|
| Qilin | 338 | Most active group for third consecutive quarter |
| The Gentlemen | 166 | Breakout growth story |
| LockBit 5.0 | 163 | Major comeback after 2024 disruption |

([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/))

Qilin’s position is especially notable: Check Point states that **Qilin alone posted more victims than the bottom 50 groups combined** ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). That is classic oligopolistic dominance.

### Why dominant operators gain share

Check Point highlights an underappreciated aspect of consolidation: larger RaaS brands invest in **operational consistency**, including functional decryptors, because their business model depends on preserving the perception that payment leads to recovery. In fragmented markets, many transient groups lacked this incentive and sometimes shipped defective encryptors. Check Point cites Obscura’s bug that rendered files over 1 GB unrecoverable regardless of payment ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

This point is strategically important. Consolidation improves attacker “customer service,” which paradoxically strengthens extortion credibility. A concentrated market can therefore produce **higher payment propensity** even if the number of groups declines.

## The Gentlemen: A Case Study in Scale Through Access Inventory

### Growth metrics

The Gentlemen rose from **40 victims in Q4 2025 to 166 in Q1 2026**, a **315% increase**, reaching third place globally according to Check Point and reproduced by Industrial Cyber and Cybersecurity Insiders ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/), [6](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/)). Dark Reading adds that in the first five months of 2026, The Gentlemen had published data from **around 332 organizations**, and Check Point reportedly ranked it second globally for the year-to-date period ([5](https://www.darkreading.com/threat-intelligence/gentlemen-raas-gang-data-leak)).

### Business model characteristics

Check Point’s most revealing point is that The Gentlemen’s growth was fueled by **pre-positioned access at scale** rather than slow opportunistic intrusion. Cybersecurity Insiders, summarizing Check Point, describes this as a **stockpile of roughly 14,700 compromised FortiGate devices** ([6](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/)). Even if treated cautiously as a secondary-source figure, it aligns with Check Point’s explanation that the group operated with “a large inventory” of access, enabling attacks “immediately and in volume” ([2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)).

This is economically transformative. The Gentlemen is not just a ransomware crew; it behaves like an **inventory-driven attacker**. In legitimate business terms, it shortened the attack cycle by holding ready-made distribution channels. That reduces customer acquisition cost, increases throughput, and supports affiliate scaling.

### Geographic implications

The Gentlemen’s victim mix was unusual:

- **Only 13%** of publicly extorted victims were in the United States.
- The ecosystem average was **49.6%** U.S.-based victims.
- Activity clustered in **APAC and Latin America**, apparently following access availability rather than victim prestige ([2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)).

This is another sign of platformization. Mature operators increasingly optimize against **available access supply**, not merely target desirability. This lowers friction and increases operational tempo.

### Internal leak and organizational maturity

Dark Reading reports that an anonymous group breached The Gentlemen’s internal back-end database and exfiltrated **over 16 GB** of communications, tooling, and data, later offered for **$10,000 in Bitcoin** ([5](https://www.darkreading.com/threat-intelligence/gentlemen-raas-gang-data-leak)). The article also notes internal discussions about rival groups and interest in **code signing**, including lessons drawn from the Black Basta leak.

This suggests The Gentlemen had evolved beyond basic smash-and-grab activity into a learning organization that benchmarks competitors, studies tradecraft, and iterates its methods. That is exactly what one expects from a structured, dominant operator.

## LockBit 5.0: Brand Recovery and Strategic Repositioning

### Return to top-tier status

LockBit posted **163 victims in Q1 2026**, a **106% increase** from 79 in Q4 2025, re-entering the global top tier after major law enforcement disruption in 2024 ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).

### Platform modernization

Check Point reports that LockBit 5.0 launched on the RAMP forum in September 2025 with:

- **Multi-platform support** for Windows, Linux, and ESXi
- Enhanced **evasion and anti-analysis**
- Faster encryption
- Randomized **16-character file extensions**
- A required affiliate **Bitcoin deposit of approximately $500** ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/))

The affiliate deposit matters economically: it acts as a **screening and anti-fraud mechanism**, improving affiliate quality while reducing opportunistic abuse of the platform. In a consolidated market, such controls are signs of mature governance.

### Geographic diversification

Check Point found LockBit’s victim geography had shifted from heavy U.S. concentration to a more even spread across Europe, Latin America, and other regions, likely to reduce exposure to aggressive enforcement jurisdictions ([2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). Cybersecurity Insiders adds that its U.S. share fell from a historical 50%+ to **21.2%** ([6](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/)).

My judgment is that LockBit is no longer just rebuilding malware; it is rebuilding **jurisdictional risk management**. That is a hallmark of a more corporate criminal enterprise.

## White-Label Ransomware and the “RansomBay” Problem

### Conceptual significance

The query specifically asks about the **white-label ransomware phenomenon**, such as **DragonForce’s RansomBay**, which allows non-technical criminals to launch attacks under outsourced infrastructure and malware branding. While the provided source set does not include a primary RansomBay technical paper, the broader market pattern is strongly supported by the available materials.

Huntress describes ransomware industrialization as a three-step model:

1. Operators **develop** malware and even support services.
2. They **lease** tools to affiliates.
3. They **take a cut** of ransom payments ([7](https://www.huntress.com/ransomware-guide/ransomware-trends)).

This is already close to white-label SaaS. The distinction is that white-label ransomware pushes abstraction further: the buyer may not need meaningful technical skill, operational depth, or even malware development capacity. Instead, the platform handles these functions.

### Why white-label matters economically

White-label ransomware changes market structure in three ways:

| Effect | Market Consequence |
|---|---|
| Lowers technical barriers | Expands the pool of potential affiliates/customers |
| Standardizes tooling | Accelerates replication of successful TTPs |
| Centralizes infrastructure and support | Strengthens platform owners and deepens concentration |

This produces a paradox: white-label models may create more nominal “brands,” but they actually deepen backend concentration. In other words, **visible actor proliferation can mask infrastructure consolidation**.

### Corporate-style partnerships and cartelization

Huntress reports growing alliances and merger-like activity among major groups, citing partnerships and examples such as links between **LockBit, Qilin, and DragonForce**, infrastructure sharing, pooled servers and botnets, and cross-group data handling ([7](https://www.huntress.com/ransomware-guide/ransomware-trends)). Huntress also notes that if a victim refuses to pay LockBit, stolen data might be passed to DragonForce for publication, illustrating cooperative extortion supply chains ([7](https://www.huntress.com/ransomware-guide/ransomware-trends)).

Even if some of these claims are directional rather than exhaustively documented in the provided corpus, the strategic implication is credible: the ransomware economy is moving toward **partnership ecosystems**, where infrastructure, leakage channels, negotiation pressure, and affiliate access are increasingly modular and tradable.

My concrete opinion is that **white-label ransomware is the economic endpoint of RaaS consolidation**. Once a few dominant backend operators control malware development, leak infrastructure, support systems, and affiliate onboarding, the front-end criminal can become almost interchangeable. This is more dangerous than classic fragmentation because it combines low entry barriers with high backend capability.

## Mandiant’s Broader Strategic Context: Why Consolidation Increases Impact

M-Trends 2026 frames the larger operational environment: threat actors are compromising third-party SaaS vendors, stealing tokens and secrets, and pivoting into downstream environments for **large-scale data theft**. Mandiant also emphasizes that ransomware groups increasingly target **backup infrastructure, identity services, and virtualization management planes**, turning ransomware into **recovery denial** rather than simple encryption ([4](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/m-trends-2026)).

This broader context matters because consolidated operators are best positioned to exploit these techniques. Recovery denial requires:

- Better intrusion depth
- Broader technical capability
- More disciplined operations
- Better coordination across access, privilege, exfiltration, and encryption phases

That is why the move from fragmented crews to dominant operators likely means **fewer amateur mistakes and more enterprise-scale damage**.

## Analytical Conclusion

The macro-economics of RaaS in Q1 2026 are best understood as **criminal market consolidation under platform capitalism**. The data does not show a fading threat; it shows a maturing one.

Three conclusions are warranted:

1. **The ransomware market is consolidating into an oligopoly.**  
   With **71% of victims attributed to the top 10 groups**, and **Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit accounting for 41%**, the RaaS economy is concentrating around firms with stronger infrastructure, better affiliate management, and more resilient business processes ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)).

2. **The Gentlemen and LockBit exemplify two successful growth strategies.**  
   The Gentlemen scaled through **access inventory and attack throughput**; LockBit scaled through **brand rehabilitation, technical modernization, and geographic risk diversification** ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/), [5](https://www.darkreading.com/threat-intelligence/gentlemen-raas-gang-data-leak)).

3. **White-label ransomware will likely intensify consolidation, not reduce it.**  
   Services analogous to DragonForce’s “RansomBay” model lower barriers for non-technical actors while centralizing the profitable backend functions—malware, infrastructure, support, leakage, and negotiation—into fewer hands. This is the same economic logic that created dominant legitimate SaaS platforms. In cybercrime, it means more attackers can participate, but fewer operators capture most of the value.

In short, **Q1 2026 was not the quarter ransomware became smaller; it was the quarter ransomware became more corporate**.
