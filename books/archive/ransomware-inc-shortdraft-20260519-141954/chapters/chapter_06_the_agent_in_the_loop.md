# Chapter 6: The Agent in the Loop

It is 2:14 in the morning in the operator's time zone, and the workstation is the kind of workstation a moderately well-paid software engineer might own. Two monitors. A mechanical keyboard. A coffee cup on a coaster. The operator — an affiliate of one of the dominant ransomware platforms, sitting in an apartment in a city that, for the purposes of this scene, does not need a name — is doing what affiliates do at two in the morning. He is working a victim.

The intrusion happened nine hours earlier. The credentials, purchased that morning from one of the wholesalers, had worked on the first try. He had walked through the perimeter, harvested a few additional accounts, and pulled down the relevant parts of the victim's email server, file shares, and SharePoint exports — roughly four hundred gigabytes of corporate data — into a staging environment on the platform's infrastructure. By midnight, the data had been mirrored to a server in another country. By one o'clock, the platform's automation had begun extracting structured information from it.

The operator is now reading a summary that no human wrote. On the screen, in tidy bullet points, is the output of a large language model that has spent the last fifty minutes reading every email and document in the staged corpus. The model has identified the company's chief financial officer by name. It has identified her recent communications with two outside law firms about a pending litigation matter. It has identified the names and roles of every member of her finance team. It has identified the regulatory filings the company is required to make in the next ninety days, and the specific reporting deadlines associated with each. It has flagged a series of customer contracts that contain confidentiality clauses likely to trigger penalty payments if the contracts are publicly leaked. It has surfaced an internal personnel matter — a harassment complaint that had not yet been resolved — that the model has helpfully categorized as a high-leverage disclosure target. It has even drafted, in a tone the operator can tweak, an opening message to the CFO that references three specific facts from her recent emails and is calibrated to produce maximum urgency without producing immediate panic. The model is not autonomous. The operator is still in the chair. But the work the operator is doing tonight — the work of reading, sorting, prioritizing, framing, and weaponizing four hundred gigabytes of stolen text — would have taken a human analyst three days. The model has done it in fifty minutes. The operator is going to be done with this victim before sunrise.

This is what AI in ransomware actually looks like in 2026.

It does not look like a robot. It does not look like a self-aware program autonomously deciding which company to destroy next. It does not look like the cinematic version of "AI hacking" that the public press has been writing about with increasing frequency since the consumer-grade large language models became widely available. It looks like a tired criminal, in front of two monitors, using a productivity tool. The tool happens to be very, very good at the parts of the criminal's job that used to take the most time. The criminal is now spending those hours sleeping instead. Or running another victim. Or both.

The 2025–2026 reporting from Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42, Trend Micro, ITBrief, and SecurityWeek converges on a single observation that the rest of this chapter will explore: the current evidence supports the use of large language models as assistants to ransomware operators, not yet as fully autonomous agents replacing them. The forecasts for what happens by mid-2026 and beyond are more aggressive — multi-agent systems capable of chaining reconnaissance, vulnerability discovery, credential attacks, payload adaptation, lateral-movement support, and exfiltration into a single orchestrated pipeline. SecurityWeek's 2026 outlook predicted that at least one major enterprise breach would be materially advanced by autonomous agentic systems by the middle of the year. But the report and its peers are careful, and the calibration matters. Most of what is verifiable today is AI-assisted, not AI-autonomous. The distinction is real, and the rest of this chapter is going to honor it.

What has changed, even at the assisted end of the spectrum, is the economics of running an attack.

Unit 42's analysts, who run one of the largest incident-response practices in the world, captured the shift in a single sentence that should be read carefully by anyone trying to understand why ransomware volume continues to scale even as the number of skilled criminals available to do the work has not meaningfully grown: the operator time required to run an attack at scale is dropping. Read that again. The operator time required to run an attack at scale is dropping. Not the time required to write the malware. Not the time required to break the cryptography. The operator time. The number of hours a human criminal must personally spend on each victim to convert that victim into a successful extortion. That number is collapsing. The collapse is the product. AI is not making the malware smarter. AI is making the human in the chair more productive. The same operator who in 2022 might have managed three or four simultaneous victims can now manage twelve, fifteen, twenty. The same affiliate workforce that produced four thousand leak-site posts a year can produce seven thousand. The same dominant brand that posted three hundred and thirty-eight victims in the first quarter of 2026 can, in principle, post twice that in the first quarter of 2027, without hiring a single additional human criminal.

This is the multiplier that the operator economy has been waiting for. It is the multiplier that Unit 42, Trend Micro, ITBrief, and SecurityWeek have all begun, in cautious language, to describe as the structural feature of the next phase of the industry. Trend Micro's 2026 forecast described ransomware as becoming faster, more persistent, and more automated — with AI managing the scanning of target environments, the support of exploitation when access has been gained, the prioritization of victims based on which ones are most likely to pay, and the orchestration of extortion workflows after the data has been stolen. ITBrief's 2026 outlook went further, predicting that AI-powered cybercrime would become, in the firm's words, fully automated within the year. SecurityWeek framed agentic AI as the connective layer of the modern ransomware operation: the soft tissue between the components that used to require a human to drag and drop the pieces from one stage to the next. The forecasts are forecasts. The trajectory they describe is, however, already visible in the assisted version of the work that the operator at his workstation is doing tonight.

What allows the multiplier to function is a parallel architectural shift in how ransomware payloads are built. For a long time, the public discussion of ransomware focused on the encryptor — the piece of malware that scrambled the victim's files. The encryptor was the malware. The malware was the attack. The 2025–2026 reporting describes a different shape entirely. The modern ransomware payload is not one piece of malware. It is a system. The same delivery package that lands inside the victim's environment includes automation logic for several distinct functions: staging the data for exfiltration, executing with privilege awareness so that the malware behaves differently depending on where it has landed, impairing whatever defensive software is running on the host, triaging the data to identify what is worth stealing, targeting cloud storage and SaaS resources alongside on-premises files, preparing the leak site with appropriate previews of stolen content, and queuing up the extortion communications that will be sent to the victim once encryption begins. Each function is a module. Each module can be updated independently. Each module can, increasingly, be parameterized and operated by an automation layer rather than by the human who originally pressed go. The payload, in 2026, is the platform — a small piece of distributed software that arrives in the victim's environment already preconfigured to run the rest of the operation on its own.

The report literature describes this modular architecture as having roughly four roles. Cryptographic denial — the part that scrambles files and demands a ransom for the key. Data leverage — the part that prepares the stolen data for leak-site display and subsequent disclosure. Defense impairment — the part that disables the endpoint detection and visibility tools that would otherwise interrupt the attack. Extortion operations — the part that handles communications with the victim, the negotiation interfaces, the countdown clocks, the harassment scripts, and the public-pressure choreography. Four modules. Each one is a different category of malware-engineering work. Each one is being increasingly orchestrated by software rather than by humans. The operator at his workstation tonight did not have to write any of it. He purchased a payload from the platform. The payload arrived already configured. He is, at this point, mostly making aesthetic decisions about how the negotiation opens.

Alongside this modular architecture has come a parallel expansion in what the operators target. The image most readers carry of ransomware is still an image of a Windows desktop with a ransom note splashed across the wallpaper. The image is now obsolete. 2025–2026 deployment plans consistently include Linux servers, virtualization hypervisors, network-attached storage devices, cloud storage buckets, identity platforms, backup systems, and the environments of managed service providers — the companies that other companies hire to run their IT. The platform expansion is not technical curiosity. It is strategic targeting. A Linux server runs the database that the Windows desktop depends on. A hypervisor runs the virtualized environment in which both of them live. A cloud storage bucket holds the data the entire company is built on. A managed service provider holds the keys to dozens or hundreds of its customers. Each new target class is a multiplier on the leverage the operator can produce from a single intrusion. The expansion of the target surface is what makes the assisted operator's productivity gains turn into real coercive power. A single affiliate, working overnight, with an AI assistant prioritizing his targets, can now run an operation against an MSP and, through that MSP, simultaneously breach forty downstream customers. The operator does not need to be skilled. He needs to be at the workstation while the AI does the routing.

There is one further wrinkle in the modular payload architecture that deserves the reader's attention. Some of the most aggressive ransomware operations no longer use the cryptographic-denial module at all. The cloud-extortion variant of a modern ransomware attack often does not encrypt anything. It abuses the victim's own cloud credentials. It uses administrative APIs to delete cloud snapshots that would otherwise enable restoration. It exposes storage buckets containing sensitive data to the public internet. It exfiltrates the victim's SaaS contents — emails, documents, CRM records, customer databases — and threatens to leak them. The leverage is identical to the encryption-based variant, but no malware ever runs in the victim's environment in the traditional sense. The attack is, from the victim's perspective, a legitimate-looking series of administrative actions performed inside the victim's own cloud account by an attacker who has the credentials to authenticate as a trusted user. The modules that remain — data leverage, defense impairment, extortion operations — are sufficient. The encryptor is now optional.

What all of this means for defenders is that the framing they have been operating under for most of the last decade has aged out of usefulness. The traditional question — how do we keep ransomware out? — assumes that the attack is a discrete event with a clear beginning and an identifiable malware artifact. The 2026 attack is not that shape. The attack is a workflow. It has many parts, only some of which are visible as malicious. It is increasingly driven by automation that has been parameterized for the specific victim by software the defender will never see. The window in which the defender might have detected the intrusion has, in many of the cases Unit 42 analyzed, closed by the time anyone realized something had begun. The 86% of 2025 incidents that Unit 42 categorized as involving business disruption — meaning the victim lost the ability to operate for some measurable period of time — were not, mostly, technical failures of detection. They were structural failures of pace. The defenders could not move as fast as the workflow that was attacking them, because the workflow no longer needed a human in the loop for every step. The human was needed for the strategic decisions. The automation was running the operation.

This is the part of the AI shift that the operator economy has, internally, been celebrating. Public press coverage of AI in cybercrime tends to focus on the most spectacular possibilities: autonomous malware, self-spreading agents, machine-versus-machine combat. The reality, in the available reporting, is more mundane and more consequential. The reality is that the affiliate at the workstation tonight is a more productive criminal than the affiliate at the same workstation was two years ago. He is moving faster. He is running more victims. He is converting access into extortion at a tempo that the defensive industry has not yet figured out how to match. The economic engine of the entire ransomware market — the rate at which affiliate labor converts into successful coercion — has been quietly upgraded. The dominant ransomware brands are now competing not only on payouts and infrastructure, as the earlier chapters described, but on the quality of the AI tooling they put in their affiliates' hands. The brand that gives its affiliates the best reconnaissance assistant, the best message templating system, the best target prioritization model, will, all else equal, attract the affiliates who can produce the most successful campaigns. The next phase of the platform competition is going to be a tooling competition. The 2026 reporting suggests that it has already begun.

What the operator finishes with, when the sun starts to come up and he sends the prepared message to the CFO of the company whose four hundred gigabytes he has spent the night reading, is a chat window. The window opens on a portal hosted by his platform. The chat begins. The conversation that follows is the subject of Part III of this book. But the first message of that conversation — the message the CFO will read in the parking lot of her office building, or on her phone over breakfast, or in her car as she drives in to work — was not written by him. It was drafted by the model. It contained three specific facts from her recent emails. It was calibrated to produce maximum urgency without producing immediate panic. The tone was clean, professional, and almost friendly. It read, in the way the prose of the best customer-service representatives reads, like the voice of someone who has done this before and is going to walk her through it.

In a sense, it had. The voice was not the operator's voice. The voice belonged to no one in particular. The voice was the AI's. And the next time the CFO heard a voice like it, the next chapter is going to argue, may not be in a chat window at all. It may be on the phone.
