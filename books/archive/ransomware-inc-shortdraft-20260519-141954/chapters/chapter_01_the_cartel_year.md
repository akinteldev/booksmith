# Chapter 1: The Cartel Year

The IT director found out the way most of them find out. A colleague messaged her at 7:14 a.m., before she had finished her first cup of coffee, with a screenshot and a single sentence: *Is this us?*

It was a leak site. Black background, gothic font, a countdown clock running in the upper-right corner. Beside the clock was her company's name, the name on her LinkedIn profile, the name on her badge, the name on the welcome sign in the lobby of the small manufacturing plant where she had worked for nineteen years. Below the name was a brief description of what the group claimed to have taken: customer records, engineering files, financial documents. Below that, a sample. A folder tree, screenshots of a few PDFs, the unmistakable layout of her CFO's invoice template. And below that, a number. A demand.

She did not yet understand that her company was now a product. She did not yet understand that the website she was staring at was a storefront in a market with thousands of items in inventory, that her name had been added to a public catalog scrolling through corporate logos from forty different countries, that the countdown clock was a marketing device tuned by people who had run hundreds of these auctions before. She thought, in that first moment, that she had been singled out. That the gang on the other side of the screen had chosen her. That this was personal.

It was not personal. It was retail.

In the first three months of 2026, ransomware operators publicly listed 2,122 organizations on data-leak sites. That number is not a body count. It is closer to a sales report. Each name on each site represents a coercive transaction in flight: an intrusion that has been completed, files that have been stolen or encrypted, a negotiation that is open, a customer whose decision to pay or not pay determines what happens next. Check Point Research, the team that compiles the figure every quarter from more than seventy criminal leak sites, called it the second-highest first quarter on record. Brandefense, working from a different dataset, counted 2,135 incidents in the same period — close enough to confirm that the volume is not a vendor artifact. Cyble's annual tally for 2025 ran to roughly 6,500 incidents. Broadcom's Symantec unit, counting both encryption cases and pure data-theft extortion, recorded 6,182 attacks in 2025, a twenty-three percent jump from the year before. Check Point's full-year figure for 2025 — 7,960 organizations publicly listed on double-extortion leak sites, fifty-three percent more than 2024 — is the cleanest summary statistic the industry has. None of these numbers are perfect. Several of them are counting different things. They all describe the same underlying reality: a market.

The market has consolidated.

This is the single most important fact about ransomware in 2026, and it is the fact most likely to surprise a reader who still imagines digital extortion as the work of scattered teenagers, foreign gangs, lone-wolf operators with grudges and grievances. In Q1 2026, the top ten ransomware brands accounted for roughly seventy-one percent of every organization listed on a leak site anywhere in the world. Just four of them — Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit — accounted for forty-one percent. Three months earlier, in Q3 2025, the top ten's share had been fifty-seven percent. The market was wider, the players were more numerous, and the long tail of small criminal brands was longer. By winter of 2026, that long tail had collapsed. Twenty-one new groups appeared during the quarter; most of them posted fewer than ten victims. The number of actively named brands fell from a peak of eighty-five to seventy-one.

Translate the percentages into something the eye can hold. Out of every hundred companies whose name appeared on a leak site somewhere in the first quarter of 2026, seventy-one were extorted by an operation that had survived everything thrown at it for the previous twelve months. Forty-one were extorted by one of the same four operations. Two-thirds of the year's named victims came from ten companies that do not legally exist.

The dominant brand of the year is Qilin. For three consecutive quarters now, no other operator has posted more victims. In Q1 2026 alone, Qilin's leak site listed 338 organizations. That single brand outpaced the combined output of the bottom fifty ransomware operations on the planet. Across all of 2025, Qilin's monthly tempo grew from roughly thirty-five new victims a month at the start of the year to more than one hundred and fifty a month by the end of it, and the operator's leak site published the names of more than a thousand companies whose negotiators refused to pay. Beside Qilin sits Akira, whose Q1 share — derived from the top-four share, since precise standalone figures vary by source — works out to roughly two hundred victims a quarter, paired with reported total proceeds in the neighborhood of two hundred and forty-four million dollars. AhnLab's 2025 dataset, counting differently, ranks Akira first with 622 incidents and Qilin second with 589; the exact rank depends on which leak site is monitored and how duplicates are reconciled. The signal is consistent: two operations are running so far ahead of the field that the field stopped being a field.

Behind them, the surprise of the quarter: a brand called The Gentlemen, which had posted forty victims in the last three months of 2025 and one hundred and sixty-six in the first three months of 2026, a more than fourfold expansion in ninety days. According to industry reporting, the explanation was not technical brilliance. It was inventory. The group had quietly accumulated access to roughly fourteen thousand seven hundred compromised FortiGate firewall appliances — internet-facing security devices belonging to other people's companies, sitting in racks in other people's data centers, already broken into and waiting. When the operation was ready to scale, it did not need to find victims one at a time. It needed only to walk down the warehouse aisle and start picking boxes off the shelf.

And then, fourth in the cluster, the brand that should not be there at all: LockBit. In early 2024, an international law-enforcement coalition called Operation Cronos seized LockBit's infrastructure, leaked its inner files, identified the man behind the brand, and pinned a wanted notice to the front page of its own site. By the middle of 2025, threat-intelligence firms were reporting that LockBit accounted for about one percent of observed ransomware activity, roughly forty-two incidents, and that the brand was struggling to attract affiliates who had moved on to operators that were not under active law-enforcement pressure. The obituary had been written. Then, in September 2025, LockBit 5.0 went live. New malware, capable of attacking Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi virtualization hosts. New evasion routines. Randomized file extensions. A new affiliate-onboarding rule requiring an entry deposit of around five hundred dollars in Bitcoin — a friction tax designed to filter out tourists. Permission, for the first time in the brand's recent history, to target critical-infrastructure sectors that had previously been politely off limits. And, in the first three months of 2026, 163 fresh victims posted to the relaunched leak site. A brand presumed dead. A brand walking. A brand back in the top four.

The pattern is the story.

For more than a decade, public conversation about ransomware has been organized around the wrong noun. Reporters and politicians have framed it as a malware problem, then as a gang problem, then as a Russia problem. Each frame captures a piece of the truth and misses the larger shape. The shape that the 2025–2026 data makes clear is that ransomware is now a market. Like every consolidating market on Earth, it follows the basic physics of platform economics. Demand pressure (victims who can pay, victims who must pay, sectors with downtime costs measured in millions of dollars per hour) flows toward the operators who can convert that pressure most reliably. Supply pressure (criminals with intrusion skill, criminals with access to credentials, criminals with negotiation experience, criminals with no skills at all who simply want a cut of someone else's score) flows toward the platforms that offer the best combination of payout, reliability, infrastructure, and reputation. Brands rise. Brands fall. Brands re-emerge. The capability beneath them — the labor, the access, the tooling, the leak sites, the negotiation panels, the cryptocurrency rails — does not vanish when a brand is taken down. It reaggregates around whoever is still standing.

The clearest demonstration is what happened to a brand called RansomHub. In April 2025, with no public warning, RansomHub went offline. The operator had spent more than a year building one of the most active ransomware platforms in the world, posting more than seven hundred and sixty victims to its leak site, offering its affiliates a reported ninety percent commission — well above market rate — and aggressively recruiting from rival operations. And then, one day, the site stopped responding. Whether the cause was an exit scam, an internal dispute, an unannounced enforcement action, or a strategic re-brand, the public reporting has not definitively said. What it has said is that within days, recruiting threads from Qilin, DragonForce, and a smaller brand called Lynx appeared in the criminal forums where displaced affiliates lived. The labor moved. Vendor telemetry from Broadcom's Symantec unit later confirmed that Akira and Qilin each accounted for roughly sixteen percent of the year's claimed ransomware attacks, much of that share inherited from operators who had been knocked offline or had walked away. Affiliates did not retire. They re-employed. The capacity that had once flown the RansomHub flag was, within weeks, flying somebody else's. The labor market is older and more durable than any of the brands attached to it.

This is what investigators inside the industry mean when they describe ransomware's defining dynamic as creative destruction. A brand falls and the capability inside it disperses, lifting whatever survives. Operation Cronos disrupted LockBit, and Qilin grew. RansomHub disappeared, and DragonForce, Akira, and Qilin grew. The takedowns are not failures. They impose real costs on real operators, they degrade infrastructure, they extract intelligence, they end the careers of specific human beings. But they do not reduce systemic risk in the way that a casual reader of the headlines would expect, because the system is not the brand. The system is the supply chain underneath it.

A second piece of this story is worth holding close, because it will return throughout this book: in 2026, encryption is no longer the point.

For the first half of ransomware's modern history, the threat was simple. A criminal got into a network, deployed a payload, scrambled the files, and held the decryption key hostage until somebody paid. The pressure mechanism was operational paralysis: your data is locked, your business stops, pay or die. That mechanism still exists. It is not, however, the only one. Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42 examined hundreds of ransomware-extortion cases in 2025 and found that encryption was present in seventy-eight percent of them, down from ninety-two percent in 2024, and ninety-six percent in 2021. Data theft was present in fifty-seven percent. Harassment of victims, their employees, their customers, their families was present in ten percent — the same year-on-year category that had appeared in only five percent of cases just four years earlier. Eighty-six percent of all the incidents the firm examined caused business disruption of some kind. The pressure mechanisms have multiplied. The lockbox is one tool in a much larger toolkit, and operators select among them depending on what the victim is, what the victim can be threatened with, and what the victim is likely to pay to make a particular threat go away. Encryption is now optional. Leverage is the product.

That is the third shift hiding inside the Q1 2026 numbers. The reason ransomware can post record volumes while payment rates fall to historic lows — coverage firm Coveware put the Q3 2025 payment rate at twenty-three percent, the lowest the industry has ever measured — is that operators no longer need any individual victim to pay. They need volume. They need leverage. They need the kind of operation that can run two thousand simultaneous coercion campaigns and accept that the majority of them will produce nothing, because the minority that succeed will produce enough. GuidePoint Security's analysts looked at Qilin's tempo and warned, repeatedly and in print, that high posting volume should not be confused with high revenue. The brand was running, in effect, the criminal equivalent of a high-volume, low-conversion sales funnel. So was every operator at the top of the leaderboard. Their goal was not to extort the IT director in the manufacturing plant whose morning had just collapsed. Their goal was to extort somebody, anybody, on the list of two thousand and rising.

By the time DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin began floating language about a coordinated cartel in the back half of 2025 — non-aggression among brands, shared infrastructure, "equal competition conditions," a posture more familiar from textbook descriptions of organized crime in physical markets than from the chaotic gang-on-gang behavior that ransomware used to display — defenders and intelligence analysts were already moving past the question of whether the alliance was operationally real. ReliaQuest's analysts, cited in the trade press, were skeptical that joint operations had actually materialized. ZeroFox assessed that the rumored coalition was mostly a signaling exercise, designed to help LockBit borrow credibility from healthier brands. CXO Today reported the announcement and noted, plainly, that no joint operations had been observed. The cartel as a press release was real. The cartel as a functioning institution was, for now, theater.

But the theater itself was diagnostic. Operators competing for affiliates were beginning to use the language of corporate strategy: "market conditions," "competition," "alliances," "channel partners," "service quality." They had built recruitment portals. They were advertising commission structures. They were issuing press statements. They were, in every observable way, behaving like a consolidating industry — and the rest of this book is the story of what that industry actually does for a living.

What the IT director in the manufacturing plant did not yet understand, on the morning she opened her phone and saw the leak-site screenshot, is that she was not the target. She was a unit of inventory. The criminals on the other side of the chat window were not visionaries or geniuses or villains; they were salespeople, working a pipeline, with quotas to meet and a brand to protect. They had a name. They had infrastructure. They had a website with a logo and a customer-service channel and a countdown timer that had been A/B tested across thousands of previous campaigns. They had a parent organization that took a cut. They had labor partners who had been recruited away from a brand that had collapsed six months earlier. They had access wholesalers who supplied them with the keys to her network for prices set by a different market that the next chapter will describe. They had everything a Fortune 500 services company has, except a tax bill.

The shock that morning, the one in her stomach as she sat in her kitchen and watched the countdown clock advance second by second, was the shock of meeting an institution she had not known existed.

It exists. It has been hiring. And the door it walked through to get to her was not a door at all. It was a market, and the market is open every day.
