# Chapter 3: The Wholesalers

The salesman did not notice anything had happened. He was tired, sitting in a hotel room in a city he did not particularly like, with a fast-approaching deadline and a slow laptop and a deadline-relevant document trapped behind a paywall. He found the paywall annoying. He found a website that promised, in the cheerful and slightly broken English of a hundred similar websites, a free download of the productivity software he needed to open the file. The installer had an icon that looked right. The dialog box asked for administrator permission. He gave it. The installer ran. A second window briefly appeared and disappeared. The software opened. He worked for two more hours, and then he went to dinner.

He did not see what had happened in the second window. He did not see that the installer had unpacked an additional executable into a temporary directory and run it once. He did not see the small process scanning the registry hives of his browsers, decrypting the saved-password databases that Chrome and Edge use to autofill his work logins. He did not see it copy the cookie store, the one that contained the active session token his employer's Microsoft account had handed him after he'd authenticated with multi-factor authentication earlier that morning. He did not see it walk through the file system harvesting any document that contained the word "password" or "credentials" or a recognizable VPN-config extension. He did not see it open a connection to a server in another country and upload everything it had found in a compressed bundle of a few megabytes. By the time he came back from dinner, the process had exited cleanly. The bundle had been sorted, parsed, and indexed by an automated pipeline on the other end. The browser passwords had been extracted to a text file. The session cookies had been mapped to the domains they unlocked. The VPN configuration had been flagged for resale. Within an hour, a new listing appeared on a criminal marketplace where buyers were already filtering for exactly his type of access. The salesman, finishing his second beer, was now an item of inventory.

He had no idea. He would not have an idea for weeks. By the time he found out, his company would be on a leak site.

This is what investigators inside the access economy call infostealer logs. Each "log" is the harvested contents of a single infected device — the passwords saved in the browser, the cookies that prove the user is already logged in to a particular site, the local files that look like credentials, the system metadata that helps a buyer evaluate the prize. The metaphor that fits is something like a stolen wallet. Not just a wallet with one credit card in it; a wallet with every credit card the victim has ever carried, every ID badge, every key, every loyalty card, every photograph of his children, every receipt that proves he buys gas at a specific station every Tuesday. A buyer can use the contents to be him for an hour, a day, a week. The session cookies, in particular, are the most dangerous element. A session cookie is a hotel keycard already activated, already inserted in the lock, already turned: the buyer does not need the victim's password, does not need the victim's second factor, does not need to fool the bank's authentication system. He can simply walk through the door that the victim has already opened.

Reporting from Lyrie Research, citing data from the threat-intelligence firm Flare, sketches the scale of this market. Sixteen percent of all infostealer-infected endpoints in 2025 contained enterprise single-sign-on credentials — corporate keys, not personal ones — up from six percent the year before. Seventy-nine percent of the logs contained Microsoft Entra ID credentials, which is the modern cloud identity system that controls who can log into a company's email, file shares, virtual servers, and security tooling. One million one hundred and seventy thousand logs, the same report estimated, contained credentials paired with valid session cookies — the keycards-already-turned — enabling buyers to bypass multi-factor authentication entirely. In the world of corporate cybersecurity, the deployment of multi-factor authentication has been treated, for the better part of a decade, as the single most important defensive measure a company can take. The infostealer economy has been quietly making it obsolete one stolen browser at a time.

The infostealers are only the harvesting layer. Above them sits a second layer, and this is where the supply chain begins to resemble a real one.

The people who buy raw infostealer logs and turn them into something usable are called Initial Access Brokers, or IABs. The English word for what they do is wholesaler. They purchase logs in bulk from infostealer operators and re-organize them: by company, by industry, by privilege level, by geography. They confirm the credentials still work. They check whether multi-factor authentication has been bypassed. They probe the corporate network behind the credentials to figure out what the buyer is actually getting — domain administrator? Local user? Remote-desktop session? VPN access? Citrix? They test the persistence: will the access still work in three days, when the buyer is ready to use it? They write the listing the way an estate agent writes a listing, with photographs and a description and a price.

A 2026 defensive playbook from Lyrie Research, summarizing prices across the underground market, found access selling for as little as twenty dollars at the low end — a single low-privilege credential to a small business, often a junk listing — and more than ten thousand dollars at the high end, for validated domain-admin-level access to a real corporate network with confirmed persistence. The spread describes not only the value of the privilege but the labor that went into validating it. The cheap listing is a credential pulled out of a log and put on the shelf. The expensive listing is a credential a broker has personally tested, expanded, mapped, and packaged. A buyer paying ten thousand dollars is not buying a key. He is buying confidence that the key works, the door is real, the alarm has been figured out, and the layout of the building has been sketched in pencil for him on the back of the listing. Lyrie's analysts called IABs the "invisible toll booth" between phishing and ransomware. The phrase is exact. Every successful ransomware attack passes through a tollbooth like this. The ransomware operator does not staff it. He buys his way past.

The Change Healthcare attack of 2024 — the one that shut down United States health-care insurance payments for weeks and produced a reported twenty-two-million-dollar ransom payment — is, in the available reporting, a textbook example of how this works. Hudson Rock's analysts examined what they could observe and concluded that the initial entry point was almost certainly a compromised Citrix remote-access account belonging to an employee whose personal device had previously been infected with infostealer malware. The Citrix account was not protected by multi-factor authentication. The infostealer harvested the credentials. The credentials were sold. The buyer used them to walk in. None of the steps in that chain required the buyer to "hack" anything in the conventional sense. The single point of failure that produced one of the largest health-care cyber events in American history was a remote-access credential, on a single employee's machine, on a single afternoon, sold to a stranger for an amount of money that nobody bothered to publish.

The ransomware affiliates downstream of the access market do not bother to break in either. They buy in.

This is the single most important fact about how modern intrusions actually work, and it is the fact most often misunderstood by everyone outside the security industry. The mental picture most people carry of a ransomware attack — hooded figure typing furiously, defeating a firewall, breaking through layers of digital armor — is closer to a 1990s film than to 2026. The reality is duller and more upsetting. The reality, captured in Mandiant's 2025 incident-response data, is that the leading initial-infection vectors for ransomware were exploitation of unpatched internet-facing systems, stolen credentials, and email phishing — in roughly that order. The reality, captured in Cisco Talos's Q1 2026 incident-response engagements, is that phishing has reemerged as the top initial-access vector in more than a third of cases, because the phishing emails are written and styled and delivered with a precision the older toolkits could not approach. The reality, in plain language, is that attackers increasingly log in. They do not break in.

The labor economics of the underground are reorganized around this fact. A ransomware affiliate working under one of the franchise platforms described in the previous chapter does not need to be an elite intrusion specialist. He needs to be a competent operator who can read the contents of a credential package, log into the indicated system, locate the data of interest, exfiltrate it, position the encryption payload, and run it. The hardest step — getting in — has been done for him by somebody else, in another part of the world, for a fee that may be a single-digit percentage of the eventual ransom. The middle of the kill chain has been bought, packaged, and delivered like any other commodity input.

The consequence is speed. Lyrie Research's 2026 playbook described the timeline from access purchase to ransomware deployment as having compressed to under forty-eight hours for some activity in 2026, down from the seventy-one-day average that earlier-decade analysis of the same supply chain had observed. Forty-eight hours. From the moment a credential leaves the broker's marketplace, an affiliate may load it, validate it, move laterally, identify the highest-value file shares, harvest data, position payload, and detonate the encryption — all of it inside a window of time short enough that most enterprise security teams will not have completed their morning ticket queue. The infostealer that produced the credential may have been running quietly on the employee's home laptop for weeks, but the moment the access is sold, the clock starts. Forty-eight hours, in some cases, between the wholesale transaction and the leak-site post.

That speed is the reason the public statistics about pre-ransomware engagements have shifted. Cisco Talos, in the same Q1 2026 dataset, reported that incidents detected during the pre-ransomware phase of an intrusion — the period after compromise but before payload deployment, the window during which a defender can still kick the attacker out — had fallen to eighteen percent of engagements. A year earlier, the same firm had measured that share at fifty percent. Half of all engagements had been detected before encryption. Now, only one in five. The decline does not mean defenders have gotten worse. It means that the operators have gotten better at skipping over the parts of the intrusion that defenders had learned to detect. Buying validated access from a wholesaler eliminates exactly the loud, noisy, scannable activity that older ransomware attacks generated on the way in. The new attack is quiet because somebody else, weeks earlier, made all the noise. By the time the affiliate logs in, he is using credentials that look legitimate, sessions that look pre-authenticated, devices that look familiar. He does not have to be invisible. He has to look like the man whose laptop he is borrowing.

The supply chain has costs and beneficiaries. Each link adds value, takes a margin, and reduces the skill burden on the link above it. The infostealer operator collects logs and sells them to validators and resellers. The validator confirms the credentials and packages them. The IAB lists the access and matches it to buyers. The affiliate purchases, intrudes, and pulls data. The ransomware platform — the franchise from the previous chapter — provides the tooling, the leak site, the negotiation infrastructure, the brand. Each role is filled by a different person, in a different country, often unknown to the others, paid in cryptocurrency, replaceable within days. None of them needs to be a master criminal. The chain is doing the criminal mastering for them.

This is the structural shift that makes the modern ransomware economy so resilient and so hard to disrupt. Attack the franchise at the top and you remove a brand; the labor, the access, and the infrastructure are still in inventory. Attack the affiliate in the middle and you arrest one operator; the credentials he was using were bought from a wholesaler who is still in business. Attack the wholesaler and you break one stall in a market with hundreds of stalls. Attack the infostealer operator who supplies the wholesalers and you have to find him among the millions of infected endpoints whose data is feeding the same supply, every hour, around the world. The economy was designed, mostly by accident and mostly through commercial competition between criminal entrepreneurs, to be exactly the shape it is: a deep, layered, partitioned market of specialists, none of whom can be removed quickly enough to break the supply of access to the operators who buy at the top.

Inside this market, there are defensive opportunities, and the analysts at BreachSense have made an explicit case for them. Between the moment an infostealer log appears on a marketplace and the moment a ransomware operator detonates inside the company that log belongs to, there is a window — days, sometimes weeks, sometimes months. During that window, the credentials are quietly listed for sale, traded among buyers, validated by brokers, and eventually purchased by an affiliate. The window is defensive territory. If a company can monitor infostealer marketplaces for its own credentials, force a session invalidation when a token appears, rotate the affected accounts, and tighten the conditional-access policy on the affected endpoints, the access can be killed before the buyer uses it. This is a real defensive lever, and a small industry has grown up around it. But the window depends on visibility, and visibility costs money, and the security teams of most organizations — particularly mid-size manufacturers, hospitals, school districts, and law firms — are not staffed to spend their hours scrolling through Telegram channels watching for their own employees' stolen browsers to appear for sale. The defensive lever is real. It is not, for most organizations, accessible.

What is left, when the salesman returns from dinner and goes to bed without ever knowing that an installer he ran six hours earlier has just put his employer on a market, is a particular kind of inevitability. The leak-site post that will appear three weeks later, with his company's logo on it and a countdown clock running, will look from the outside like a sudden act. It will be reported as such in the local press. The board of his company will demand to know how the attack happened. The forensic team will produce a report. The report will say, in tactful language, that the original compromise occurred on a personal device belonging to an employee, that the credentials harvested from that device were sold through an underground marketplace, that the access was purchased by an affiliate of a particular ransomware platform, and that the encryption was deployed forty-six hours after the access was purchased. The board will read the report and not entirely believe it. Surely something more dramatic happened. Surely there was a moment of confrontation, of resistance, of attack and defense.

There was not. There was a salesman who needed a piece of software. There was an installer that did what it was paid to do. There was a wholesaler that recognized a valuable listing. There was an affiliate that needed a job. There was a franchise that needed a victim. The market did the rest.

What it has not yet done is choose. The franchises and the wholesalers can supply ransomware operators with thousands of viable victims a quarter, but not every viable victim is worth attacking. Operators have preferences. They pick. They prioritize. They specialize in certain industries and certain geographies, and they have learned, the way every coercive business eventually learns, exactly where the leverage lives.
