# Chapter 2: The Franchise Floor

## Core Question
How did digital extortion adopt the language and logistics of franchising — and what happens when criminal brands try to behave like a cartel?

## Purpose in the Book
Chapter 1 established that ransomware is a market. This chapter zooms in on the organizational mechanics of that market — how the platforms run, how affiliates are recruited and governed, how white-label "franchise" arrangements lower the skill barrier, and how operators have begun signaling cartel-style coordination. The reader should leave this chapter understanding that the criminal organization chart of a modern RaaS brand looks uncomfortably similar to the org chart of a small software company.

## Required Content

### Must Cover
- The platform / affiliate division of labor: core operators provide branding, malware development, leak-site infrastructure, negotiation workflows, payment handling, and affiliate governance; affiliates supply intrusion capability, initial access, victim selection, and operational execution.
- LockBit 5.0 as the worked example of platformization: September 2025 relaunch; multi-platform targeting (Windows, Linux, ESXi); randomized file extensions; faster encryption routines; affiliate onboarding requirement of an approximately $500 Bitcoin deposit as a filter and formalization mechanism.
- White-label ransomware as criminal franchising. DragonForce's 2025 cartel formation and white-label model with 80% profit share to affiliates; the prior RansomHub 90% share before its abrupt shutdown; the resulting affiliate competition.
- Corporate-style partnership behavior: DragonForce's attempted coalition with LockBit and Qilin, the "equal competition conditions" language, the non-aggression framing, the implication of cartel-like coordination.
- The careful distinction between *signaling* and *operational integration*: these alliances are not a conventional merger; they are a market-discipline mechanism and a brand-credibility move.

### Should Include
- A grounded opening: a vignette of a newly recruited affiliate paying their entry deposit and gaining access to a RaaS platform's onboarding portal. Use only mechanics described in Report #1.
- The competitive dynamics: brand reputation, affiliate retention, tooling quality, payment terms, and resilience to takedowns as the variables operators compete on.
- Why white-label matters strategically: it separates brand, infrastructure, and technical skill, allowing operators with weaker technical chops to extract a share of extortion proceeds while routing intrusion and negotiation through the bigger platform.

### Optional (if it fits naturally)
- The "governance risk" angle: when many small brands run on shared infrastructure, attribution becomes harder for defenders and law enforcement.

## Structural Requirements

### Opening
Open inside an affiliate onboarding moment — the moment when a criminal contractor pays in, signs the implicit terms, and gains access to the platform's tools. Translate the moment into terms the reader can recognize: it looks like signing up for a SaaS reseller program, except the product is extortion.

### Body Flow
1. The franchise opening → widen to the platform/affiliate model in general.
2. LockBit 5.0 as a platformization case study (relaunch, deposit, tooling) → transition into white-label.
3. DragonForce and the white-label / cartel signaling → contrast with RansomHub's collapse.
4. The strategic implication: criminal brands compete on terms similar to legitimate platforms (margin, reliability, reputation), which makes the ecosystem harder to disrupt.

### Closing
End on the realization that takedowns of brands do not eliminate the franchise. Set up Chapter 3, where the reader meets the people who actually supply the raw material — credentials, access, sessions — that the franchises depend on.
