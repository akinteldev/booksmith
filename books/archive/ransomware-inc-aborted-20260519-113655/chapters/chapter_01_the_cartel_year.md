# Chapter 1: The Cartel Year

The IT director found out on a Tuesday morning, before her first coffee, because her phone would not stop buzzing.

She worked for a mid-sized parts manufacturer in the American Midwest — the kind of company that supplied other companies, that nobody outside its industry had ever heard of, that employed around eight hundred people and made things that ended up inside things that ended up inside cars. Her job was unglamorous. She kept the network running. She managed the patch cycles. She fought, in the polite way of corporate IT, for budget. She had been doing this for fourteen years.

The first text was from her counterpart at one of their distributors, three time zones away. Have you seen this? A screenshot was attached. A countdown clock. Their company name. A logo she did not recognize at first, rendered in the cold, clean, professional sans-serif font that all of these logos seemed to use now, as if the brand identity had been workshopped by a real design firm. The site looked, at a glance, like a SaaS company's homepage. There was an About page. There was a roster of "clients." There were terms of service.

She opened her laptop. By the time she had her email loaded, the second text arrived. Then the third. Then a missed call from the CEO's assistant. Then another. Somewhere underneath the noise of her phone, the company she had spent fourteen years protecting had been listed on a leak site, alongside its annual revenue, a sample of its stolen files, and a deadline measured in hours.

She read the countdown clock the way one reads any deadline written by strangers: with the slow, peeling realization that the people who wrote it had thought about her company more thoroughly than her own board ever had. Underneath the countdown there was a polished one-paragraph description of what would happen if the deadline passed. There was a list of "client" names — the gang's previous victims, displayed as case studies, almost as social proof — and a sentence about how those previous victims had eventually settled. There was a discount tier visible in the structure of the page, the kind a SaaS company might use to incentivize early renewals. There was, somewhere down the page, a chat link. The aesthetic was unnerving precisely because it was professional. It was not designed to look like a threat. It was designed to look like a transaction.

She would spend the next forty-eight hours discovering what every IT director, hospital administrator, school superintendent, and small-firm partner discovers the morning they wake up on the wrong side of this market. The thing that had happened to her company was not bespoke. It was not personal. It was not even rare. It was inventory. Her company was one item in a catalogue. The catalogue had two thousand entries.

In the first three months of 2026, ransomware operators publicly listed 2,122 organizations on data-leak sites. The number, reported by Check Point Research, made it the second-highest Q1 on record. It was also, in the language of the people who had spent years measuring this ecosystem, a continuation rather than a spike. Ransomware activity had reached 7,960 leak-site listings across 2025 — a 53% year-over-year increase. The pace had not slowed because law enforcement had taken down brands. It had not slowed because companies had hardened their defenses. It had not slowed because affiliates had aged out of the work. It had not slowed at all. What had changed, between 2024 and 2026, was not the size of the problem. It was the shape.

The 2,122 number itself was not the most important thing in the Q1 2026 dataset. The most important thing was how those victims were distributed. Ten ransomware brands — out of the seventy-one active that quarter — accounted for roughly 71% of all listed victims. Four of those ten — Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit — collectively claimed 41%. A single brand, Qilin, accounted for 338 victims by itself, more than the combined output of the fifty smallest operators in the market. To understand what that means, picture an industry — any industry — in which the top ten companies control seven out of every ten customers, and the single dominant firm produces more output than the bottom two-thirds of the industry combined. That is a market. That is a market with winners, with platforms, with brands, with consolidation pressure, with affiliate competition, with capital, with marketing, with HR.

And it is the market that put the IT director's company on a countdown clock.

Six months earlier, the same Q1 share would have looked different. In the third quarter of 2025, the top ten ransomware brands had captured only about 57% of leak-site victims. Eighty-five active brands had competed for the remainder. The picture, from the analyst's perspective, was a noisy ecosystem of mid-sized operators, breakout brands, defunct relaunches, and constantly shifting affiliate allegiances. By Q1 2026, the noise had compressed. Fewer brands were producing the same — slightly more — total victim volume. The market had not shrunk. It had hardened. It had begun to look less like a flea market and more like an oligopoly.

This was the cartel year. Not in the literal sense that ransomware operators had formed a binding price-fixing agreement, although they had tried something close. The cartel year in the sense that the term applied to any maturing illicit market: a year in which a handful of recognizable brands captured most of the activity, set the operational norms, defined the affiliate pricing, and absorbed the talent of competitors who had been disrupted, shut down, or simply outcompeted. The 2,122 victims of Q1 2026 had been processed by a market that was beginning to consolidate around its leaders. The companies inside that 2,122 — the manufacturer in the Midwest, a clinic in Hamburg, a regional grocer in Lille, a county government in the American South — did not get to choose which platform extorted them. The platforms had chosen them, through affiliates, through brokers, through automated reconnaissance, and the platforms competed with one another for the most efficient way to do it.

The hardest part of understanding ransomware in 2026 is unlearning the older instinct that a ransomware "group" is a group of people. It is not, in the way that the public imagination still pictures it, a basement of hooded figures hunched over keyboards. It is a business model. Qilin is not a gang. Qilin is a platform. LockBit 5.0 is not a hacker. LockBit 5.0 is a relaunched brand operating cross-platform encryption tooling, a leak-site, a payment-handling backend, and an affiliate program. The Gentlemen are not a crew. The Gentlemen are a service company with a fast-growing customer base of criminal contractors. DragonForce is not even, primarily, a ransomware operator anymore. DragonForce is closer to a franchise headquarters, providing infrastructure and back-office services to affiliates who run their own branded operations on top.

This is the language the industry has begun to use about itself: platform, affiliate, brand, share, deposit, payout, governance, partner. The criminals borrowed the vocabulary of platform capitalism because the model is the same. The thing being sold has changed. The structure has not.

To see how complete this transformation has been, follow one of the brands. Qilin is the cleanest case study because it is the one that ran away with the quarter. Through the end of 2025 and into the first months of 2026, Qilin posted more victims than any other ransomware operation on earth — 338 in Q1 2026 alone, after a 2025 in which its monthly volume grew from roughly 35 victims a month in the first quarter to more than 150 a month by the fourth. By the time the IT director in the Midwest discovered her company on a leak site, more than a thousand other organizations had already been posted to Qilin's data-leak page over the course of 2025 after refusing or failing to pay. GuidePoint Security, which has tracked Qilin closely, calls it the most active ransomware operation it has ever observed in its dataset — surpassing even LockBit at its 2023 peak.

But Qilin's significance is not the raw number. It is what the raw number means about how the operation is structured. Qilin functions as a recruitment and retention machine for criminal affiliates. The platform — and this is GuidePoint's word for it, used as plainly as it would be used for any legitimate technology company — offers an administrative panel through which affiliates access the ransomware payload itself, manage their victims, post to the leak site, run negotiations, and receive payment splits. The reported revenue terms are aggressive: 80% of any ransom under three million dollars goes to the affiliate; 85% goes to the affiliate above that threshold. The core operator keeps the remainder, plus the value of its growing platform.

That math is the math of a marketplace. An 80% payout means Qilin is not optimizing for margin per intrusion. It is optimizing for scale. Each affiliate the platform retains is a self-employed contractor whose victims feed the operator's brand equity, its payment pipeline, and its bargaining position. The platform has lowered its take so far that the only sensible reason to do so is to win the affiliate market — to be the brand criminals choose to work with when they could be working with anyone else. And there is plenty of competition for them to consider. DragonForce, according to AhnLab, offers a similar 80% revenue split. RansomHub, before its abrupt collapse in early 2025, had reportedly offered up to 90%. The math of these splits is not the math of greed. It is the math of a labor market.

That labor market is the engine. Affiliates are the operational workforce of the ransomware economy. They are the human beings — and, increasingly, the AI-assisted toolchains — who conduct intrusions, escalate privileges, exfiltrate data, deploy the encrypting payload where applicable, and run the negotiations that lead to payment. They do not work exclusively for one brand. They migrate. When RansomHub disappeared in April 2025 — went offline overnight, no warning, no public statement — it left behind a documented backlog of more than 760 victims and an unknown number of affiliates whose tooling, infrastructure, and payment workflows had just vanished. Within weeks, Qilin and DragonForce were recruiting those affiliates publicly on criminal forums. AhnLab describes the RansomHub collapse as the largest structural shift in the affiliate market since Operation Cronos disrupted LockBit in 2024 — and as a windfall for the operators that absorbed the displaced labor. The Q1 2026 numbers are, in part, the receipt for that absorption.

The mechanics of that absorption are worth slowing down on, because they describe how every previous "victory" against this market has been quietly metabolized into the next quarter's growth. When a major brand collapses, the affiliates do not retire. Their skills do not evaporate. Their tooling does not disappear. They have, in some cases, already exfiltrated data from active intrusions they had been preparing to monetize under the old brand. They have working relationships with initial-access brokers who supply them with footholds. They have negotiation patterns and payment-handling instincts that took months to develop. What they lose, in a takedown, is a brand and a platform — the front end of the operation. The back of the operation walks across the street to whichever platform will accept it. Operators understand this perfectly and recruit accordingly. Qilin's forum posts in the weeks after RansomHub's disappearance, according to multiple analyst summaries, were essentially job listings — payout terms, infrastructure assurances, references from current affiliates, the standard hiring language of any growing services firm. DragonForce did the same. The collapsed brand's labor force was, within a quarter, fully re-employed. The intrusions resumed under new logos. The leak sites filled. The market rebalanced. By Q1 2026, the casual observer could be forgiven for not noticing that RansomHub had ever existed. The capacity it had organized — the affiliates, the brokers, the negotiation muscle — had simply moved.

This is the dynamic GuidePoint's GRIT analysts captured in their full-year 2025 numbers, which deserve a moment alongside the Q1 2026 figures. In 2025, GuidePoint tracked 124 distinct ransomware brands active across the year — the highest count it had ever recorded, a 46% year-over-year increase. Total observed victims rose 58% year over year. By every measure of activity, 2025 was a record. And yet 2025 was also the year that began the consolidation visible in Q1 2026. The high brand count was not a sign of health; it was a sign of churn. Brands appeared, posted a few dozen victims, and disappeared. Mid-tier operators rose and faltered. The ecosystem behaved like a tournament with a high turnover at the bottom and an increasingly stable apex. By the time the year ended, the surviving operators had absorbed the durable affiliate base, and the count of active brands had begun to fall. The Q3 2025 peak of eighty-five active groups gave way, by Q1 2026, to seventy-one. The market had not stopped producing new entrants — Check Point counted twenty-one new ransomware brands that emerged during Q1 2026 alone — but most of those new brands posted fewer than ten victims each. The long tail of the ransomware market had become a graveyard of micro-brands with no affiliates and no infrastructure to attract them. The action was elsewhere, on the platforms that could host the work at scale.

This is the dynamic that has frustrated law enforcement and bewildered the public for half a decade. Disrupting a ransomware brand does not destroy the underlying capability. It only redistributes it. Operation Cronos, the international takedown that crippled LockBit's infrastructure in early 2024, did remove LockBit from its dominant 2023 position. It did not remove the affiliates, the access brokers, the negotiators, the leak-site operators, or the demand. By Q1 2026 LockBit was back, in a relaunched form — LockBit 5.0, with reworked encryption routines, multi-platform targeting across Windows, Linux, and ESXi, randomized file extensions, and a $500 Bitcoin deposit required to join the affiliate program. It posted 163 victims in its first full quarter back. That deposit was not, primarily, a way to make money. It was a way to filter applicants and signal seriousness. Affiliates who were willing to put real cryptocurrency at stake to join were affiliates the operator could vet. The deposit is what a legitimate platform business would call onboarding friction. It is what LockBit calls trust.

The Gentlemen tell the same story from the other side. In Q4 2025, the brand had posted forty victims. In Q1 2026 it posted 166. A more than fourfold quarter-over-quarter increase is not the kind of growth a small crew produces by working harder. It is the kind of growth that comes from access. According to the reporting that tracks them, The Gentlemen scaled through a pre-staged pool of compromised perimeter network appliances — roughly 14,700 FortiGate devices already broken into and held in inventory, ready to be matched to affiliates who wanted to ransom whoever happened to be on the other side of the firewall. The Gentlemen did not need years of brand-building. They needed access and a working extortion workflow, and they had both. They walked into the Q1 2026 top tier because the supply chain underneath them was already humming.

Akira fills the last seat of the top four with a different competitive strategy. Where Qilin pursues raw scale and Q1 2026 victim volume, Akira appears to pursue margin. Reporting in Industrial Cyber attributes approximately $244 million in total proceeds to the operation, with a deliberate sectoral focus on consumer goods and industrial manufacturing — categories with strong incentives to settle quickly because production downtime is unforgiving and supply chains are fragile. Akira's leak-site volume is significant, but its strategy seems closer to a high-margin services business than to Qilin's high-volume marketplace. In a maturing market, this is the kind of differentiation you expect to see. Not every platform competes the same way. Some optimize for scale, some for revenue per customer, some for resilience to disruption, some for niche dominance.

Stack these portraits next to one another and the picture changes. Ransomware is not a malware category producing a steady stream of victims. It is a market in which a small number of platform operators compete on payment terms, infrastructure quality, brand recognition, and affiliate retention. Their customers — the affiliates — choose among them the way a developer chooses among cloud providers. Their victims are inventory. The market is not entirely orderly: most of these platforms are violently competitive, betray each other regularly, and operate in a legal environment where the only contracts they can enforce are the ones backed by reputation. But the structural logic is consistent. They are trying to win the affiliate labor market by being the most attractive place to sell extortion services. And they are succeeding, by their own metric, because more affiliates are choosing them — and more victims are paying for that choice with their data, their operations, and their balance sheets.

The clearest sign of how far this transformation has gone is that the largest platforms have begun to behave, at least rhetorically, like a cartel.

In late 2025 and early 2026, open-source reporting tracked what looked like an attempted coalition among the most powerful brands. DragonForce, in particular, was reported to have approached LockBit and Qilin with a proposal that read more like the founding language of an industry consortium than the chatter of competing criminals. The aim, according to the reporting, was to create "equal competition conditions," avoid public conflict, and "dictate market conditions." The participants did not, ultimately, formalize a stable alliance — ZeroFox and several other analyst houses assessed the proposed coalition had not produced verifiable coordination by the close of Q1 2026. But the rhetoric matters even when the structure fails. Operators who would have been knifing each other on forums a few years earlier were proposing market discipline, shared infrastructure, mutual-aid logic, and reputational coordination. The language was that of an industry association feeling out the boundaries of what it could collectively control.

Some of this is bluster. Cartel signaling is itself a marketing tactic, designed to convince affiliates that the participating brands are durable, well-connected, and likely to survive the next round of enforcement actions. A platform that can credibly claim alliance with two or three of its rivals is a platform that looks safer to work with. The signaling, in other words, is part of the affiliate-recruitment competition. But some of it is real. Infrastructure pooling — sharing leak-site hosting, payment-handling backends, or negotiation panels across nominally separate brands — has been documented in the white-label arrangements that DragonForce in particular has built. Backend consolidation can hide behind frontend fragmentation. A market that appears to contain twenty active brands may, in practice, be running on five infrastructure stacks.

The white-label model deserves a closer look, because it is the structural innovation that may matter most in the long run. In ordinary RaaS, an affiliate operates under the brand of the core platform: a victim sees the LockBit logo, the Qilin logo, the Akira logo, regardless of which affiliate executed the intrusion. In white-label RaaS, the affiliate operates under its own brand, designed by the affiliate, while paying the infrastructure provider for the technical backbone underneath. DragonForce is the cleanest example. The reporting from AhnLab, Dark Reading, and ReliaQuest converges: DragonForce introduced a model that lets affiliates run independent-looking ransomware operations using DragonForce's encryption, leak-site, negotiation portals, and payment-handling — for an 80% affiliate revenue share. The affiliate gets to look like a new criminal startup with its own logo, its own data-leak site, its own brand personality, while paying rent to the franchise that does the back-office work. Imagine a fast-food chain that licenses its menu, kitchen equipment, supply chain, and customer-management software to anyone who wants to open a restaurant under a name of their choosing, anywhere in the world, and takes a 20% cut of the receipts. That is what the DragonForce model is, applied to extortion. It is criminal franchising. It explains why the market can appear to have more brands than it has operators. It also explains why some of those brands, with no apparent history or staff, can post double-digit victim counts almost immediately. They are not building anything from scratch. They are operating a turn-key shop on top of someone else's platform.

That franchise dynamic is what makes the Q1 2026 active-group count of seventy-one slightly misleading on its face. Some of those seventy-one are independent platforms with their own infrastructure. Some are mid-tier brands attempting to scale. And some, almost certainly, are white-label storefronts running on the backbone of one of the larger franchisors. The visible diversity of ransomware brands has decoupled, partially, from the actual diversity of ransomware operations. A defender looking at a leak site does not necessarily know whether they are facing a five-year-old criminal organization or a three-month-old branded reseller of someone else's payload. The economic logic underneath both is identical. The platform takes its cut. The affiliate takes the rest. The victim pays or doesn't.

The Brandefense dataset for Q1 2026 captures this dynamic at the market level: 2,135 confirmed ransomware incidents, sixty-eight active groups, ransomware activity recorded across ninety-eight countries, and a 50% quarter-over-quarter growth rate. Numbers from different vendors do not perfectly converge — Check Point counts 2,122 victims, Brandefense counts 2,135 incidents, GuidePoint's GRIT tracks 124 groups across all of 2025 — and the differences reflect methodology, monitoring scope, and the inherent messiness of measuring a criminal economy from the outside. But the convergence at the level of trend is overwhelming. Every major vendor that has measured this market — Check Point, Broadcom's Symantec, AhnLab, GuidePoint, Brandefense, Cyble, and the others — sees the same shape. The ecosystem grew. The brand count compressed. The top platforms widened their lead. The encryption-as-primary-event model gave way to something more flexible and more menacing. Broadcom/Symantec alone tracked 6,182 extortion attacks in 2025, up 23% over the previous year. Cyble's count is similar — approximately 6,500 incidents — and Cyble added a finding that the dominant initial-access route in 2025 was no longer vulnerability exploitation. It was identity compromise. Stolen credentials, valid logins, working sessions. The locks were not being broken. The keys were being stolen first, sold to an affiliate, and used to walk through the front door.

That observation — that 2025 was the year identity overtook exploitation as the primary intrusion route — is the bridge to the rest of this book. It implies that the mental model defenders have been working from is at least one cycle out of date. The attacker is no longer the chaotic genius who finds a flaw in the system. The attacker is a worker drawn from a labor pool that buys access at retail prices, walks in through legitimate authentication, and operates a stage-managed extortion campaign on top of a vendor's platform. The work is divided. The roles are specialized. The platforms compete to host the work, the affiliates compete to perform it, the brokers compete to supply the access, and the negotiators compete to close. Each layer optimizes the next. Each layer feeds the rest.

And the optimization has come at a particular point in the kill chain: the lock is no longer the most important part.

For most of ransomware's public life, the defining event was encryption. The screen went dark, the files were renamed, and a note appeared demanding payment for the decryption key. Backup hygiene became the orthodox defense. Disaster-recovery exercises focused on restoring from clean snapshots. The implicit contract was: if your data is gone, you pay. If your data is recoverable, you don't.

That contract has been quietly rewritten. Palo Alto Networks' Unit 42, which has tracked the tactic mix in its incident-response practice for years, reported that encryption appeared in 78% of 2025 extortion cases. That number sounds reassuringly stable until you put it next to the historical baseline: 92% in 2024, 96% in 2021. The trend line is unambiguous. Encryption is no longer the universal monetization event. Data theft is now the more stable coercive pillar: present in 57% of 2025 cases, holding consistently above half of incidents for the past five years. Harassment — direct outreach to executives, customers, patients, partners — appeared in 10% of 2025 cases, down slightly from a 2024 peak of 13% but up sharply from 5% in 2021. Eighty-six percent of incidents in Unit 42's data involved business disruption regardless of whether encryption was deployed at all. The leverage no longer requires the lockbox. It requires the threat of exposure, the reality of operational chaos, and the willingness to pressure the humans who can authorize a payment.

The IT director in the Midwest learned this in the way most victims learn it. Her backups were intact. She had, over the past four years, fought for the budget and the test cycles to make sure they were intact. She had, in fact, restored from those backups during a tabletop exercise less than six months earlier. None of that information appeared on her countdown clock. The countdown clock was not, in 2026, primarily about decryption keys. It was about thirty-two gigabytes of internal documents — engineering specifications, supplier contracts, employee health records, an email thread between her CEO and a board member containing language about a contemplated acquisition — that the affiliate working her case had quietly exfiltrated three weeks before the encryption tripped. The encryption was, in a sense, the trailing event. The data was already gone. The threat had already shaped itself into the form it would keep for the next forty-eight hours: not "pay for the key," but "pay to keep this private."

What had changed, in the operational sense, was that the affiliate working her case did not need to pick a single coercive move. Modern extortion is modular. The 2025–2026 playbook reads less like a single attack pattern and more like a set of pressure dials the affiliate can turn up or down depending on which one bites. The dial labeled encryption is one option, often optional. The dial labeled data exposure is reliably on. There are additional dials, recurring across the campaigns the reports describe: customer or partner notification, executive harassment, regulatory disclosure leverage, denial-of-service noise stacked on top of an active restoration effort. In the language of the analysts, this is multi-extortion — double, triple, sometimes quadruple — and it is the reason the encryption-only threat model now feels antique. The affiliate working the Midwest IT director's case did not need the lockbox to be unrecoverable. They only needed her board to believe that the exposure of an unreleased acquisition discussion, plus the employee health records, plus a sample of the engineering files appearing on the leak-site countdown page, would be more expensive to weather than the ransom. The lockbox was, almost, decoration. The real product was leverage. The market had taught its affiliates exactly which dials to turn.

She paid. Or her board paid. Or the cyber-insurance carrier paid, partially. The details that determined exactly how the money moved would not, in the end, change what the market learned from her case. The market learned: another item processed. Another data point in the 2,122. Another small contribution to the brand equity of the platform that had run her extortion. Another reason for an affiliate, somewhere, to choose that platform again the next time they had a fresh foothold to monetize.

She was not the unluckiest IT director in the world. She was simply, statistically, on the receiving end of a thoroughly mature industry. The platforms had won. The affiliates had been recruited. The access had been brokered. The leak site had been built. The negotiation playbook had been written. By the time her phone started buzzing on a Tuesday morning, all of that scaffolding was already in place around her company. Her job was not to defeat ransomware. It was to be on the other side of a transaction whose terms had been set without her.

What this means for everyone else is, perhaps, the only useful place to end a first chapter. The shift from a fragmented to a consolidated ransomware market changes the threat model for every reader of this book — not in an abstract industry-analyst sense, but in the concrete sense that the apparatus described in the next eight chapters will, with high statistical likelihood, route through some part of their life. The hospital that holds their MRI. The school district their child attends. The accountant who files their taxes. The supplier their employer depends on. The cloud vendor their phone backs up to. The franchise their hometown grocer is part of. The platforms behind the 2,122 of Q1 2026 are not running out of customers. They are running out of room to scale at the current organizational level, which is why they have begun to behave more like an industry and less like a scene. They are improving their platforms. They are hiring better affiliates. They are buying cheaper access. They are professionalizing their negotiation desks. They are signaling, to one another and to the affiliates they need, that the market is here to stay.

This is the second-order effect that most readers never quite reconcile. The threat model of a 2018 ransomware incident was, in some real sense, individual: a specific gang, with a specific tool, finding a specific weak spot. The threat model of a 2026 ransomware incident is institutional: a market that has identified a class of victims, an access economy that supplies the keys, a platform that hosts the work, and a brand that markets the leverage. The hospital that holds your medical records is not, in this model, fighting a hacker. It is participating, on the unfavorable side, in a transaction with a multi-billion-dollar industry. So is the small accounting firm where your tax return is sitting. So is the bus company that holds your employer's freight schedule. So is the regional water authority. There is no opt-out. Every institution that touches your life sits somewhere in the supply chain that the platforms have learned to monetize. When the IT director in the Midwest realized her company was on a leak site, what she was discovering, beneath the immediate operational disaster, was that the company had been quietly catalogued — and sold — by an industry she had never quite understood was an industry. Most people in her position discover the same thing. Almost none of them discover it before the countdown clock starts.

There is a quiet caution buried inside the volume data, and it is worth carrying into the rest of the book. Leak-site postings are not the same as paid ransoms. GuidePoint's analysis of Qilin's high posting tempo notes, drily, that "high victim volume does not necessarily mean high revenue." A platform that publishes a thousand victim names in a year can still struggle to convert those names into payment. Coveware, cited by GuidePoint, reported that ransom payment rates fell to a historic low — around 23% — by the third quarter of 2025. Fewer than one in four named victims paid. That number is, in one sense, encouraging: it suggests that organizations are getting better at refusing, at restoring, at riding out the storm. But it is also the reason the platforms have started to behave like platforms. As payment rates fall, the operators have to compensate with volume, with leverage, with more credible proof of theft, with sharper threats, with bigger leak-site theater, with harassment campaigns directed at customers and executives, with regulatory pressure, with quadruple-extortion stack-ups designed to break the will of victims who would otherwise have refused. The market has not been deterred by the falling payment rate. It has industrialized in response to it.

The right way to understand the year that produced 2,122 publicly listed victims in three months is, therefore, not as an aberration. It is as the new baseline of a maturing industry. The brands have consolidated. The affiliates have organized. The access has been packaged. The leverage has multiplied. The takedowns will continue, and they will matter, and they will not, on the available evidence, be enough on their own. What used to be a malware problem is now a market. What used to be a market is now beginning to behave like a cartel.

The IT director in the Midwest closed her laptop in the late afternoon of her second day, finally, after thirty-six hours of triage and conference calls and lawyer-to-lawyer negotiations she had never expected to be part of. The next morning, two thousand other organizations would still be on a leak site somewhere. The platforms would be sorting through the day's intake. The affiliates would be choosing which brand to deploy under. The brokers would be packaging fresh inventory. The negotiators would be opening new chat threads with finance officers in cities she would never visit. Somewhere, on a forum she would never see, a recruiter for a platform she now knew by name would be welcoming a new affiliate to the program. The cartel year was not the year ransomware peaked. It was the year ransomware learned to scale.
