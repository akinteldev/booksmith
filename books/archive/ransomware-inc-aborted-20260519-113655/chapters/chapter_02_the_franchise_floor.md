# Chapter 2: The Franchise Floor

The wire transfer was small. Five hundred dollars in Bitcoin, roughly. The kind of figure that would not register on any compliance system, the kind a curious teenager might assemble from a credit-card cash advance, the kind a mid-tier criminal in a small city anywhere in the world could put together over a weekend.

Imagine the moment in concrete terms. A man in his late twenties, sitting in a rented apartment in a city neither he nor the platform he was about to join would ever publicly identify. A laptop. A pre-paid SIM in a burner phone on the desk. A Tor browser open to a forum the public had never heard of and never would. He had been negotiating in private messages with a recruiter for over a week. The recruiter had asked for references — other criminals who could vouch for him. He had supplied two. The recruiter had asked about prior intrusions, about tooling, about whether he understood the platform's rules of engagement around critical-infrastructure targets. He had answered carefully. He had been patient. And now, finally, the recruiter had sent him the wallet address.

He paid the deposit. He waited. Eight hours later, the welcome message arrived: links to the affiliate panel, the encryptor builder, the chat-based negotiation system, the leak-site dashboard, the file-storage backend, the support channel. A small PDF outlining the platform's affiliate handbook — rules of conduct, escalation policies, payout schedules, dispute resolution. The recruiter ended the message with a sentence that could have come from any technology company onboarding a new sales partner: "Welcome to the program. We're looking forward to working with you."

He was now an affiliate of LockBit 5.0. The platform he had paid to join had, eighteen months earlier, been the most disrupted ransomware brand on earth — its servers seized by an international law-enforcement coalition called Operation Cronos, its leaks reversed and turned into a press conference, its keys handed back to victims. By any reasonable reading of public coverage at the time, LockBit was finished. By the time he sent his five hundred dollars, the brand had relaunched in September 2025, rebuilt its infrastructure, rewritten its encryption tooling to cross the boundary between Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi environments, randomized its file extensions into sixteen-character strings that would frustrate static signatures, and reopened its affiliate program. By the first quarter of 2026 it had posted 163 victims — a 106% increase over the previous quarter — and re-entered the global top tier. The five-hundred-dollar deposit he had just paid was not, primarily, how LockBit made money. It was how LockBit decided whether to let him in.

That deposit is the door. Behind it is the franchise floor.

To understand how that floor is laid, it helps to abandon the language of the criminal underground and adopt, instead, the language of platform capitalism. Modern ransomware operators do not run "gangs" in any sense the term retains operational meaning. They run platforms. The platform provides branding. It provides the malware payload. It maintains the leak-site infrastructure, including the customer-facing pages, the countdown clocks, and the data-publication mechanism. It provides the negotiation portal — a structured chat system, usually with built-in escalation rules and pre-written templates. It handles the payments, including the crypto wallet plumbing required to receive and split the proceeds. It administers affiliate governance: who is admitted, who is sanctioned, who is dismissed, what targets are off-limits, what the dispute resolution rules are. The platform is, in every sense that matters operationally, a managed-services business. What it manages is extortion.

The affiliate is the labor. Affiliates supply the intrusion capability — the technical skill, or the social-engineering chops, or the purchased access — that turns a victim organization into an inventory item the platform can monetize. Affiliates conduct the reconnaissance, perform the lateral movement, exfiltrate the data, deploy the encryptor where it will be deployed, and sometimes run the negotiations themselves. They do this in exchange for a percentage. In a mature RaaS platform, that percentage is high — high enough that the operator's economics depend entirely on the volume of affiliates it can attract and retain, not on the margin it takes from any single incident.

The percentages are the clearest evidence that this market is now a labor market. The numbers vary by platform, but the range is consistent across the reports that track them. Vectra's overview of contemporary RaaS economics describes a dominant affiliate model in which affiliates earn between 60% and 80% of any ransom paid, while operators retain between 20% and 40%. Qilin's reported terms are at the high end of that band: 80% to affiliates on payments under three million dollars, 85% on payments above that threshold, leaving the operator with the slimmer fifteen or twenty. DragonForce's white-label model — to which we will return shortly — offers a similar 80%. RansomHub, before its abrupt collapse in early 2025, had reportedly offered up to 90%. The math is not the math of a syndicate skimming off its workers. It is the math of a platform desperate to keep them.

The deposit is the other side of that math. Qilin, LockBit, and the other established platforms cannot afford to fill their affiliate ranks with anyone who shows up. They are operating in a hostile environment — international law enforcement, hostile intelligence services, rival operators looking for opportunities to discredit them, undercover researchers looking for actionable insight. An affiliate program that does not vet its applicants is an affiliate program that gets infiltrated, exposed, or sabotaged. The deposit is the simplest possible filter. A would-be affiliate who is willing to put five hundred dollars in Bitcoin at risk has at least demonstrated criminal intent and basic competence with cryptocurrency. The operator can then conduct further vetting: interviews on encrypted channels, requests for references, demonstrations of prior intrusions. The deposit, in the language of legitimate hiring, is the application fee that separates serious candidates from window-shoppers.

Other platforms calibrate this differently. Vectra's overview describes recruitment requirements ranging from deposits as low as 0.05 BTC to demands for verifiable proof of prior attack activity. Some lower-tier platforms operate on a more open subscription model — monthly fees in the range of forty to a hundred dollars for low-end access, one-time license fees from five hundred to as much as eighty-four thousand dollars for premium kits. The pricing tiers function the way pricing tiers function in any software-as-a-service company: there is an entry-level offering for small operators, a professional tier for serious affiliates, and an enterprise tier for buyers who want full control and customization. The market is segmented because the labor force is segmented. A platform optimizing for scale serves a different affiliate than a platform optimizing for elite operators with their own malware and their own access pipelines.

The platforms compete on terms an ordinary technology executive would recognize immediately: brand reputation, payout reliability, tooling quality, infrastructure uptime, negotiation support, payment-conversion track record, and resilience to enforcement actions. An affiliate considering where to send their next intrusion has reasons to choose Qilin: the highest published throughput in the market, infrastructure that has not been disrupted, a payout split at the high end, an administrative panel that includes spam capabilities and an automated ransom-negotiation tool. An affiliate has reasons to choose Akira: a deliberately selected sectoral focus — consumer goods, industrial manufacturing, places where downtime is unforgiving — that historically converts at higher rates. An affiliate has reasons to choose DragonForce: the white-label option, which lets the affiliate operate under their own brand while paying rent for the infrastructure. An affiliate has reasons to choose LockBit 5.0: a brand whose intimidation value, despite the takedown of its predecessor, still extracts payments faster than the newer entrants, plus a relaunched payload built specifically for the cross-platform environments that have become the dominant targets. Each platform has a value proposition. Each affiliate is choosing among them. The market is, in the most precise sense of the term, competitive.

The platform that has done the most to formalize this dynamic is LockBit. Its September 2025 relaunch is worth treating as a case study in platformization, because every element of the rebuild was calibrated to attract and retain affiliate labor. The technical upgrades, on their own, would have been impressive: a complete rewrite of the encryption routines, multi-platform targeting that crossed Windows, Linux, and ESXi in a single payload, anti-analysis features designed to frustrate sandboxes and security tooling, and the sixteen-character randomized file extensions that, according to Arete's reporting, were added to every encrypted file precisely to defeat the kinds of static-signature defenses that had matured around earlier LockBit variants. But the technical upgrades were not the headline. The headline was the affiliate program. The five-hundred-dollar deposit. The published payout structure. The expanded rules of engagement — AhnLab notes, with appropriate caution, that LockBit 5.0 explicitly authorized affiliates to attack critical-infrastructure categories, including nuclear and thermal power facilities, that previous LockBit policies had nominally avoided. Whether that policy was performative recruitment signaling or a serious operational shift cannot be determined from public reporting. But the rhetorical move was unmistakable. LockBit was telling the affiliate market: we are back, we are aggressive, we are not constrained by the old rules, and we are open for business.

By the close of Q1 2026, the recruiting effort had measurably paid off. The platform's 163 victims, ranking it fourth in the global top tier, reflected affiliates who had chosen LockBit over its competitors and brought their access, their data, and their negotiation work to the platform's infrastructure. Bitdefender, monitoring the leak site through the close of the first quarter, reported a running total of more than two hundred victims claimed in the months following the relaunch — manufacturing, healthcare, government, construction, the sectoral mix of a generalist platform whose affiliates were targeting whatever they could reach. LockBit had not survived Operation Cronos by accident. It had survived by understanding that the brand was an asset that, with enough investment, could be relaunched. And it had relaunched by treating the affiliate market the way a SaaS startup treats developer adoption: as the thing that has to be won first, before anything else can work.

The competitive logic does not end with the platform-versus-platform dynamic. It is now actively pushing the market into something more structurally interesting: white-label ransomware.

In the conventional RaaS model, an affiliate operates under the platform's brand. A victim that gets posted by a Qilin affiliate sees the Qilin logo. A victim ransomed by a LockBit affiliate sees LockBit's leak site. The brand and the operator are visually inseparable. The white-label model breaks that link. The affiliate operates under their own chosen brand, with their own logo, their own leak site, their own ransom note, while paying the underlying infrastructure provider for the technical backbone — the encryptor, the leak-site hosting, the negotiation portal, the payment handling, the data-storage backend, the affiliate panel. The infrastructure provider becomes invisible to victims. The visible brand becomes whatever the affiliate has chosen to call themselves.

DragonForce is the cleanest publicly documented example. The Secureworks Counter Threat Unit, in reporting summarized by Dark Reading and Cyberpress, has described DragonForce's affiliate model as offering, in essence, an "everything but the malware" option. Affiliates pay for access to an administration and client panel, encryption and negotiation tools, a file-storage system, a Tor-based leak site with its own onion domain, and ongoing support services. They can deploy DragonForce's own malware payload, or they can bring their own — the platform doesn't care. They can present to victims as DragonForce-branded, or they can present under any logo they invent. They keep 80% of any ransom paid. DragonForce keeps 20% and bears the cost of maintaining the backend.

The right legitimate-business analogy is not really franchising. It is closer to business process outsourcing. A company that decides to outsource its customer service to a managed-services vendor in another country does not stop being itself. Its brand stays the same. Its products stay the same. What changes is that the unsexy back-office machinery — the call routing, the case-tracking, the QA, the support infrastructure — is now somebody else's responsibility. The company keeps the margin on its top line and pays the vendor a slice of operating costs for keeping the machinery running. DragonForce's white-label model is, with very few modifications, that same arrangement. The "company" is a criminal affiliate. The "back office" is extortion infrastructure. The "margin" is the ransom. The "vendor fee" is the platform's twenty percent.

The market consequences of this model are bigger than the rhetorical novelty might suggest. The first consequence is that the visible brand count of the ransomware market becomes an unreliable proxy for the actual operator count. A leak site with twenty active brands listed on it may, in practice, be running on five backend platforms. A defender looking at a new criminal logo cannot know whether they are facing a freshly minted independent operation or an affiliate operating a turn-key shop on top of DragonForce. AhnLab, with appropriate caution, has warned that the white-label phenomenon makes it harder to identify attack groups based on the brand alone. Technical attribution is still possible — payload analysis, infrastructure correlation, wallet flow analysis can still produce attribution-grade evidence — but the names on the leak sites are no longer reliable signals on their own.

The second consequence is that the addressable criminal market widens. Before white-label, an aspiring ransomware operator needed either to acquire and customize a payload, build a leak site, design a negotiation portal, set up the payment plumbing, manage the storage of stolen data, and recruit affiliates — or work for someone else's brand. After white-label, the aspiring operator can buy access to all of those services from DragonForce or its imitators, design a logo over a weekend, and start operating. Some of the affiliates flowing into white-label arrangements are technically capable criminals who simply do not want to build infrastructure. Some are technically minimal criminals who would never have been able to enter the market under the old model. Either way, the labor pool expands. The Secureworks reporting captures this dynamic precisely: the model can attract both criminals with limited technical ability who benefit from an established backend, and skilled actors seeking infrastructure support without the burden of maintaining their own systems.

The third consequence is more strategic. The same logic that makes white-label work for encryption-based ransomware also makes it work for extortion that has nothing to do with encryption at all. Broadcom and Symantec, tracking the 2025 extortion ecosystem, reported that encryption-involving attacks held steady at roughly 4,700 incidents annually, while data-theft-only extortion rose enough to push the total to 6,182 — a 23% increase over 2024. That growth was not coming from new encrypting malware. It was coming from affiliates who had data, or access, or both, and who needed an extortion platform to monetize them. A white-label provider does not require its affiliates to encrypt anything. Some affiliates do. Some affiliates only steal data, post it on the platform's leak site, run the platform's negotiation portal, and threaten publication. The platform monetizes both kinds of activity. The platform does not care which one the affiliate prefers, as long as the affiliate is paying for the infrastructure.

Anubis, another platform documented in the Secureworks reporting, has taken this productization further than DragonForce. Its affiliate package reportedly includes a "data ransom" option in which the operators prepare what they call an "investigative article" — a structured analysis of the victim's stolen data, formatted for use as a negotiation artifact. The affiliate uploads the stolen files. The platform produces the editorial. The editorial gets staged on a password-protected Tor site, alongside a negotiation link the victim is invited to use. The platform reportedly claims it will, on the affiliate's behalf, also notify the victim's customers and regulators in the United States, the United Kingdom, and Europe. Dark Reading, summarizing the model, compared it to managed content marketing for threat actors. The comparison is uncomfortable precisely because it is accurate. The Anubis model is not selling malware. It is selling crisis-leverage production. It is selling, in the most literal sense, the work product of extortion.

It is worth lingering on what that model implies, because the implication runs underneath the rest of this book. A non-technical affiliate who has acquired access to a victim network — perhaps through a credential they bought from an access broker, perhaps through a successful phishing campaign run by a partner — is now able to outsource not just the encryption, not just the leak-site hosting, not just the payment plumbing, but the editorial framing of the threat itself. The affiliate does not need to know which documents inside the victim's data set will create the most pressure. The platform's analysts will tell them. The affiliate does not need to know how to write a credible threat letter, how to time a customer-notification campaign, how to draft a regulator-notification template, how to assemble the public face of an extortion case. The platform does all of it. The affiliate's only remaining job is to have acquired the access in the first place, and to collect the money when the victim pays. This is, in the most precise sense, what platform capitalism does to any service economy it touches. It separates the labor that can be commoditized from the labor that cannot. The commoditizable parts get absorbed into the platform. The remaining parts get pushed out to the affiliates. The platform takes a cut of everything that flows through.

Negotiation support, in particular, has become the single most important competitive differentiator among the major platforms. Many affiliates are technically capable but commercially clumsy. They can break into networks, but they cannot price a ransom, sustain pressure without collapsing the conversation, make credible claims about what data they have exfiltrated, or read the signals a frightened CFO is sending in a chat window. Platformized RaaS fills that gap with structured negotiation portals, standardized message templates, scripted escalation patterns, and in some cases — Qilin's reported "Call Lawyer" feature is the most discussed example — bolt-on services that purport to provide legal counsel to affiliates who are running cases against targets with sophisticated legal teams. Check Point, in its 2026 cybersecurity reporting, has documented Qilin's platform features in detail: the encryptor itself, the affiliate's leak-site posting tooling, the structured payment-negotiation dashboard, the operational support, and the additional services that have accumulated around the core offering like the feature accretion of any maturing SaaS product. Each addition increases the platform's value to its affiliates. Each addition also, quietly, increases the percentage of the criminal enterprise the platform controls.

The deeper consequence is that the operator's competitive advantage no longer lies primarily in the technical sophistication of its payload. It lies in the operational completeness of its platform. An affiliate choosing among Qilin, LockBit, Akira, DragonForce, and the rest is not, primarily, choosing among encryption algorithms. They are choosing among managed-services bundles. The platform that offers the most reliable payment splits, the most credible decryption assurances, the most polished negotiation portal, the best affiliate support, the strongest leak-site presence, and the most flexible options for branding and target selection is the platform that wins the labor market. Everything else — the malware, the technical innovation, the cryptographic primitives — is, increasingly, table stakes. The market is competing on service quality.

This is the moment when the corporate-style language starts to feel less like a metaphor and more like a description. A platform that takes a 20% cut for hosting extortion infrastructure, that offers tiered affiliate programs, that maintains a support team, that publishes rules of engagement and dispute-resolution procedures, that runs onboarding for new affiliates, that does crisis-content production as a value-add — that platform is not, by any structural measure, doing something different from what a legitimate technology company does. The product is different. The structure is the same. The platforms have read the management literature. They have built the org charts. They are running ransomware the way other people run software.

And, increasingly, they are signaling that they would like to run it together.

Cartel coordination among ransomware operators is one of the harder topics to write about with appropriate calibration, because the public signaling is loud and the verifiable structure underneath it is thin. The clearest documented case, as Secure Blink and several other analyst houses have reported, is DragonForce's September 2025 proposal to LockBit and Qilin for a public coalition. The proposal, made on the kind of dark-web forum where criminal operators routinely do their business, used language that read more like a press release from a fledgling industry association than the back-channel chatter of rival gangs. "Equal competition conditions." Avoidance of public conflict and "public insults." A coordinated effort to "dictate market conditions." LockBit's operator responded affirmatively. DragonForce subsequently declared the coalition active. Whether anything operationally meaningful happened underneath the rhetoric is, as of the close of Q1 2026, unclear. ZeroFox and other analyst houses have assessed that the proposed coalition had not produced verifiable joint operations, shared affiliate rosters, or technical infrastructure correlation that would prove a true merger. What it had produced was the signal.

The signal matters even when the structure is empty. Cartel signaling functions, primarily, as a recruitment tactic. An affiliate evaluating which platform to join sees an alliance announcement and reasons, plausibly, that platforms publicly committing to mutual aid are platforms more likely to survive the next round of takedowns. Affiliates value continuity above almost everything else. They have already lived through what happens when a platform collapses — the RansomHub disappearance of April 2025, the slow drift of disrupted brands into irrelevance, the lost intrusions and stranded data and forfeited payouts. A coalition that promises non-aggression and infrastructure pooling is, in marketing terms, a guarantee of business continuity. It does not have to be true. It only has to be believed.

But some of it is true, or could become true, and that is what makes cartel signaling worth watching even when it is mostly performative. Infrastructure pooling — the actual sharing of leak-site backends, payment-handling systems, or negotiation portals across nominally separate brands — is documented to a meaningful degree in the white-label arrangements. DragonForce's twenty percent take from its affiliate brands is, by definition, infrastructure pooling. The visible "DragonForce" is one brand; the visible affiliates are other brands; the underlying platform is one platform. A defender looking at three different leak sites with three different logos may be looking at three different brand presentations of one operator's service stack. AhnLab's caution that brand-based attribution is increasingly unreliable applies most strongly to exactly this dynamic. The visible market is fragmenting. The infrastructure market may be consolidating. Both can be true at the same time.

There is a governance problem buried in this arrangement, and it cuts in both directions. Platform operators have an interest in keeping their affiliates inside the rules of engagement the platform has published. Reckless affiliates draw enforcement attention. Affiliates who target categories the operator has prohibited — pediatric hospitals, emergency services, sometimes critical infrastructure — generate the kind of public outrage that converts ransomware from a tolerable nuisance into a top-of-government priority. But the operators have weak enforcement mechanisms. Their leverage over affiliates is essentially limited to dismissal from the program and forfeiture of payouts on disputed cases. An affiliate determined to ignore the rules can usually find another platform that will accept them. AhnLab's reporting that LockBit 5.0 explicitly authorized affiliates to target critical-infrastructure categories that previous LockBit policies had prohibited cuts both ways: it may signal a genuine operational pivot, or it may signal that the platform was tired of losing affiliates to less restrictive competitors. Either reading exposes the structural fragility of the model. The platforms have built sophisticated managed services. They have not built sophisticated managed governance. The principal-agent problem inside the franchise floor is, in a real sense, unsolved.

The fragility runs the other way, too. Shared infrastructure increases efficiency but creates correlated failure. Secureworks, in the same reporting that documented the DragonForce model, warned that a compromise of one affiliate in a shared environment could expose the operational and victim details of other affiliates using the same platform. The white-label promise of brand autonomy is, in this sense, an illusion at the technical layer. A single backend compromise, a single insider leak, a single operational-security failure on the platform's side can affect every affiliate brand riding on top. The platforms have replicated, in the criminal ecosystem, exactly the kind of systemic concentration risk that legitimate platform economies have learned to dread. A failure at the platform level cascades to every brand on the platform. The same logic that makes the model efficient makes it brittle. When the brittleness eventually breaks something — and the operators understand it will eventually break something — every affiliate riding the franchise will be in the same fire at the same time.

The most useful way to think about the cartel year — and we are now able to name it, having seen the structural mechanics — is to imagine a maturing legitimate industry that has begun to develop the soft governance of trade associations, professional standards, and competitive norms. There is no enforcement mechanism. There is no legal contract. There is, however, a shared interest among the major operators in not crashing the market with chaos. Reckless affiliates who attack hospitals or schools and trigger emergency law-enforcement responses are bad for everyone. Aggressive pricing wars between brands that drive affiliate splits past 90% leave nothing for the platforms to live on. Public mockery and disrespect between rival operators reduces the perceived professionalism of the entire ecosystem, which makes it harder to extract payments from terrified executives who would prefer to believe they are negotiating with disciplined professionals rather than capricious vandals. A cartel arrangement — even an aspirational one — is, in part, an effort to discipline the market. It is the criminal equivalent of an industry compact, signed by no one, enforceable by reputation, and made credible only by repeated public performance.

The harder governance question is what happens when those compacts fail, as compacts among criminals invariably do. Operations that publicly commit to non-aggression continue to compete for the same affiliates, the same access, the same victims, and the same paying customers. Affiliates migrate. Platforms poach. Trust collapses. The history of the underground is littered with alliances that fell apart within weeks of their public announcement. The DragonForce coalition may turn out to be one more entry in that list. Or it may, in some attenuated form, mature into the durable infrastructure-sharing arrangement that the white-label model has already proven viable. The outcome will not be obvious from public reporting for at least another year.

But there is a more important question hidden inside the cartel signaling, and it is the question this chapter has been quietly building toward. If law-enforcement disruption can erase a brand but not its capability — if a takedown of Qilin tomorrow would simply scatter its affiliates across LockBit and DragonForce — then what is the strategy for actually reducing this market? The reports converge, with unusual unanimity, on the same answer: the strategy is to attack the supply chain underneath the platforms, not the platforms themselves. Because the platforms run on inputs they do not, themselves, produce. They run on access. They run on credentials. They run on victim networks already compromised by other criminals — by initial-access brokers and infostealer operators who specialize in breaking into networks and never run a single negotiation themselves. The franchise floor is a building. The wholesalers who supply the building with raw material are the ones who actually keep the lights on.

Most of the affiliates the platforms have recruited are not, in fact, world-class intruders. They are increasingly people whose primary skill is purchasing access and operating an extortion workflow on top of it. The platforms have made this possible by lowering the technical barriers to participation; the wholesalers have made it possible by providing a constant supply of pre-compromised networks at retail prices. The IT director at the Midwestern manufacturer in the first chapter was not, primarily, the victim of LockBit or Qilin or DragonForce. She was the victim of whichever infostealer ran on whichever employee's laptop, whichever access broker quietly packaged that infostealer's output into a working VPN credential, and whichever affiliate bought that credential, walked through her authentication system, and made the choice to deploy under a platform's logo. The platforms have learned to industrialize the back half of the kill chain. The wholesalers have industrialized the front half.

The five-hundred-dollar deposit that admitted our hypothetical affiliate to LockBit's program was, in some real sense, the simplest expense he would face. The next purchase he would make — the working credential to a real victim organization, sold to him by an access broker who had bought the underlying infostealer log for a few dollars — could cost between five hundred and ten thousand dollars depending on the size and richness of the target. He would not need to know how to crack a password. He would not need to find a zero-day. He would not need to write a single line of malware. He would only need to buy a key, walk through a door, install a payload he had not written under a brand he had not built, and let the platform's negotiation desk do the rest. The franchise floor is a finished building. The wholesalers we are about to meet are the ones who supplied every key that opens every door inside it.
