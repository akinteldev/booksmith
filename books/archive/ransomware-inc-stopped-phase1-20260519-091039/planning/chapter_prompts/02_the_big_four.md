# Chapter 2: The Big Four

## Core Question

Who are the dominant ransomware operators of 2025–2026 — Qilin, Akira, LockBit, and the rising challengers — and how do they compete for affiliates, victims, and criminal market share?

## Purpose in the Book

Chapter 1 explained the model. Chapter 2 names the players. By grounding the abstract RaaS framework in specific, documented criminal organizations, the chapter makes the threat tangible. The reader should come away understanding that these are not anonymous "hackers" but organized criminal enterprises with brands, strategies, competitive pressures, and reputations — and that the competitive dynamics between them have direct consequences for their victims.

## Required Content

### Must Cover

- Profile of Qilin: most active operator Q1 2026 (338 victims), third consecutive quarter at top, 2025-era affiliate growth model, why high victim count doesn't automatically mean high revenue
- Profile of LockBit 5.0: post-disruption comeback story — Operation Cronos, the September 2025 relaunch with multi-platform targeting, $500 affiliate deposit barrier, 163 victims in Q1 2026
- Profile of Akira: approximately 16% of 2025 claimed attacks alongside Qilin, ongoing SonicWall targeting, approximately 203 victims derived for Q1 2026
- The Gentlemen: breakout growth — 40 victims Q4 2025 to 166 Q1 2026, rapid scaling

### Should Include

- DragonForce: March 2025 cartel restructuring, white-label model, 80% affiliate share, the attempted coalition with LockBit and Qilin
- The competitive logic: how operators differentiate on tooling quality, profit splits, brand reputation, infrastructure reliability — not just malware
- Why brand matters in the criminal market: affiliates compare programs like workers compare employers; reputation, payment reliability, and victim conversion rates are competitive advantages

### Optional (if it fits naturally)

- The non-aggression proposal narrative: DragonForce's attempt to coordinate "equal competition conditions" with LockBit and Qilin — the clearest example of cartel-style market thinking in criminal ransomware

## Structural Requirements

### Opening

Open with a concrete detail that illustrates criminal market competition: the moment DragonForce publicly proposed its non-aggression pact with LockBit and Qilin on criminal forums, calling for "equal competition conditions" and an end to mutual disruption. This is the chapter's governing image — criminal enterprises trying to manage their competitive landscape like a trade association.

### Body Flow

1. The dominant operators: profile each major group in terms of scale, model, and competitive positioning — not as a list, but as a portrait of a marketplace
2. The competitive dynamics: what operators compete on (profit splits, tooling, brand reputation, infrastructure reliability) — the criminal equivalent of employer branding
3. The cartelization attempt: DragonForce's coalition proposal and what it reveals about how these operators think about their market

### Closing

End on the revelation: this is not a chaotic underground — it is a maturing market with recognizable competitive dynamics, brand strategy, and even attempted market coordination. That maturity is what makes it so dangerous.
