# Chapter 5: Hiring the Criminals

## Core Question

How do ransomware cartels recruit, vet, compensate, and retain a distributed criminal workforce — and what does it mean that cybercrime now has an HR function?

## Purpose in the Book

This chapter takes the workforce angle the book has been building and makes it explicit: ransomware is not just organized like a business, it is managed like one. There are recruitment pipelines, compensation structures, performance incentives, retention challenges, and workforce governance problems. The chapter humanizes the criminal side without sympathizing with it — showing that the people running these operations have management problems, labor disputes, and competitive pressures just like any other employer. That normality is what makes them dangerous.

## Required Content

### Must Cover

- Affiliate recruitment: how RaaS platforms attract operators, the forum-based vetting process, reputation systems, deposit requirements
- Criminal "HR": compensation engineering (revenue splits, non-cash benefits), performance expectations, affiliate management infrastructure (panels, dispute resolution)
- Workforce governance: how operators control affiliates without contracts, formal authority, or legal recourse
- Affiliate drift: what happens when affiliates lose confidence in a platform and migrate to competitors

### Should Include

- The rogue insider recruitment model: ransomware gangs targeting IT/cloud/security professionals, contractors, and employees with trusted access; formal incentive structures (commissions, anonymity guarantees)
- Gig-worker exploitation: how ransomware groups outsource commodity tasks (credential validation, bulk phishing, money mule operations) to low-skill workers who may not even know they're working for ransomware
- The post-disruption hiring wave: how brand collapses (RansomHub, LockBit) create a sudden labor market where competing operators recruit aggressively
- NCC Group reporting: ransomware gangs adopting formal recruitment strategies targeting employees with IT/cloud/identity access

### Optional (if it fits naturally)

- Reported 47% increase in publicly reported attacks in 2025 despite declining ransom payments — the pressure on affiliate incentive structures this creates

## Structural Requirements

### Opening

Hypothetical vignette: a cloud administrator at a healthcare firm receives a direct message on a dark-web forum. The message doesn't sound criminal — it reads like a recruiter's pitch. They're offering a "commission" for access to the company's Azure tenant. The message is professional, confident, and alarmingly specific about the target's value. Walk through the recruitment psychology: why some people say yes.

### Body Flow

1. Affiliate recruitment: the forum vetting process, reputation mechanics, how dominant platforms attract the best talent
2. Criminal HR in practice: compensation design, panel infrastructure, dispute resolution — governance without contracts
3. The insider recruitment pipeline: why trusted employees are the most valuable targets for criminal recruitment, and how these approaches have formalized

### Closing

End on the revelation: the most dangerous criminals in the ransomware ecosystem may not be the ones writing malware. They may be the ones sending LinkedIn-style recruitment messages to people who already have the keys to your network. The perimeter is not just technical. It is human.
