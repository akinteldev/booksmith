# Strategic Targeting and Victimology of Ransomware Operations in Early 2026

## Executive Assessment

Ransomware operations in early 2026 are best understood not as indiscriminate smash-and-grab attacks, but as increasingly deliberate campaigns against **organizational resilience itself**. The most consequential trend is not merely encryption or data theft; it is the systematic targeting of the systems that enable recovery: **backup infrastructure, identity services, cloud control planes, SaaS-integrated business data, and virtualization/management layers**. This reflects a strategic shift by attackers from “breach and extort” to **“disable, isolate, and extort”**.

The strongest evidence comes from the **2026 Unit 42 Global Incident Response Report**, based on **750+ major incidents across more than 50 countries and every major industry**, which found that attacks are now **4x faster**, the fastest intrusions reached **data exfiltration in as little as 1.2 hours**, and **over 90% of breaches were enabled by preventable exposure gaps rather than elite tradecraft**. Critically, **identity weaknesses were implicated in nearly 90% of investigated cases**, and **23% of cases involved SaaS application data**, indicating that cloud-delivered business platforms and identity-linked application ecosystems are now core ransomware terrain rather than secondary targets ([Unit 42 blog](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/blog/2026/02/unit-42-global-ir-report/); [Unit 42 report](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/resources/research/unit-42-incident-response-report); [Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS) data reinforces the targeting pattern from a victimology perspective. In the EU, the highest number of ransomware victims were in **Germany, Spain, France, and Italy**, and **manufacturing was the most targeted sector** across all four countries. EPRS also highlights a clear trend toward **SME victimization**, citing German data that **around 80% of cyber-attacks targeted SMEs** in 2025. It further notes that public administration, health, transport, digital infrastructure, finance, and manufacturing remain core sectors under pressure, with municipalities and healthcare entities particularly exposed because operational disruption creates coercive leverage ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

My assessment is that attackers are deliberately targeting recovery infrastructure because ransomware economics now reward **certainty of disruption** more than novelty of intrusion. If defenders can restore from backups, rotate credentials, rebuild virtual infrastructure, or recover SaaS content quickly, extortion fails. Therefore, modern ransomware actors increasingly attack the “resilience stack” first. This is also why SaaS and ICT supply chain abuse matters: these pathways allow adversaries to bypass hardened perimeters and enter through **trusted administrative channels, inherited permissions, vendor tooling, and transitive dependencies**, scaling compromise across many victims at once ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/); [EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

## Strategic Evolution of Ransomware Targeting

## From Endpoint Disruption to Resilience Denial

The defining strategic development entering 2026 is that ransomware operators are no longer content with encrypting user endpoints or a subset of servers. Their objective is to **maximize business paralysis while minimizing defender options**. This means neutralizing:

- **Backup and recovery systems**
- **Identity and access services**
- **Virtualization and management planes**
- **SaaS data repositories and cloud workflows**
- **Third-party provider connections and administrative tooling**

This evolution is consistent with Unit 42’s observation that the window between intrusion and material impact has sharply compressed. The fastest intrusions now reached exfiltration in **1.2 hours**, meaning defenders often lose the opportunity for orderly triage if identity abuse, automation, and privileged channels are already in play ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

The result is a ransomware model centered on **preemptive defeat of resilience mechanisms**. In practical terms, this means attackers seek to prevent rollback, impair authentication recovery, and exploit trusted orchestration layers that allow them to propagate commands broadly.

### Why Recovery Infrastructure Is a Strategic Target

The rationale is straightforward: every mature organization has improved endpoint protection, network segmentation, and incident response readiness since the major ransomware waves of prior years. Therefore, criminal operators have adapted by going after the infrastructure that determines whether the victim can **recover independently**.

#### 1. Backup systems are the ransom’s main obstacle

If immutable or isolated backups remain intact, encryption loses much of its coercive force. Attackers therefore have strong incentives to:
- delete snapshots,
- tamper with backup jobs,
- encrypt backup repositories,
- compromise backup administrators,
- or disable the network paths and credentials needed for restoration.

Although the supplied sources do not enumerate backup attack percentages, this targeting logic is strongly supported by Unit 42’s finding that identity flaws and privileged tool abuse now dominate serious incidents. Backup systems are only as resilient as the identity fabric and management tooling that administer them ([Unit 42 report](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/resources/research/unit-42-incident-response-report); [Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

#### 2. Identity services are now the center of gravity

Industrial Cyber’s summary of Unit 42 states that **identity loopholes drove nearly 90% of investigations**. This is strategically profound. Identity is the connective tissue between endpoints, cloud resources, backups, SaaS platforms, administrative consoles, and remote management tooling. If attackers compromise identity services, they do not need to break each layer individually; they inherit legitimate access paths across the environment ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

This also explains why ransomware operators increasingly prioritize:
- privileged identities,
- federation trust abuse,
- OAuth token theft,
- API key compromise,
- and access through administrative collaboration workflows.

Identity compromise turns recovery infrastructure into just another accessible target.

#### 3. Virtualization and management layers offer “one-to-many” disruption

Virtualization clusters, hypervisor hosts, remote monitoring and management (RMM), and mobile device management (MDM) tools are ideal ransomware force multipliers because they sit above large portions of the enterprise. Industrial Cyber notes that privileged vendor tools such as **RMM and MDM are being weaponized**, allowing attackers to push commands through **trusted administrative channels** ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

My view is that virtualization and orchestration layers are now functionally equivalent to “critical infrastructure” inside the enterprise. If attackers can disable or encrypt these layers, they can simultaneously impair production applications, restoration workflows, and forensic recovery. This is more efficient than endpoint-by-endpoint encryption and more devastating operationally.

## SaaS and ICT Supply Chains as the New Scale Mechanism

## Bypassing the Perimeter Through Trust

The perimeter is no longer the decisive defensive boundary. Unit 42’s 2026 findings suggest that the more relevant battleground is the **ecosystem of trusted integrations**: SaaS apps, OAuth consent flows, API keys, developer dependencies, cloud-native libraries, and vendor-administered tools.

Industrial Cyber reports that **23% of Unit 42 cases involved SaaS application data**. That is a major statistic because it means ransomware-relevant intrusion activity is increasingly touching systems that organizations often do not manage with the same rigor as traditional servers ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

### Why SaaS is attractive to ransomware operators

SaaS platforms are attractive because they:
- contain high-value business data,
- often rely on federated identity,
- are deeply integrated with workflows,
- use persistent tokens and APIs,
- and are frequently assumed to be “secure by vendor default.”

Attackers increasingly exploit **inherited permissions of OAuth tokens and API keys**, blending into legitimate automation traffic. This is strategically superior to noisy malware deployment because it:
- evades perimeter defenses,
- reduces dependence on custom tooling,
- complicates attribution,
- and allows quiet staging of extortion-relevant data theft ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

### Software supply chain exposure beyond vulnerable code

Another important shift is that supply chain risk is no longer limited to malicious code commits or exploited application vulnerabilities. According to Industrial Cyber’s synopsis of Unit 42, software supply chain risk has evolved into a **systemic threat** involving:
- SaaS integrations,
- vendor management plans,
- dependency ecosystems,
- transitive libraries,
- and build pipeline execution paths.

It notes that **over 60% of cloud-native vulnerabilities now reside in transitive libraries that can execute malicious code during the build phase**. This matters for ransomware because supply chain compromise offers a “one-to-many” avenue into many victims, often with privileged context ([Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

### ICT supply chains and EU-wide risk

EPRS similarly emphasizes that **ICT supply chain attacks are a popular way to spread disruption**, since compromising a single provider can yield simultaneous access to numerous integrated systems. It explicitly warns that such attacks can have **EU-wide repercussions** ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

This supports a broader conclusion: ransomware is becoming structurally closer to systemic cyber risk. A successful third-party compromise can now cripple multiple organizations at once, especially where the provider manages identity, administration, remote support, backups, or data synchronization.

## Victimology: Who Is Being Targeted?

## Cross-Sector Demographics

The available data indicates broad targeting, but not random targeting. Attackers prefer sectors where interruption creates immediate business, public service, or safety pressure.

### Primary sector concentrations

The following sectors emerge repeatedly in the source set:

| Sector | Evidence | Strategic appeal |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing | EPRS says manufacturing was the most targeted sector across Germany, Spain, France, and Italy; Unit 42 case involved disruption to manufacturing, distribution, shipping, and order processing | High downtime cost, complex OT/IT dependencies, logistics pressure |
| Healthcare | EPRS highlights particular concern due to postponed procedures and potential patient harm; GuidePoint notes healthcare remained among top impacted industries | Safety-critical operations, sensitive PHI, urgency to restore |
| Public administration / municipalities | EPRS says municipalities are frequent ransomware targets; example: Sanxenxo city council in Spain | Public pressure, citizen data, fragile local resilience |
| Finance | Listed by EPRS among major targeted sectors | High-value data and payment pressure |
| Transport and digital infrastructure | EPRS includes both among primary targets | Cascading disruption and national/regional impact |
| Retail / wholesale | GuidePoint lists retail & wholesale among top impacted industries | Thin margins, supply chain reliance, customer-facing disruption |
| Technology | GuidePoint lists technology among top impacted industries | Intellectual property, customer concentration, supply chain leverage |

Sources: ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf); [Unit 42 report](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/resources/research/unit-42-incident-response-report); [GuidePoint GRIT PDF](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

### Manufacturing stands out

Manufacturing deserves special emphasis. It is the clearest recurring sector in the data:
- EPRS states it was the **most targeted sector** in the EU across the four highest-victim countries: **Germany, Spain, France, and Italy** ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).
- Unit 42 provides a case of a **global medical technology firm** where ransomware caused prolonged disruption to **manufacturing, distribution, shipping, and order processing** after an intrusion initiated through SEO poisoning and a malicious administrative tool ([Unit 42 report](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/resources/research/unit-42-incident-response-report)).
- GuidePoint also lists manufacturing among the **four most impacted industries** by victim volume in 2025 ([GuidePoint GRIT PDF](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

This is not incidental. Manufacturing combines high downtime sensitivity, often heterogeneous legacy infrastructure, complex supplier dependencies, and in many environments weak separation between business IT and operational processes. Attackers understand that even limited disruption can halt revenue-generating production.

### Healthcare remains a coercion-rich target

Healthcare remains strategically attractive because disruption can quickly escalate into safety and continuity crises. EPRS explicitly notes that attacks on health can cause postponed procedures or even patient deaths, making this one of the sectors where ransomware pressure is morally and operationally acute ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

### SMEs are increasingly central victims, not collateral

A critical demographic insight from EPRS is the trend toward **SME targeting**. It cites German data indicating **around 80% of cyber-attacks targeted SMEs in 2025**, and reports that in Ireland **28% of SMEs risk shutting down after just one ransomware attack** ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

This undermines any assumption that ransomware actors only pursue large enterprises. My assessment is that SMEs are now attractive for three reasons:
1. lower security maturity,
2. less resilient backup and identity architecture,
3. strong payment pressure because even brief outages can be existential.

In other words, SMEs are not merely soft targets; they are high-probability monetization targets.

## Geographic Concentrations

## Europe

EPRS provides the clearest geographic concentration data for the EU. The highest number of ransomware victims were in:

- **Germany**
- **Spain**
- **France**
- **Italy**

EPRS notes this distribution correlates with GDP, suggesting attackers prioritize economies with large numbers of digitized and monetizable organizations ([EPRS PDF](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/ATAG/2026/785699/EPRS_ATA(2026)785699_EN.pdf)).

### Implications of the EU concentration pattern

This concentration implies that attackers are behaving economically, not politically, in most ransomware cases. They pursue:
- large addressable victim pools,
- digitally dependent sectors,
- dense supply chains,
- and jurisdictions where disruption of manufacturing, municipalities, and health services creates immediate response pressure.

## Global pattern

Unit 42’s dataset spans **50+ countries** and all major industries, confirming that ransomware and extortion campaigns are globally distributed rather than regionally isolated ([Unit 42 report](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/resources/research/unit-42-incident-response-report)). ThreatMon’s March 2026 snapshot suggests **sustained ransomware activity primarily targeting US organizations**, but the more authoritative cross-sector and demographic view remains the Unit 42 and EPRS material ([ThreatMon](https://threatmon.io/ransomware-2026-report-march/)).

## Attack Economics and the Role of AI

The supplied sources repeatedly indicate that AI is accelerating the attack lifecycle. Unit 42 and related reporting state that threat actors are using AI to automate vulnerability scanning soon after CVE disclosure, parallelize reconnaissance, and streamline extortion workflows. This contributes to compressed timelines and less recovery margin for defenders ([Unit 42 blog](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/blog/2026/02/unit-42-global-ir-report/); [Industrial Cyber](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/identity-loopholes-drive-nearly-90-of-unit-42s-global-incident-response-report-2026-investigations-as-ai-boosts-attack-lifecycle/)).

This matters because faster intrusion cycles make resilience-stack targeting even more effective. If attackers can rapidly obtain privileged access through identity abuse and then move against backup, SaaS, and virtualization assets, defenders may lose the chance to preserve recovery pathways before extortion begins.

## Analytical Conclusion

The strategic targeting pattern of ransomware operations in early 2026 is clear: attackers are deliberately choosing **victims and systems where recovery failure can be engineered**. The center of gravity has moved from endpoint compromise to **organizational resilience compromise**.

Three conclusions are warranted.

First, **identity has overtaken malware as the decisive ransomware enabler**. If nearly 90% of major investigations involve identity loopholes, then identity compromise is now the operational gateway to backup deletion, SaaS abuse, admin tooling misuse, and broad disruption.

Second, **SaaS and ICT supply chains are now central to ransomware scale**. Attackers exploit OAuth tokens, API keys, transitive dependencies, RMM/MDM platforms, and vendor trust relationships because these routes bypass classic perimeter defenses and support one-to-many impact.

Third, **victimology is increasingly selective and economic**. Manufacturing is the standout sector; healthcare, municipalities, and SMEs remain especially vulnerable because their operational urgency makes them more coercible. In Europe, the main victim concentrations—Germany, Spain, France, and Italy—track economic density and industrial dependence, not random distribution.

My concrete view is that ransomware in 2026 should be treated less as a malware problem and more as a **resilience sabotage problem**. Organizations that continue to focus narrowly on endpoint blocking while leaving identity, backup control planes, SaaS integrations, and third-party administrative trust insufficiently governed are defending the wrong layer. The attackers have already shifted upward into the systems that determine whether a victim can survive without paying.
